TheChirurgeon's Road Through 2026, Part 19: Unexpected Hobby Progress

May 7th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Welcome back, Dear Reader, to my ongoing blog of hobby and competitive progress through 2026. Last time around I was talking I was slowly inching along with my Red Corsairs and planning my list for the 2026 GW Dallas Open. This time around well, I've been sidetracked by some hobby progress that wasn't quite what I expected.

The Rebrand

It's old news by now, but a few weeks ago we rebranded, changing the site from Goonhammer to Tabletop Battles. Since then, many would-be marketing and branding experts have emerged from the woodwork to tell us how boring and forgettable the new name is, usually simultaneously demonstrating a lack of understanding for how domains work and the added challenges you have to face when your URL contains a term associated with porn. But the upsides here far outweigh the few minor downsides and traffic to the site has continued pretty much apace since the change, only with a better, faster interface that costs us substantially less to run.

One of the more important things about the rebrand is that it has given us a clear visual and brand identity we didn't have. This was, in some ways, inadvertent, but was also a reason we chose to go with Tabletop Battles as our name. We already had an icon for the app and a color scheme for it. Building a cohesive color scheme from there was relatively easy, and color matching tools have been around forever to streamline this process. Fun fact: you can use these to plan model color schemes as well. Turns out color theory is more a science than an art.



This is something we struggled to do for a long time with Goonhammer - we didn't really have a logo, just the site's name in Redeye Serif font, and we only picked that because it was the font used on 7th edition 40k rulebooks. That was just plain black/white text, and so we struggled to come up with good imagery for the site or a coherent color scheme. At best, we had "Start Competing blue" and we used Gregbot on a lot of things. Which was funny, but was also pretty obscure, especially if you weren't on our Discord. One of the funnier aspects of seeing people complain about the new brand is how often people misremember the old branding, often citing aspects of it that didn't exist. Aside from the word "Goon" there was nothing memorable about "Goonhammer."

Rest in piss, you stupid site name.

That said, my favorite logo I've done for the site is still this one:



It's more clever than good, and doesn't work at all as a silhouette, but it's a great encapsulation of the start-to-finish of the hobby. It's something we may revisit for Tabletop Battles at some point, but it's the only part of the old name/logo that I'll miss. And even then it's not a great logo.

On that note, I should mention that all of this falls on me, as the site's resident artist and graphic designer, so making new logos, figuring out our brand colors, and doing new icons has been a big part of what I've had to work on over the past month. Part of rebranding the site is that we've needed new banners. Our new site does a much better job resizing images to cut down on bandwidth usage and site loads, making for a much, much faster site that isn't loading full 1920x1080 banner images every time it shows you a thumbnail on the front page. That change alone saves us thousands of dollars and makes your experience faster, but for some older images in our DB we need to swap out the banners to create new ones which can be resized accordingly. I also need to update our banners to reflect the new branding and fonts.

So I've been doing a ton of new banners for the site.

Loading...


Some are full redraws, others are updates of older banners. The idea is to replace everything that used the old font. Yes, those are updated banners for Ruleshammer and Hammer of Math. Yes, they'll be back soon as we get into 11th edition proper.

I've also been working on some new art. We're working on a new Lore Explainer series around Armageddon and in the process of planning for that decided we need to do articles on Ghazghkull, Yarrick, and Grimaldus. So I sat down last Friday and started doing a "quick" Ghaz and then six hours later had this finished piece, which is my favorite I've done for the site:

Credit: Robert "TheChirurgeon" Jones

And while I wasn't planning on it initially, I ended up liking how it turned out so much that I wanted to do Yarrick as well. So I did him next.

Credit: Robert "TheChirurgeon" Jones

Yarrick took a ton less time, thankfully. I was really happy with him and that's when I realized I should go ahead and do Grimaldus.

Credit: Robert "TheChirurgeon" Jones

Grimaldus also turned out great, and I'm really happy with how the lighting turned out on him. Ghaz is still my favorite of the three by far, but the perspective and lighting are stronger on Grimaldus.

These are all drawn and colored digitally in Photoshop. I use a Wacom Cintiq tablet I've had for about ten years now (I'm liable to buy a new one soon; this one is good but is starting to show its age a bit). These took most of my hobby time this past weekend - I made some progress on my Red Corsairs Rhino and a marine but didn't finish - so I'm going to talk about how these were painted for a change.

The Process

Working with Photoshop and getting the most out of it is all about using layers, which are by far the most powerful tool in your arsenal when you're doing digital painting. A piece like Ghaz will typically have a dozen or more, though if I'm doing something really big and involved it can be 50+ layers, easy.



I tend to look for reference art whenever I do a known character, and especially when I draw Space Marines. The Joytoy figures are often my go-to because I can find photos of them in the poses I need, but there's inexplicably no Ghaz Joytoy so I used a mix of the model and the cover of Ghazghkull Thraka: Prophet of the Waaagh!, drawn by Alexander Mokhov. It's a great piece and I love the way he did Ghaz's bionic eye. I started my drawing using the model as my reference, then came back to Mokhov's piece for color reference.



The whole thing typically starts with a sketch. I use light blue when I do this, a holdover from doing traditional penciling and inking, but with photoshop you can work in black/gray and just recolor it later if you want. This is cargo culting in some sense - the reason they used to do this was because non-photo blue pencils were invisible to camera film, scanners, and xerox machines, making it easy to remove them after inks were added. It's also a light color that contrasts with the dark ink color, making it easy to see what you're doing when you draw over the lines.



You may notice some green in there. After I drew the initial blue sketch, I came back over it and added additional lines, refining and changing some shapes. When I do that, I often work in green. I also tend to do my blue sketch with a larger, thicker pen tool size, which makes it easier to add detail later.



Once that's down, I lower the opacity of the blue and go back over it, drawing the link lines. This takes a long time, and I will often re-sketch areas as needed during the process. If you think this version of the piece looks awesome well, yeah I really like how the linework turned out and there's just something about having crisp black linework over rough blue pencils that looks great to me.



You don't have to work with thick, black outlines - it's certainly a stylistic choice I make most of the time, but it does have its advantages. One of those is that I can vary line width to do some volumetric work. Sometimes that'll mean thicker lines on the bottom edges of objects and shapes; often it means thicker outlines for hey objects. In this case I went back in and added thicker outlines to the head and the jaw teeth, to give them sharper delineation from the face and make them pop more. Color will help with that as well.

Speaking of which, it's on to color. When you have layers, you don't really paint "in" the lines so much as under them, creating layers under your linework which can be adjusted separately.



This starts by blocking out your color area. One cool thing about layers is that you can block the areas you need and not worry about what's nearby if that's going to be on a layer above your current one - you can just mask that upper layer accordingly. You can also lock your layers once you've set the base color, making it impossible to paint outside your defined area.



After base color comes the rendering part, where I'm using different brushes and opacities to render the skin to the appropriate level of detail. I used softer brushes here than I do most of the time, going for a softer, more painterly look.



I liked what I had but wasn't happy with the shading. It wasn't dramatic enough, and to get more drama I needed more contrast. So I added a second layer on top of that with very heavy shading in dark dark green over the areas I wanted to be in shadow, then adjusted the opacity of that layer until I had the right tones.



The inside of the mouth goes on the next layer up, following the same process.



The blocking out the armor's jaw.



Doing Ghaz's mechanical/metal skull.



Then I started to add in details on top of that, painting the eyes and the big red teeth stuck to his armor jaw. Repeat this process for the rest of Ghaz and you more or less have the idea.



On that note, there's a final, upper layer to this that sits above the linework that's all for very small, light edge highlighting.

I posted Work-in-Progress shots of the other two to the GH discord while working on them, so you can see them to get a feel for what the "in progress" step looks like for these.



These took between 4 and 8 hours each, and they notably don't have legs, hands, or backgrounds. More detailed pieces like the site banners can take substantially longer, and anything that's a full-fledged scene with multiple characters can take a week or more. So while I'm light this week on mini painting, know that I still did my share of painting.

Next Week: More Games, Probably with Defilers

That does it for this week's update, which was unfortunately pretty light on actual model painting and playing. There are just gonna be some weeks like that, unfortunately. I'm still very happy with the hobby progress I made however, and I'm looking forward to trying to get in another game or two before next week, testing out a triple Defiler Death Guard build just to see how insane it is. Yeah, I know the situation is fucked, but I'm not above taking advantage while the getting is good.

See you next week.
