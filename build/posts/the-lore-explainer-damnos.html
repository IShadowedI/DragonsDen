The Lore Explainer: Damnos

April 29th, 2026 via Goonhammer
In our Lore Explainer series we take a deep look at the lore of various games, settings, and factions. In this article we're looking at the lore behind the Damnos Campaign.

In our recent exploration of the lore behind The Ultramarines, we touched briefly on the Damnos Campaign. With the relaunch of Apocalypse in June 2013, Games Workshop published new rules for playing massive games of Warhammer 40k using the sixth edition rules framework. That initial book featured rules and Detachments for every faction as well as a campaign based around the second War for Armageddon. Following that, Games Workshop would give additional support to each non-Imperium faction through campaign books, including War Zone: Valedor for Drukhari, Aeldari, and Tyrands and War Zone: Damocles for the T'au Empire. In between these was War Zone: Damnos, which detailed rules for the Necrons.

Welcome to Damnos

Damnos is a frozen, ice-covered mining world in the eastern Ultima Segmentum, settled in the early days of the Great Crusade. Humans lived on Damnos for the better part of of ten thousand years, diving deeper beneath the planet's surface until they stumbled onto the ruins of a Necron tomb. In 972.M41, the planet's surface erupted with powerful tremors and seismic shocks which wreaked havoc on the planet's geothermic fusion stations. When repair teams journeyed beneath the surface to investigate, they found buried Necron ruins marked by a series of sealed doors.

It didn't take long for word of their discovery to get out, and soon Magos Karnak of the Adeptus Mechanicus was on hand, assuming command of the excavation with his fellow Tech-Priests. But despite their efforts, they had no luck actually entering the slumbering tomb. They kept at it, but to no avail.

Credit: Robert "TheChirurgeon" Jones

The Fall of Damnos

After about a year of Mechanicus workers picking at that particular scab, the Necrons woke up, emerging from those tombs to bring death to the planet. Phalanx after phalanx ravaged the world, smashing their defense forces and growing in number as more of the tomb complexes were reactivated. And ahead of the Necron forces came powerful electromagnetic storms that made distress calls impossible. Eventually the Necrons unveiled their endgame: A powerful, floating Necropolis of immense size, literally draining the planet's oceans. It turns out Damnos wasn't so much a naturally occurring planet but an artificial construction of fused-together tomb complexes, covered in ice, rock, and water.

The planet's Lord Governor sent for help, calling for any guard regiments who were available. Fortunately for him, his message was intercepted by Chief Librarian Tigurius, who handed it off to Cato Sicarius of the 2nd Company of the Ultramarines. It would take them time to arrive, however - and that was a problem because a week later a pic-cast with speech in High Gothic sent by the Necrons conveyed their message with perfect clarity: This world was theirs, and they would scour humanity from its surface.

The Necrons laid siege to Kellenport, the capital city of Damnos Secundus, bringing a Necron army of unprecedented size to bear. The Astra Militarum fought bravely, but were ultimately outmatched. Before they could break however, the Ultramarines arrived, led by Cato Sicarius. Cato and his forces smashed through the front of the Necron lines, helping destroy and disable the Gauss Pylons holding their reinforcements at bay. Despite this, however, the Necrons continued to advance.

Sicarius eventually fought his way through to the Necrons' leader, a gilded giant of a Necron known as the Undying. The two fought and Cato lost, injured by a Warscythe. He was only saved by the intervention of the Venerable Dreadnought Agrippan, who shielded Cato and injured the Undying, forcing him to phase out and return to his tomb.

Without their leader, the Necrons fell back on defensive protocols that made them predictable and slow, allowing Sicarius and his men to escape and drive the Necrons back before withdrawing to the evacuation site. Cato Sicarius had tasted defeat in battle for the first time and in total the Ultramarines lost more than half of their 2nd company before they were able to evacuate with the rest of the planet's defenders. In the process, Agrippan was destroyed, his reactor going critical and taking out Kellenport's space port as the last defenders left the planet.

This campaign would go on to be referred to as The Fall of Damnos.

Ultramarines Cato Sicarius. Credit: SRM

The Second Battle of Damnos

The Fall of Damnos, although one of the first full-scale conflicts between humanity and the Necrons, was a relatively normal occurrence in the early days of the Necron resurgence. The Necrons would awaken on some world that hadn't been adequately surveyed ten to twenty millennia prior, spew out a horde of robotic warriors, and scour life off the planet in a relentless and merciless grind. The whole thing was starting to get pretty embarrassing for the Imperium as more worlds fell and so about twenty five years after the Fall of Damnos in 999.M41, the High Lords of Terra made the call to send the Ultramarines to reclaim Damnos.

The Deathwatch would play a key role in this retaliatory strike, and they were the ones who delivered the orders to Ultramar. Among those Deathwatch forces was Davian Imocles, an old squadmate of Cato Sicarius. The High Lords believed the specialized skills of the Deathwatch would make the difference in this second campaign. Chapter Master Marneus Calgar wasn't so sure; in particular he was worried about the scale of the Tomb World lurking beneath the surface of Damnos and just how bad this was going to get, especially given that they'd be facing a fully awakened foe this time around.

Ultimately pride won out: Despite his misgivings, Calgar couldn't let the Ultramarines' failure in the first Damnos campaign to stand. The Ultramarines mustered their entire chapter, joining the Deathwatch and a massive force of Imperial Guard and made for Damnos. Cato Sicarius had been itching for this fight particularly hard, aching to avenge the indignity he'd suffered the first time around.

Calgar started the fight with a ruse, by detonating warheads near the planet's asteroid belt and sending them hurtling toward the planet's surface. The fleet then advanced with the radioactive rocks, confusing Necron sensors and masking their approach. By the time the Necrons were aware of the Ultramarines, they were already in orbit and launching their drop-pods. Many of these were destroyed by Necron Pylons but the ones that made it to the surface were largely unmanned Deathwind drop pods. The real Planetfall assault came from a second fleet, who struck just afterward, showering the planet in drop pods and Imperial Guard regiments.

The Imperial forces struck at the massive tomb complex believed to be the heart of the Necron grid. Despite massive bombardments from orbit and coordinated strikes, the Imperial attackers were stymied at every turn, met with implacable foes who could stand back up after suffering grievous blows and return to the fight and an impenetrable energy shield protecting the Tesseract Vault at its heart. In an act of desperation and using his own two (powered) fists, Calgar turned a Gauss pylon back on the floating Necropolis and used its beam to destroy the energy shield.

The destruction of the energy shield also destroyed the containment cage of the Tesseract Vault, freeing the Transcendent C'tan housed within the Necron structure. Rising from the destroyed ruins was a shard of the star god Yggra'nya, the Moulder of Worlds, capable of breaking apart and reshaping planets with just a thought. Yggra'nya was not real happy with its imprisonment, and took to lashing out at Necron and Marine alike. It started by crushing the body of the Necron Lord known as the Undying, forcing him to phase out back to his tomb.

While this was going on, Squad Lazarius of the Deathwatch made their way deep into the Necron tomb complex where they found the sarcophagus of the Undying. Having just been forced to return by the release of the Transcendent C'tan above, the Undying was attempting to repair and revive himself. Unfortunately for him however the Deathwatch had rigged his Sarcophagus with melta bombs and they detonated, destroying the Necron Lord once and for all.

Back on the surface the Ultramarines were contending with a new foe. They'd turned the tide against the Necrons but now were struggling with the unleashed C'tan. Unfortunately for Yggra'nya, it was facing off against Cato Sicarius, a named- and modeled-captain of the Adeptus Astartes, and as such rendered impervious to harm. Cato had just finished killing a Flayer Lord when Yggra'nya attacked, grabbing him with a massive hand formed from rocks. Before Cato could be crushed to death, he lobbed a vortex grenade he'd received from Imocles at the C'tan, blinking it out of existence in a moment.

The Aftermath

The Ultramarines spent the next few days destroying the rest of the Necrons and collapsing their tomb complexes. The population that had been rescued 25 years earlier and relocated to Tarentus were shipped back to Damnos and given the world to claim as their own. The planet rejoined the Imperial fold, and Calgar recorded his notes on how to beat Necrons. The High Lords of Terra were happy, and everyone lived happily ever after.

Szarekh, The Silent King. Credit: Rockfish

The Third War for Damnos

Haha, just kidding. No one lives happily ever after in the grim darkness of the far future. In M42 a distress call was sent out to the Ultramarines form Damnos: An enormous Necron invasion force of the Szarekhan Dynasty had descended upon the planet, decimating their forces. With much of their strength devoted to the Indomitus Crusade, the Ultramarines called for their allies to aid in their efforts to defend Damnos. They were joined by Marines of the Libators, Iron Hounds, and Brazen Consuls as they rushed to aid the planet's embattled defenders. Things were going pretty poorly until additional Marines from the Salamanders and White Scars arrived to assist them.

As of this writing, the Third War for Damnos is still ongoing.

Notes

The meat of what has been written about Damnos is all in Apocalypse War Zone: Damnos and a pair of novels by Nick Kyme. Although the Deathwatch and Adeptus Mechanicus appear to be involved in the conflict, the rulebook limits their actual page time. This is because neither would receive full army releases until well after the publication of War Zone: Damnos, and when it came to campaign books and rules, Games Workshop didn't tend to feature groups without real armies (see also: Genestealer Cults being absent in Shield of Baal).

Where to Read More

If you're looking to read more about all this, you have a few options, covering different eras of the conflict.
  • Fall of Damnos (by Nick Kyme) - This 2011 novel details the Necrons' emergence on Damnos and the response by the Ultramarines, led by Cato Sicarius and Chief Librarian Tigurius. I have no idea if this one is any good; most reviews say it's only OK. The book's release notably predates the publication of the fifth edition Codex: Necrons and so served as the first introduction to their revised lore.
  • Spear of Macragge (by Nick Kyme) - This novella takes place during the same Fall of Damnos and details the heroic efforts of Tigurius and Sergeant Chronus to survive as things fall apart around them.

Final Thoughts

Damnos isn't exactly on the level of Armageddon in terms of importance, but it has seen its share of combat. It's notable for being a location of legitimate defeat for the Ultramarines, and being the "Necrons" Warzone that the lore can return to from time to time. I expect we'll eventually see more around Damnos in the future, though the Third War for Damnos was introduced around the same time as the Pariah Nexus at the start of 9th edition. It's helpful for making the Necrons seem like more of a widespread threat, but it does make the one planet seem less important compared to an entire region of space.
