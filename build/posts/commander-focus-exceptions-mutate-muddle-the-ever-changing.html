Commander Focus: Exceptions, Mutate, & Muddle, the Ever-Changing

May 7th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Though Magic's Judge program has essentially been discontinued, at least in an official capacity, those who take stock of the game's rules are a critical part of in-store infrastructure. Whether you're asking your local judge to clarify specific timings or rulings, or need a quick reference when building out a complicated list, those who understand the foundations of the game's syntax are an often underappreciated and indispensable resource; I say all of this as someone who's passed the certification to be a Level 1 judge back in the day, and whose main interface with the game is through the lens of weird card interactions. Case in point, without that education, and a community of those who adore the intricacies of the game's rules, I would not be covering Muddle, the Ever-Changing today. In my initial review of the Otter, I passed him over as the centerpoint of a fragile combo deck, built on the back of cards like Port Razer—been there, done that. In digging deeper, and looking for an interesting option in Prismari for the site's coverage of Secrets of Strixhaven, however, there was one nagging word that dominated my read of Muddle: "Except". Except in Magic is a titillating word, because it calls to mind another phrase that appears frequently in my articles, 'copiable values'. We're not just using this Otter as a path to value by way of Myriad, though that's certainly a viable thrust for gameplay. Instead, we're turning the text of this card on its head, and reverse-engineering one of the most peculiar Prowess lists I've ever laid eyes upon. To add more layers of rule-bending, the way forward also involves a judge's worst nightmare: Mutate.



"Target Nonlegendary Creature...Except It Has Myriad."

First, let's talk about copiable values. While I go more in-depth into its rulings in my coverage of Firion, Wild Rose Warrior, what's important to know is that not every part of a card comes along when copied. You do borrow what's printed on the card, physically, but things like counters, most lingering abilities, etc don't transfer to a relevant Clone effect. If you make a copy of, say, Grinning Ignus with Twinflame, and then Populate that token, creating a copy of the copy, what comes with it? You have everything printed on Ignus itself, such as mana cost, name, text, & Power/Toughness, but it doesn't get exiled in the End Step like the actual token created by Twinflame. That's because the delayed trigger isn't a copiable value; that Populated token will, however, have Haste...because essentially everything after 'except' in Magic text is a copiable value. To bring things back to Muddle, that means if we have the Otter become a copy of Grinning Ignus (except it has Myriad), and then Clone said Ignus copy, it enters as an Ignus with Myriad...and never reverts to Muddle, because that delayed trigger to return to its Ottery original at end of turn isn't a copiable value. This is explained in and around Comprehensive Rule 707.2, but in truth Cloning is one of Magic's most esoteric aspects.

Given we know the Myriad text is copiable, and the reversion isn't, it's now worth confronting the fact that Muddle can only become a copy of something while it isn't already something else. It doesn't retain its innate ability, just Myriad, so once Muddle's stuck as something with no other triggers on the stack, it's like that until the effect wears off. This sucks a little bit, given you can't really slip between forms to dodge removal via Hexproof on one Creature, and then dive back into a value piece afterwards, making Muddle decidedly not ever-changing. What if there were a way to make Muddle's ability a copiable value, however? If we were able to have Muddle copy itself, it would remain the very same, plus an additional instance of Myriad, but sadly there's a nonlegendary clause there we'd have to work around. Crucially, cards that make 'the Legend rule' not work like Mirror Gallery & Mirror Box don't actually remove the Legendary supertype, just make it such that additional copies don't need to die as state-based actions. We'd need to somehow remove the supertype itself from Muddle, and have the fact it isn't Legendary be a copiable value. There are ways to do so readily, such as with Sorceries like Irenicus's Vile Duplication & Quantum Misalignment, or Enchantments like The Apprentice's Folly or The Clone Saga. What I'm interested most in, however, is doing so via Creatures.

New Mutants

How do you do, fellow value lists?

Mutate is a beautiful mess of a mechanic, and undoubtedly the origin of many headscratchers among local playgroups. It allows you to stack Creatures on top of one another, and in its simplest terms, results in the merged Creature being whatever card is on top...plus the textboxes of anything below it. Merged Creatures follow internal logic, with effects applying in the layers you'd assume (Tarmogoyf and other P/T setting effects override the topmost Creature's stats, even if they're below it), and the entire stack is copiable as though printed together. If you take an Everquill Phoenix, and stack it onto your Muddle, therefore, you're left with an Everquill Phoenix that has "Whenever you cast an instant or..."—Muddle's entire ability. Everquill Phoenix isn't a Legendary Creature, meaning it can be the target of Muddle's ability, and even once copied, still has the associated text to become a copy whenever you cast an Instant or Sorcery Spell, except with an extra instance of Myriad. Putting all this together, if you somehow create either a Nonlegendary version of Muddle, or a Merged Creature with its text below, you've arrived at a Commander that has an odd sort of Prowess, but instead of getting +1/+1, you're getting two additional attacking copies when you swing out. This gets...out of hand, and fast.

While you're likely only swinging with ~3-5 power at one opponent, the other two players seated at the table likely face triple that damage, spread across a number of bodies. Muddle is uniquely interesting to politick with, because the same player who you can wheel & deal to allow the original copy to hit unimpeded also benefits by not having a swarm of mutant Otters thrown their way. There's also much to be said about the quality of some Mutate options, drawing tons of cards with Sea-Dasher Octopus or cycling back ways to self-clone Muddle with Lore Drakkis. Broadly speaking the actual thing your Mutate body does is less important than it having the alternate casting cost, but know that there's some side benefits in the right scenarios. Something especially interesting is the fact that, like Auras, Mutate targets its intended merge choice, and coupled with a need for cheap, advantage-neutral Instants & Sorceries, a side theme of targeted Spells emerges. You can extract extra value from each Mutate by way of Frontline Heroism (thank goodness the Soldiers aren't Human), and Vesuvan Duplimancy is a powerful card that we can enable via cards already great in this list. In a similar fashion, though, Orvar, the All-Form is no slouch when it comes to copying the right permanents, and the fact it's a Changeling has profound benefits. Why, you might ask? It means he's an Elemental.

Elementary, My Dear Watson

While I've been lovingly referring to Muddle all this time as an Otter, the more relevant type it possesses is Elemental, primarily because of the ramp suite in this list. That's Smokebraider & Flamebraider, two 2 mana Creatures which each tap for 2 mana, spendable only on Elementals. We can use this to catapult ourselves to Muddle rather quickly, but there's a number of fantastic pieces included in our 99 that also make use of this pair. Orvar we've already touched upon, but what about Dreamtail Heron? It's a tutorable Mutate piece, by way of Flamekin Harbinger, and in terms of our Mutate options it's likely in the top 3 regardless. Runaway Steamkin helps us storm off, and Ashling, Rekindled//Ashling, Rimebound approximates the 'braiders, helping us pay for the somewhat-overcosted Mutate Creatures. Kindred comes home to roost though, because being an 'Elemental Spell' doesn't just mean bodies—Kindle the Inner Flame, Ashling's Command, and even Blades of Velis Vel bolster our Elemental density. These cards are each reasonable individually, but if we aren't spending our type-specific mana, that huge upside gets wasted. I cannot stress enough, the fact Muddle gets to play these accelerants is a huge boon, and I feel makes this side-package of Elementals worthwhile. All three of these Spells target, touching on that synergy, and there's even clones among them, on top of being ways to trigger Muddle itself.

Total sidenote, Corporeal Projection should be in 99% of UR lists.

The final option worth mentioning is Grinning Ignus, being a way to launder our 'braider mana into non-Elemental sources, and assist in the deck's sole infinite combo. The only Elemental that didn't make it in, largely because of a lack of consistent fuel, is Tempestra, Dame of Games; her not only being an Elemental, but being able to clone Muddle with a copiable Nonlegendary characteristic, makes her in theory perfect for us. The problem stems from running a whopping single Artifact across the whole list. If you want to swap around the ramp package to signets & talismans, she becomes far more appetizing! Our ramp lineup is largely based in Creatures, whether that's persistent generation via Birgi, Steam-Kin, & Electro, Assaulting Battery, or closer to the mana rocks we're used to, via the 'braiders. While it may at first appear we lack ramp, much of our power comes from hinging around pop-off turns & usage of high value, type-specific mana.

Stifling Creativity

Last week, I spoke about making your interactive suite work for you proactively, in terms of Quandrix, the Proof. There, we were playing Unsubstantiates and the like to fish for more Cascade triggers, but here, how are we breaking parity with what Muddle's doing, as an aggro deck? The answer arrives in the acknowledgement that Myriad's delayed trigger to exile those copies at end of combat is a single trigger, per two tokens...and only checks once. If you can avoid or counter their removal, you're left with three times the Muddles, each of which can stack up Myriad next turn (or next combat, if you're pushing for a win). We can flex cards like Stifle, Spider-Sense, & Consign to Memory not only as ways of disrupting opposing gameplans, but also ways of keeping spare Muddles around, and resulting in significantly more damage if uninterrupted. Myriad being a single check, however, also means we can make use of Phasing—if a card is phased out, you can't exile or sacrifice it, meaning you've got it next turn once the Otter phases back in. March of Swirling Mist & Slip Out the Back are great not just for insulating your own gameplan, but can be disruptive if all you need is one more turn to remove a problematic player. After all, things phasing back in aren't a problem if their controller isn't alive to see it. Along those lines, though, is Teferi's Veil, a repeatable mechanism of keeping your attackers safe from Sorcery-speed board wipes, including your Myriad copies. It's a card that should be in every single Muddle list, and I'm not kidding there. While they've just barely not made the cut, it's also worth noting that ending the turn with Myriad's triggers on the stack prevents them from resolving, so Discontinuity & Sundial of the Infinite are great ways of preventing your copies from going away; the reason they've been resigned to the sideboard comes down to space, and not being as obviously justifiable as those similar effects which can be flexed as interaction, or protection.

Splitting the Red-Blue Sea with one non-token.

Like many of the lists I've built recently, the path to victory is via combat, although that's not to say it's without a combo. I tend to find that, while I personally am a combo player, it behooves you to at least try to 'win fairly', and have an infinite endgame only for if you're so far ahead as to have already won, or if a game is going too long. If you do include a combo like that, it likewise helps for every individual piece to do something on its own, and not be shackled to the combo itself. The chosen package is Grinning Ignus plus either Birgi, God of Storytelling//Harnfel, Horn of Bounty, or Runaway Steam-Kin. Either of our mana engines make the Ignus mana neutral, and help accelerate our gameplan on their own (with Ignus & Steam-Kin both even being Elementals), and you can cap this route to infinite Storm count with a Grapeshot, which is a targeting Sorcery itself. Every piece is independently justified, and together allows players to shuffle up for the next game if things get too complicated. It's the platonic ideal of a Bracket 2.5 combo.

Example Decklist: Muddle's Quiet Council

There's a ton of cool things going on in our 99 when you take a peek beneath the hood, even beyond the rules nightmare that serves as the crux of our gameplan. Sure, we're making use of Mutate & copiable values to fuel an aggro shell, but to enable it we've got to cast tons of Instants & Sorceries, ideally that target. Ergo, let me introduce you to Flame Jab. Running out of Spells is one of the nightmares in Storm, and we're on a reasonable amount of Lands which could lead to a drought during our midgame. Not only does Flame Jab cost a mere {R}, meaning things like Birgi & Electro make it functionally free, but it even targets, giving us repeatable access to Orvar & Vesuvan Duplimancy shenanigans. Less repeatable but similarly cool is the underrated Crowd's Favor, a free Spell that's been making its way into more of my lists. If something like Gut Shot is playable (and boy, it sure is here), I think Favor is as good or better in most scenarios. You do need at least one body to Convoke it out, but it's Instant-speed, targets, and can make Muddle less appetizing to block in a pinch. Awesome card.

A weird little package we're on is the theft Drakes, being Gilded Drake & Volatile Stormdrake. If we hold priority with their enters trigger on the stack, we can turn Muddle into a copy of one, by casting an Instant, and when we swing out with Myriad we can permanently steal two Creatures! That's a ton of value, and even beyond that, we're on cards like Snap & Snapback to repeatedly disrupt opponents, or reset our Mutates. Finally, I wanted to touch on why we aren't currently on things like Roaming Throne or Twinflame Travelers. At their face, these seem insane, doubling both Muddle's copy (resulting in two Myriad instances per cast) and Myriad itself (four attackers per trigger). The issue is, at least if we want to use Mutate to smooth out our gameplan, Muddle isn't always guaranteed to be an Elemental itself. There's a world where those options don't do anything for you, and would require we retool the list to run more Nonlegendary copy effects which retain the originals' type. That absolutely is a path forward, and a good one at that, but the consistency behind Mutate is what's guided me to where the list is now.

Decklists are kept updated, and may change with set releases.



This list, like so many I build now, exists at the precipice of Bracket 3, in the limbo of 'Game Changer-less 3'. I've talked about it before on Commander Focus, but more and more my testing sees me playing with folks that want to toe the line without actually jamming cards like The One Ring, etc. The removal of these auto-includes opens up more space, and ideally sees the game run longer, and I feel it behooves Wizards to consider creating another Bracket where so many people already stand. It's the new 7, and Muddle lives in it. If you wanted to speed the Otter up, that's fairly easy, and you can run more wheels & fast mana to churn through cards and get swinging, as well as win conditions like Displacer Kitten lines. If you wanted to power down, or just reduce the cost of the deck from a monetary perspective, just getting rid of Gilded Drake, Wheel of Fortune, & Volcanic Island craters the cost down to ~$600. You could pare to just Birgi as the most expensive card in the list, even get rid of something like Quantum Misalignment, and have a similar gameplay experience. That's for folks not looking to proxy, because I do genuinely believe this version of Muddle is worth everyones' time, especially for players who already love aggro/Prowess archetypes.

Muddle the Mixture (Is Not in This List)

Andrew Mar weaves between cute and unsettling with this otter...lobster-squid.

Prismari was the final school I penned a decklist for, in Tabletop Battles' coverage of Secrets of Strixhaven, because the beauty of this list isn't obvious from 10,000 feet. Looking at the set from above, there's a ton of inoffensive value Commanders that do something cool, but not especially unique, and I wrongly labelled Muddle as such; it was only in helping a friend build out the Otter that I stumbled upon this version, in my quest to skirt all its restrictions, and use what I learned as a judge. Like with anything that's piqued someone's interest, there are those phrases which activate some bundle of neurons tucked deep in the brain, triggering you like a Winter Soldier-esque sleeper agent. For Magic Judges, reading 'except' and seeing Mutate synergy ought to light up someone's cerebellum like a Christmas tree, and while the actual play of this Muddle list is fairly linear as an aggro pile, arriving to it was no small feat. If we look at the EDHREC page for the Otter, not a single Mutate Creature appears, nor a single Stifle effect. At a current ~1,900 decks, what we see is that banal value pile I believed Muddle to be, the reason I skimmed through it in spoiler season. It feels amazing to be wrong, and in such spectacular fashion.

I think there's something to be said about 'finding the fun' with a lot of modern Commander design, because while there's generally an obvious path to take, eking out the last few percentage points and finding perfect, weird cards to slot in feels wildly rewarding. How much better is a Muddle list like this than your average 99? That's hard to say, given you're aiming to, instead of accruing value via a single instance of Myriad on a great creature, swing in with a swarm of token copies; you're far more comparable to the Balmor, Battlemage Captains of the world than a midrange-y clone deck. That version isn't bad, and has a very high ceiling as just a Creature-based goodstuff pile, and there's an element missed of copying something like Pyrogoyf and blowing someone up. The fun we've found, however, is well enough to doff those value clones. I wouldn't say it 'plays like nothing else', because it fundamentally does, but in such wacky fashion as to deserve full coverage as you've read here today. Muddle's not reinventing the wheel, it's reinventing the road.

Until next time, otter up.
