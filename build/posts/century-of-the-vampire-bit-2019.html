Century of the Vampire: Bit (2019)

May 8th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Welcome to the Century of the Vampire, an ongoing weekly feature where Tabletop Battles managing editor Jonathan Bernhardt watches some piece of vampire media, probably a movie but maybe eventually television will get a spot in here too, and talks about it at some length in the context of both its own value as a piece of art and as a representation of the weird undead guys that dominate western pop culture who aren’t (usually) zombies.

Last time, Bernhardt reviewed the 2009 Paul Weitz movie Cirque du Freak: The Vampire's Assistant. Today, he looks at the 2019 Brad Michael Elmore film, Bit. This article will contain spoilers.



This is an aggressively fine vampire flick. It’s on the good side of fine, to be clear -- I wouldn’t watch this again, but I would recommend it if someone was putting together a list of horror-inflected movies that both won’t make you think too hard and won’t bring down the room with any harsh vibes. Byzantium, for instance, is a better movie with more compelling performances, but it’s not really a movie for chilling out on the couch with the gang and half-paying attention to while you smoke a little and drink a little. This movie is much better at that. But the “half-paying attention” part is crucial, because if you want a movie to actually watch in that scenario, I’d go with Only Lovers Left Alive. Bit, you can kind of drift in and out of.

The movie opens with the line, in voiceover from our protagonist Laurel (Nicole Maines, who has had a very eventful and inspiring life outside of the acting business, and is perfectly charming here), “You know those teen vampire movies that feel like the horny soap opera fever dreams of a sentient 8th grade diary? Here's how this one ended,” over a woman vampire turning her male lover into a vampire and getting him executed and herself imprisoned by a vengeful coven of female vampires. We’ll pause on the implications there for a moment; I want to watch one of these “teen vampire movies that feel like the horny soap opera fever dreams of a sentient 8th grade diary,” because I’m not quite sure what that means. Not in the sense that I don’t know what the line is referring to -- this is a shot at Twilight, as is made clear in the outro voiceover over the ending title card, where Laurel hopes they get to make sequels and promises not to get pregnant in the fourth film -- but those things were created in a lab to be inert and sexless, no matter how many abs Taylor Lautner developed. But then I suppose that’s what the eighth grade part is there for. I guess I don’t want to spend any more time thinking about what “horny” things are written down in an eighth-grade diary, but I would prefer a little bit more precision in my shade.



 

Anyway, the coven kills the hell out of this dude and throws the woman in vampire jail, which is a pit below their cool nightclub. Our head witch here is Duke (Diana Hopper), the real star of the film -- as is the case in a lot of vampire fiction, the foil gets a hell of a lot more to work with than the lead. She gets better wardrobe, she gets better hair, she gets better lines, and being evil and menacing is so much more fun than being a Normal Girl in the Big City who is Seeing All This Stuff For The First Time. As is also the case many times in vampire fiction and especially queer monster fiction, our villain also turns out to be an extremely sympathetic figure with understandable motives who perhaps just took things a little bit too far.

We join Laurel to start the plot of the movie in earnest and it’s very boilerplate: Laurel comes to town; Laurel argues with her slacker older brother she’s crashing with, Mark (James Paxton), who is mainly in this movie for exposition; Laurel meets Izzy (Zolee Griggs), a junior member of the coven, and gets turned into a vampire by her after brief flirtation. That’s fine by the coven’s rules, since she’s a woman. I was a bit worried going into this plot point, but no, Laurel being trans doesn’t come up here; in fact it doesn’t come up in the entire film, in the fashionable way that being gay, being trans, being non-binary, or being in basically any LGTBQ+ identity group was treated in the 2010s; by 2019 it was safe to depict lesbians on screen being lesbians, but you would not know Laurel is a transwoman from anything in the film itself. Now, of course, if you’re already bringing that knowledge to the table, yes -- there are one or two lines between Laurel and Mark referring vaguely to her past, accompanied by a knowing look, that are obviously this backstory. But that (and the above outro-credits promise not to get pregnant in the fourth movie) are about all you’re getting.



And honestly, that’s perfectly fine. Despite any marketing airs it might have had, this film is very much not trying to say things. It would not be better with a transmisogyny plot, or if Duke was a radfem. There are films that can handle that stuff, and they are not Bit (2019), the film that handles the villain’s tragic background of mind controlled sex slavery with a montage set to an up-tempo remix of Boney M.’s “Rasputin.” So let’s talk about that: Duke’s backstory is that, along with the vampire woman she’s consigned to the pit, Siran, she was previously a bride of Dracula -- when she was a runaway sex worker in Europe, he turned her into a vampire and forced her to be his servant. This is not played for humor, precisely; Vlad is somewhat schlubbed up to show there’s nothing remarkable about him and that he was not some irresistible carnal monster, just a guy with mind control powers. Still, like. C’mon. It’s a montage set to “Rasputin.” Despite its own subject matter, that’s too fun a song for this spot; or at least, it would be if the film itself was expecting you to do more than get the ick about the gross vampire rapist and be happy she killed him, which she does in short order.

So the ideological disagreement between Laurel and Duke boils down to first, whether or not to kill their victims, and second, whether or not to kill all male vampires; Duke is into killing, Laurel prefers the sustainable, liberal, humanitarian vampire option of…it’s unclear in the film how this will work long-term, but any long-running Vampire: The Requiem playgroup can probably give you a rundown on how they’d implement kinder, gentler monstrous predation. At last their conflict comes to blows on the terrain of Mark, who is finally useful to the plot of this story when Laurel bites him (she’s been doing the “I won’t feed because I don’t want to kill people” thing with the predictable results) and he begins turning himself. In the course of their fight, the heart of Dracula is introduced as a macguffin, and when Laurel frees Siran from the pit she frees Vlad as well, who immediately chugs her like a beer and reasserts his mind control over everyone present. When he uses it on Laurel, she’s able to resist him just enough to do the old lighter and moonshine spit trick to breathe fire and light him up. (I saw this described elsewhere as Laureal resisting his mind control more than the other women because she’s trans; this is not in the text as I read it. The mind control works on Laurel, she gets the camera defocus and stiff body movement of being compelled to act against her will; offscreen, before she’s controlled, she prepares an attack for one moment in his lapse of concentration, takes her shot, and one-hit kills him. It’s clever ingenuity and strong willpower defeating hubris like you’d see in any other version of this plot.)



We clean up matters by locking Duke in the pit (for the time being, it’s stressed) to learn her lesson and with Laurel, who has usurped leadership of the coven, feasting on the heart of Dracula along with her fellow witches to gain his power. Siran’s back here, so she got better after Vlad attacked her. So what are the ramifications of that? Well, a kinder and gentler coven of vampire witches running Los Angeles, one imagines; we’re outta here, it’s time for credits.

It’s a perfectly fun little film. It’s good for teens, probably; they’re certainly the target audience with the “be true to yourself, and don’t be a rapist” messaging and the general vibes, but you know how millennials making movies for zoomer teens turns out. Sometimes we’re just making movies for ourselves. Anyway, the bulk of it that matters is Laurel, Duke, and Izzy bouncing off each other for the middle third of the film and if you find those actors and characters charming, as I did, you’ll find this a perfectly acceptable way to spend precisely 90 minutes.



Next week, the original Buffy the Vampire Slayer. The film; I’m not reviewing the whole show. Not yet, at least.
