What We Know About 11th Edition 40k So Far: Recapping the New Rules Reveals Through May 15th

May 16th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Welcome back to our weekly recap of the reveals of 11th Edition Warhammer 40k. We're now in the second week of Games Workshop's Faction Focus reveals, complete with newly-discussed Detachments as well as core rules. Getting a glimpse at the systems & philosophy behind 11th Edition 40k via these previews has been a treat, and there's no sign of stopping the excitement until Armageddon drops!

If you missed any of our coverage so far, you can find the older articles here:
  • Adepticon First Look
  • Reveals Through April 11 - Detachments, List Building, Force Dispositions, Cover, Objectives, Towering, Plunging Fire, Terrain
  • Reveals Through May 1 - Engagement Range, Charging, Reserves, Pile-ins and Consolidation, Fights First
  • Reveals Through May 9th - Battle-shock, Stealth, Close Quarters, Heavy, Orks, Marines, Imperial Guard, Tyranids, Chaos Space Marines,
At the current pace of reveals, a Faction Focus will be released for each faction by early June. The goal with this recap series is to condense & extrapolate using the information that's been officially revealed, and boil it down to exactly what you need to know. With that prelude out of the way, what's new this week?

Sisters Showcase New Detachment Styles

Credit: Dunkelbrau

First up in terms of reveals is the Sisters of Battle, the zealous forces of the Adeptus Sororitas. Sisters has a shockingly deep model range, albeit mostly in the form of Characters, and especially thematic combinations of these storied saints-to-be form a key facet of their identity in 11th Edition.
  • Sanctified Orators has a line of text I expect we'll see show up in armies for whom Leaders are key, in that its enhancements don't count towards the maximum available to an army. The rule beyond that is minimal, but as the Faction Focus states, you're paying the 1 Detachment Point for the luxury of paying for these 'especially good' enhancements. This is illustrated immediately with the Hagiomnifex, which (once per turn) gives you access to one of five different abilities, all of which feel slightly less strong than a Stratagem individually. That said, given Stratagems are only once per unit, per phase, the ability to stack them alongside buffs via non-strat means could lead to similar key units as we've seen in 10th.
  • Speaking of rules repeating, the same line that's appeared before for the Guard's 'please still play artillery' Detachment arrives again, with the Chorus of Condemnation. That phrase—to increase the Detection Range of a targeted unit by 3"—is meant to help make long-range firepower feel a little bit better. In fact, one of the Detachment's Stratagems specifically calls out Exorcists, which would otherwise have trouble impacting the game from afar. Indirect Fire was a problem multiple times in 10th Edition, though, so I imagine the designers will remain conservative on this front; going from 15" to 18" still means you need to press into the midboard, and being on a Rhino chassis there's still worry of being blown to bits from some stray Lascannon fire. Notably, though, this does not preclude you from taking it alongside other Detachments, as we've seen with similar recent reveals; if the DP costs work out, you might be able to go deep on Jump Pack units alongside Army of Faith as a fantastic finesse-oriented force.
  • For those looking to play more elite, the Sacred Champions delivers, calling out Celestian Sacresants by name and providing them a bevy of buffs that should seem familiar to anyone who played Champions of Faith in 10th Edition. Yes, here you can also give them +1 WS/BS, and potentially +1OC via an enhancement, and Faithful Fortitude mimics the old Shield of Denial, providing Feel No Pain versus Mortals. The degree to which this is similar to a differently-named Detachment implies that there's huge changes abound for Champions of Faith...or perhaps that Sacred Champions is simply replacing it for 1DP. It remains to be seen if thematically & mechanically-similar Detachments will exist in parallel at different Detachment Point costs, depending on how deep you want to skew your individual list, but the similarities are so egregious I'd imagine Champions of Faith goes the way of the dodo.
It's been confirmed that each of these Detachments costs a mere 1DP, meaning you could fold them together, or combine one with a Codex Detachment, likely all to be 2-3DP. Bringers of Flame, however, has been specifically called out as being 2DP.

Detachment Tags Control for Overlap

We've seen a pervasive bit of text at the end of many newly-revealed Detachments, which provides the Detachment itself with a tag, forbidding you from combining it with another of the same tag. That being said, in no single article have two Detachments which share a tag been shown off, as of yet; we don't know how widespread these may be, among existing Detachments, but suffice to say it's a good balance lever for the designers to have. This also comes down to controlling for skew lists that already exist, and would purely benefit from additional rules for units they're already playing. Recon Element & Designation Force for the Astra Militarum probably key off of similar units, so taking both could afford too much benefit to units you're already happy taking, given these benefits can in theory stack. This is all to say, the Detachment Point system is intended to make for more diverse army lists, not less, so it can be expected your existing force may need a splash of some other portion of the army to get full benefit.

World Eaters Go Elite

World Eaters has been an odd duck in 10th, rarely a bad army, but with one singular Detachment hogging nearly all the top placements: Berserker Warband. The 1DP Detachments revealed are meant to give those generalist lists more directions, and specific centerpieces to base themselves around.
  • If there's one thing you'll hear from a World Eaters fan, it's that they yearn for the Red Butchers in 40k—dedicated Terminators descended from the original Legion's most rabid stock. While there's no new models, the Butchers of Khorne gives a savvy player the ability to live out that fantasy, and stack Blessings of Khorne across huge chunks of 2+ save bodies. Butchers transplants the 10th Edition-style Vessels of Wrath rule to gain an extra Blessing for specific units onto a Terminator-centric Detachment, and alongside means of making them 2+ to hit and potentially able to crack Vehicles & Monsters, a more elite bent to the army seems possible.
  • Speaking of Vessels of Wrath, though, it's been simplified and shifted to 1DP, now being World Eaters' Character-centric option. When a Character's fighting, you can either get CLEAVE 1 (A new keyword that functions as a melee BLAST) or +1AP, both of which do a ton for carving through specific units. This seems especially well thought-out, as World Eaters has a plethora of ways to give out Sustained/Lethal hits, so tacking those on would be redundant. This is also a fairly generic option, as every army's going to have some number of Leaders, so pairing it with a favored 2DP Detachment is an easy sell. Taking your Slaughterbound and using its once per game ability, plus potentially every Blessing, give or take a 1CP Stratagem for +1A/+2S, and you're set to nuke nearabouts anything.
  • Finally, tagged as Onslaught is Brazen Engines. Contrary to Goretrack Onslaught, which is mostly meant for your Rhinos & Land Raiders, this keys off of DAEMON VEHICLES, forcing Battle-shock tests at -1LD for starting in combat with them. This is awesome for denying strats in the Combat Phase, but critically if you intend to wipe out the unit you're engaged with, the changes to Battle-shock matter less for you. After all, while it no longer wears off until passed, being dead precludes you from having OC anyway. Instead, this serves as a potent deterrent for being in combat with powerful choices like the Defiler or Forgefiend, increasing their survivability and punishing opponents for stepping up; the Defiler especially loves this, with its comically large 160mm base. This is all accentuated with Apoplectic Clarity, letting you ignore modifiers in either fighting...or shooting, which certainly helps the new cover rules sting a bit less.
Credit: Games Workshop.

The noncommittal language surrounding which existing Detachments are 2DP vs 3DP leads us to infer that almost every army will have at least one option priced at the full 3...and for World Eaters more than nearly any faction, that choice is clear. The more interesting question is whether non-Berserker Warband Detachments see a rise in playability in 11th, now that you can field the world's killiest Slaughterbound with 20 ablative wounds, in something like Blood Cult+Vessels of Wrath.

T'au Control the Shooting Phase

XV26 Stealth Battlesuit. Credit: Greg Chiasson

The throughline for Tau's reveals this week come in the form of the Hidden rule—as a reminder, if an INFANTRY, BEAST, or SWARM hasn't yet shot, and is within a Terrain Area (footprint), it functionally has a 15" Lone Operative rule. This is devastating for armies that rely on long-range firepower, and Tau is perhaps the faction most dedicated to that archetype.
  • Auxiliary Cadre returns as a 1DP Detachment, letting your Kroot & Vespids provide the +3" Detection Range text to units within 12" of them, and similarly, Ghostkeels & Stealth Suits allow their alien allies to remain Hidden after shooting while within 6". This new focus on pairing your auxiliaries with the stealthier Battlesuits is interesting, and the Ghostkeel has been a mainstay of Tau armies throughout 10th, but it's difficult to say if the reliance on non-Crisis, non-Riptide units is too bitter a pill to swallow.
  • That said, the ability to remain Hidden isn't just for Auxillary Cadre, as the Advanced Acquisition Cadre applies that rule to Pathfinders & Stealth Battlesuits...though critically, not Ghostkeels, given they aren't INFANTRY/BEAST/SWARM. There's tools here which can turn the Detection Range of an enemy within 24" to 24", or give the old +1 to saves that cover did in 10th, but ultimately I worry if there's substantial enough impact here to warrant its inclusion. The moment you're facing an army that can close the distance, that extra Hidden potential dries up, and Stealth as a rule is redundant if you already need to be in cover to receive Hidden.
  • Finally, Experimental Prototype Cadre returns as a dedicated Character Detachment, and it's bringing back the risk-reward gameplay that's been a blast in 10th. Providing 6" of range to all Character guns is solid enough, but do note that this is worse than it would be in 10th, given it does nothing to extend the Detection Range of smaller targets. Still, if you want to put the hurt on armored forces, there's a bevy of ways to increase Strength in the Shooting Phase, and crack tough targets if you risk a few mortals in return.
Going purely off what we've seen, it's hard to gauge the true power of Tau's capacity to remain hidden. Per rules reveals, it only works while in a Terrain Area, not just while receiving the Benefit of Cover, so forcing units that already have Stealth to sit inside footprints feels a bit like putting a hat on a hat. Still, if the Force Dispositions see action/objective-oriented play be rewarded enough, having truly untouchable teams of Pathfinders and Stealth Suits could be the start to a frustratingly untouchable faction in 11th.

Custodes Keep their Toys

Telemon Heavy Dreadnought. Credit: Pendulin

The specter of squatting has loomed large over the Adeptus Custodes, whose range is made up in part of kits also used in Horus Heresy. After so many armies lost access to these 'Relic' models at the outset of 10th Edition, the lions of The Emperor have been rightfully nervous about seeing how their faction might change in 11th. Be not afraid: At a bare minimum, you're keeping your new plastic Dreadnoughts.
  • March of the Moritoi makes your Dreadnoughts fast, letting them book it up the board with an extra +2" to move, and an added +1" for Advanced/Charge rolls. You'll notice this is the same bonus provided by the current Solar Spearhead, and with this being tagged 'Armoury', it can be assumed the two cannot be taken together given what we've seen so far. This is paired with a CP reduction for the new Crushing Impact—11th's version of Tank Shock—incentivizing you to throw gold-plated armor at your opponent with all alacrity.
  • Untagged, however, is Silent Hunters, allowing advancing Sisters of Silence to also perform actions, and providing them a 12" ability to add 3" to a target's Detection Range. Both of those are commonplace across 1DP Detachments, but together the potential for Sister squads as utility pieces seems solid indeed! There's an enhancement for Explosives (the new term for Grenades), with some added bonuses against Psykers, but the meat here is the ability to give Witchseekers BLAST 1 for 1CP. For any rebel trying to make Null Maiden Vigil work, this is a huge buff...but the bar was low.
  • Tharanatoi Hammerblow is all about putting obnoxious amounts of Allarus Terminators in your opponent's face, and asking them to try killing them. Not only do you get to reroll charges with your Terminators after coming in via Ingress, but the Mnemo-Locked Shrine Cipher even lets you Deep Strike on the first turn. Ingress moves are a great way to get around Hidden, by appearing 8" away, and while it's still just a coin flip to hit a charge from Deep Strike with your reroll, playing an otherwise resilient list and absolutely crippling the opponent's opener half of the time could be worthwhile. It's like No Country for Old Men, but with gold-plated juggernauts.
While we haven't heard about the other refreshed Custodes units, such as the Venatari & Coronus, it would make sense they return as well, given the other plastic introduced to the range in their Horus Heresy refresh is remaining legal in Warhammer 40k. Moreover, the designers mentioned the Telemon by name in the March of the Moritoi overview, so that's also in the clear. As far as things like the Aquilon Terminators, etc go, I'd be a bit more hesitant to purchase them at present. While exceptions are made for Custodes with regards to their Horus Heresy arsenal, it would not be unreasonable to expect some units be culled at the start of 11th, from Custodes or other armies, based on how new edition launches have gone in the past.

More Core Rule Reveals

Credit: Games Workshop.
  • The new version of Tank Shock, Crushing Impact, is not without a bit of risk. After all, using your own hull as a battering ram might cause a few dents, here in the form of Mortal Wounds returned on each 1 rolled. This does disincentivize using your heavily-damaged Monsters & Vehicles to finish off units, as you might find it a pyrrhic maneuver with some bad rolls.
  • Cleave & Blast work similarly, providing +1A per 5 models in the target unit, but notably can come in Cleave 1/2/3/etc, giving the designers an extra tool to use for especially splashy weapons, much the same as Sustained Hits.

The Reach of the Death Guard Increases

The pervasive malaise that follows Nurgle's Faithful came to a head in 10th Edition, with Death Guard being a scourge of its middle third for competitive play. On the whole, what's been showcased for 11th Edition attempts to deal with the faction's main weakness—speed—but the tools afforded to Death Guard are already solid, so they're one of the strongest beneficiaries of keeping current codex rules. Even in spite of a number of nerfs, the fundamentals for the faction are strong, so points will be the main determinant.
  • Contagion Engines supports the mechanized arm of the Death Guard, bundling all their Bloat Drones, Blight Haulers, & Helbrutes into a new group: CONTAGION ENGINE. These light vehicles all gain ASSAULT on their guns, and are afforded on-demand Affliction for 1CP, meaning your Helbrutes can in theory throw anti-armor downrange at +1 to wound, and functionally +1AP, via Rattlejoint Ague. Still, given these parts of the army tend to function as trade pieces, dedicating 1DP to giving them extra movement flexibility doesn't seem especially thrilling; you want to position these such that you kill something of equal or greater value, and then explode.
  • I think Flyblown Host may be the most exciting Detachment to come out of this week, as it provides Infiltrators to 2 Plague Marine units, letting you position them aggressively and in Terrain Areas for easy Hidden. Instead of needing to pay the Rhino tax to avoid footslogging your lethal brick of marines, you can instead position them in the midboard from the get-go, with access to the same 1CP 10th Edition Stratagem to shoot while in melee (now CLOSE-QUARTERS rather than PISTOL). I'm extremely bullish on this Detachment, putting your normally-slow INFANTRY brick exactly where it needs to be, with the only caveat being that the Foul Blightspawn loses a lot of value from the nerfs to Fights First. Perhaps they might be replaced with the Noxious Blightbringer, to force a ton of Battle-shock tests?
  • Last up is Paragons of Putrescence, the dedicated Character Detachment for Death Guard, providing +3" of Contagion Range for their units. That's right, units, meaning paired with something like the above Flyblown Host you can Infiltrate two bricks of led Plague Marines whose Contagion Ranges start the battle at 6". This also helps your Character-led Terminator bricks arriving from Deep Strike, and it seems likely nearly everything can consider itself wracked with pox from Battle Round 2 onward, if positioned correctly. You can even double up on chosen Plagues via an enhancement, or spend 1CP to grab an extra 6" of Detection Range for a unit in range of your Character brick. On the whole, this seems tailor-made to pair with Flyblown Host, but elsewhere it's probably...fine.
Credit: Games Workshop.

This is the first Faction Focus for whom I've felt all three of the revealed 1DP Detachments form a reasonable core in their own right, with the synergies between Paragons & Flyblown Host solving a lot of the army's issues. Contagion Engines doesn't especially excite, but you'll likely feel fine jamming some Foetid Bloat-Drones in existing lists that have 1DP to spare, given their capacity to trade. Still, the thought of Infiltrating Plague Marines for 1DP seems a stand-out, and pressures the opponent immediately to deal with them, or suffer.

Clarifications Arrive from the FAQ Stream

This past Friday, Warhammer Community fielded a number of questions collected from players to provide some insight into the minutia of changes coming in 11th Edition. This was unexpected, but welcome, and a shotgun approach to rules reveals has given us some insight into fairly impactful changes to things like Psychic, Hazardous, & healing...while not showcasing the literal text of these rules. Given that, take each of these bullet points with a grain of salt.

Tau Riptide. Credit: Jack Hunter
  • HAZARDOUS and Desperate Escape have been unified into a 'Hazard Roll', rolling dice based on the number of models in a unit, failed on a 1 or 2. This, instead of removing a model or taking 3 Mortal Wounds, is simply 1 Mortal Wound inflicted to the given unit attempting to flee or fire weapons; while you'll be failing twice as many of these rolls than previously, the damage still works out to be far less. The effects of this change vary based on the wounds characteristic of a given unit. For models with Hazardous that have 3 wounds, like Kataphron Destroyers with Plasma Culverins, this is a massive buff, given you don't lose a model per failed roll. For units with 2 wounds, like Hellblasters, there's functionally no difference, and for all those lovely Plasma Pistols on 1W models, you'll be dying far more often. We've yet to see the specific wording of this change, but on the whole it should shake up the playability of high-risk squads with 3 wounds.
  • As expected, Stealth & Cover don't stack, instead with the former simply applying the latter regardless of whether you're hiding by a Terrain Area or not.
  • Combat Disembark/Disembarking from a destroyed Transport does not count as a Charge, meaning no Charge bonuses.
    • The three means of getting into the fray are Charging, Disembarking, or Surging, all of which have slightly different meanings and rule implications.
  • No armies are being squatted, meaning despite its lack of mention in the Space Marine Faction Focus, Deathwatch remains in 11th Edition.
  • Different Detachments have different numbers of provided Stratagems, likely with 1DP Detachments providing 3, and the rest providing 6.
  • Army sizes are about the same, and the relative weakness of things like Artillery given the changes to things like Hidden are being accounted-for via points. Infantry should be a bit more expensive, and long-range shooting a bit less so.
  • No rerolls to hit with Overwatch, with changes made to it & Heroic Intervention meant to smooth out phases and reduce 'gotcha's.
  • Healing now returns models, if you could bring back a number of wounds worth equivalent to dead models. If you heal 3 wounds, you can bring back 1 single 3W model. There's a chance this also works to bring back dead models at remaining wounds equivalent to the healing value, which would be absolutely insane for things like multi-model VEHICLE units.
  • Indirect Fire can target Hidden units, but use the new Snap Shots rule when shooting like this, only hitting on 6s. If you have another unit which can see the target, however, you have a better chance to hit, even though the target is technically not visible to the artillery piece. This...is concerning.
  • Point granularity exists for 'big ticket' items, such as different guns on a Riptide Battlesuit costing different amounts. For small changes like guns on Infantry, though, it should work as it does in 10th Edition.
  • Heresy-era Knight chassis remain legal.
  • Genestealer Cults have a character-specific Detachment(s) similar to the Marine variant that buffs solely the Emperor's Champion, denoting a specifically-storied rebel within your uprising.
  • PSYCHIC attacks ignore modifiers to hit and wound, as a true weapon keyword. This is functionally Ignores Cover for free, on top of ignoring negative modifiers to wound. Rejoice, Grey Knight & Thousand Son players. We've seen some abilities/Stratagems that resolve as 'Psychic Attacks', meaning they likely ignore modifiers as well.
  • Not only will unpainted terrain be produced as normal, but Games Workshop is debuting brand-new 'prepainted'/finished terrain for 11th Edition, making the assembly of a table far easier. You can read our initial review of this news here.

Final Thoughts

Following the FAQ Stream, a lot of the systems & philosophy behind 11th Edition have come into focus. There's an increased emphasis on visibility, proper target selection, and tactically-engaging skirmishes, while removing much of the finickiness surrounding reactive play. Next week's reveals begin with the Faction Focus for both Imperial & Chaos Knights, always a contentious topic at the outset of a new edition. The train has left the station in terms of 11th's hype, so expect another review of everything revealed this coming week next Saturday! There are a ton of sources to scour for these, from WarCom to videos to socials, and we're happy to put it in one place for you.
