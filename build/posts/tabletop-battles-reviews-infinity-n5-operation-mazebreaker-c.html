Tabletop Battles Reviews: Infinity N5 Operation Mazebreaker Corregidor Rules and Units

May 12th, 2026 via Goonhammer

Introduction

With the preview information in our greasy hands, it’s time to look at the rules behind an updated Corregidor Sectorial. The big news here is the split between a Surface Division and a Deep Space Division. The big caveat is that we don’t have the full picture - we have a set of preview information (many thanks to Corvus Belli), which includes rosters for both Divisions, a Fireteam Chart for Surface Division, and profiles for the 5 new units, as well as for the 2 existing units included in the Mazebreaker box, which have been tweaked. We do not have any access to the full Army update which is coming for Corregidor, so there are a lot of unknowns, which we will highlight as they come up. Ultimately, without any information on updates to existing units, or indeed any changes to the Fireteams of Deep Space Division, an overall verdict on either flavour of the new Corregidor Sectorial, how they stack up against each other and against other Sectorials in the game, won’t be reached just yet. We aim to publish a revised Corregidor Faction Focus article as soon as possible once that update drops. 

Let’s dive into the details.

Faction Split: Surface & Deep Space Divisions

We aren't sure exactly how this split will be displayed in the Army app. Our initial read was that the two Divisions are effectively separate, but heavily overlapping, Sectorials, but we’re told that lists will still be selected from within the Corregidor Sectorial option in the app, and Division-exclusive units will be marked out somehow. The inference is that players could take Corregidor to an event, and submit one list of Surface, and one list of Deep Space. This is new organisational territory for Infinity, so we will have to wait on more details. 

To put it bluntly, all of the new stuff is for the Surface Division. They receive all 5 of the new units, none of which join the Deep Space Division, but they lose access to some of the existing units. 



As we can see, Surface Division gains access to the 5 new units, which we will look at in detail below, but here’s the summary:
  • Territorials are gunfighting MI (BS13 with Marksmanship) which are also specialists, and mostly Tactical Awareness profiles. They offer a new Lt option for Surface Div.
  • Coyotes are gunfighting MI (BS13 with Warhorse, MSV1 and BS Attack +1SD)) which are also specialists. Sounds like an overlap, but beyond the details, these guys have a selection of profiles with FD+4” and Synchronised Peripherals, the Bambabots. Both the Coyotes and their little pals are Forward Observers, so this is a really interesting fighting specialist sort of unit. 
  • Bambadroids are a REM Trooper that is sort of a variant of the Bambabot, much like existing REMs that have synchronised and FTO versions. Players are limited to 1 Bambadroid per Coyote, which must be in the same Combat Group. These are nice, relatively cheap Fireteam filler with utility kit. 
  • Shiftas are a support/utility LI. They don't have particularly great fighting skills, but are Fireteam Wildcards with Strategic Deployment, and can bring EM minelayers, or Chain of Command and a Discoballer, for reasonable prices. They are also all Specialists (we’re seeing a trend here) and Sensors.
  • Vigilantes are the first purely non-Fireteam new unit. They are forward deploying, Camouflaged MI, all various stripes of Specialist (again), and all profiles bring Counterintelligence. As welcome as that is, and even though camo specialists in the midfield are a staple of good Objective play, this strikes us as one of the less optimised new units. They are capable, but you pay a very fair price (25-29pts) for them. 
  • Bearcats are the only HI in the new release, and bring a more aggressive flavour as seriously fighty Combat Jump/Parachutists. They are the most mobile, as well as most resilient, new Corregidor unit - 6-2 with Super-Jump (Jet Propulsion) is very nice to have with their airborne deployment skills. All their profiles are Specialists (this is just getting ridiculous now), with most featuring short range weapons, which keeps their price very reasonable. We know every experienced player is thinking about the dangers of that kit on Hackable models, but the Bearcats are all Warhorse, and include Hacker profiles with inbuilt Firewall(-3). This looks like a very fun addition.
In return for this, Surface Division loses access to Gators, Hellcats, Tomcats, Carlota Kowalsky, EVAders and Sombra. 

Note that both flavours of Corregidor are, per the preview info, losing Tsyklon & Lunokhod Remotes, and the Raveneye (an AVA1 loaner troop from O-12). We believe this to be an editing oversight - someone simply forgot to list them - since it would be unprecedented to strip out a couple of uncontroversial, in-production units.

How do these gains and losses make Surface Division look compared to Deep Space Division? 
  • It does seem slightly stronger. The units it gives up [all of this is pending any adjustments] aren't particularly unique capabilities. The Gator is a good TAG, but many players prefer the Iguana. EVAders offer Specialist budget HI, but weren't regarded as highly optimised. They’re probably less competitive than the Specialist MI Fireteam units (Territorials and Coyotes) in this update, which offer gunfighting skills. The old Corregidor airborne units, while good for Objective play, are less threatening than the Bearcats. Sombras are a wash for Vigilantes, the units do similar roles, neither are vital to the Sectorial. Against the losses, various fighting Specialist profiles offer up new angles of play, but most importantly, the defensive capabilities of Switcha and Bambadroids will help cover up some existing weaknesses.
  • Honestly, the old selling points of Corregidor on the competitive scene - an excellent repeater net, good hackers, widespread Specialists, decent TAGs, remain unchanged and are equally present in both flavours of Corregidor. Ditto for some of the fun, fairly playable elements like Correctional (Lobo/Diablo/Jaguar) teams, the ability to Haris Gecko TAGs, and some iconic troops like Intruders or Mobile Brigada. 
  • Some of the new ‘feel’ of Surface Div also rests on their more permissive Fireteam chart, with liberal application of the (Corregidor) tag; this has also been extended to Deep Space and makes that Division feel at least a bit refreshed. 

Surface Division Impressions

Does Surface Division seem like a fun, strong Sectorial on its own merits? It seems fine, but not an enormous step over the existing reputation of Corregidor. Better, certainly, but we don't think it challenges the current top meta factions. These are still Sectorials without some big tools like Mimetism-6 or Hidden Deployment; their multi-wound models lack any significant gunfighting skills; many of their coolest signature units are fundamentally still Fireteams of mid-cost infantry walking up the field without special mobility. 

That sounds quite critical, but we do expect Surface Division to be a solidly strong Sectorial. My long term position has been that N5 Corregidor was incredibly close to being good. Fireteam changes do help bring the more lacklustre units up towards the level of the excellent solo pieces like McMurrough and the Iguana. Time will tell how the community reacts to the potentially oppressive parts of competitive Corregidor (e.g. Bandits deploying Repeaters directly outside the enemy DZ) being available with less drag and dead weight.

Deep Space Division Impressions



The only information we have available here is the above roster, which as existing Corregidor players can see, adds nothing. It removes a couple of things, but as noted we think those might be errors. There’s not much to say except that Deep Space is old Corregidor, and we don’t know how it will change until we’ve seen the Fireteam Chart and all the changes to existing profiles - we don’t know any details, but were assured that there are some! 

Clearly, the competitive relevance of this Division will depend heavily on whether EVAders, Sombra, and the drop troopers get meaningful buffs, and whether the Fireteams become more flexible. We would like to think, with the strong structure of Surface Div Fireteams, we would see a mirrored improvement to Deep Space’s options, and that alone would help their forlorn Corregidor-Fireteam units. But unless there are some profile tweaks, and they include the Deep Space-exclusive units, the danger is that Deep Space will never be chosen over Surface Division.

Gripe Opinion 

This could have been one update to a unified Corregidor Sectorial. Pre-Mazebreaker Corregidor had 38 units in its roster. 5 new units have been added. Very welcome indeed, but not exactly unprecedented. Imperial Service, when reworked, saw 8 entirely new units, with major changes to many of their existing units, taking them to 44 in their roster. We haven't done a mathematical average, but to pick some other examples, OSS is coming out of this box with 46 units; Next Wave, the newest Sectorial, has 35; Bakunin, a long established and notably broad Sectorial, has 48; on the high end, vanilla PanOceania has 51 and vanilla Combined Army 53. Admittedly, some Sectorials are more focussed with numbers in the high-30s, but the only ones with significantly narrower rosters are the out of print (especially Ariadnan) types. CB could have just added the 5 new units to Corregidor and kept it simple. It wouldn’t have been anything unusual in terms of Sectorial size or design. If there were concerns about possible combinations in Fireteams, whether for thematic or game design reasons, those could have been dealt with by a more complex Fireteams chart, with Corregidor, Surface Div and Deep Space Div Fireteams. Some choices could be restricted, e.g. Switcha and Bambadroids might be options for new-look teams, instead of on the Wildcards list, if their use was a concern.

In a game with 45 factions, what sense does it make to add a pseudo-46th, rather than risk making the 45th faction marginally deeper? Organisationally, this is just another hurdle for new Corregidor players to get their heads around. We’ll have to explain to them that those EVAders are sold in a ‘Corregidor Fireteam Alpha’ box, yes, but no, they can’t be in an army list with most of the Mazebreaker box. 

We wracked our brains here and struggled to find anything that made a single, unified Corregidor list unworkable or overegged for competitive play. Honestly, it seems like the standout strongest  units, the ones experienced players might identify as the high points of the faction that they expect to see across the table, are common to both Divisions anyway. 

I appreciate that from a fluff-oriented, background perspective, CB may well expect players to define themselves as just Corregidor players and dabble in both rosters. But the Division split does affect the Sectorial’s power on the game table. It has minor, but real downsides over a single roster. Keeping opponents unsure, at deployment and through the game, of what you might have off the table, or under your camo markers, who your Lt might be, makes a real difference. Opponents of the new Corregidor divisions won't have to sweat over whether they’re defending against the brute force of a Bearcat or the precise backline entry of Carlota; those units don't exist in the same list. That may seem like a partisan appeal for a stronger Sectorial, but I’d say it’s more of a plea for some measure of flexibility. There are plenty of other factions which can pressure opponents with greater numbers of threat vectors - why not give Corregidor even a bit of that capability?

While I'm always glad to see new stuff, and the new things here (models and rules) are genuinely interesting and stylish, the organisation of them leaves a bitter taste. Corregidor got a quality of life update back in Operation Crimson Stone (released in 2022), facing off against Kosmoflot. New (and Updated) Corregidor Units

If you’re a dedicated Corregidor player, or thinking about becoming one, now we’re going to dig into each of the units in the bundle in turn. 

Territorials



These are Fireteam-intended gunfighters. They seem like good examples of that MI gunfighting archetype; not as loaded with skills as, say, Next Wave’s Stingers, but Marksmanship is perhaps the single best gunfighting skill for a high-Burst, Active turn model. There’s also value in being a Specialist with Tactical Awareness in a Fireteam - there aren’t many turns where that TacAware Order won’t be as useful as an extra Regular Order. Beyond a fairly high points cost, there are some weaknesses. They lack a template, so want protection from minelayers and/or ablative models. Their high burst weaponry (AP Marksman rifle or AP Red Fury) is only PS7, so might struggle against resilient AROs, and only good to 24”.

These are models which very much rely on being in Fireteams for that extra boost, but beware of the strategic risks in moving multiple 1VITA models together into positions where they can be counter-attacked. There’s some very clear synergy with Shiftas here, for defense and Strategic Deployment, to get them that bit closer when playing first. At 1VITA, but Shock and Crit Immune, Territorials could also benefit from a Daktari to follow them around and pick them up if they cop an unlucky ARO.

Finally, Territorials can be Lts. These would fall squarely into the Wildcat Lt trap - expensive without being tough - but there is CoC in-faction now (see Shiftas again) so an active Lt which essentially carries 3 Orders, rather than using a slot on a more defensible Lt which just gets squirreled away in a corner, might have uses. The problem is that means going without, or abandoning, the Fireteam they rely on so much.

Note that Territorials have FTO marked on the Fireteams chart; no profiles have FTO marked and there doesn’t seem to be any major profile difference which would merit some to be in teams over others. We will have to wait and see if any of their profiles are excluded from teams, and if so, those won’t be nearly as popular. Of those we see now, the Engineer profiles are cute, and possibly usable in support of a TAG, but ultimately not what you take an expensive gunfighter for. We are more drawn to the high-Burst weapons. X-Visors aren’t terrible, given Marksmanship, but given the low PS, we value the higher Burst, as well as the Specialist status, on the AP Red Fury. Whether a Lieutenant or not, we consider that the obvious profile for this unit. Musterkrux: Normally I hate the MI profile design paradigm (particularly in Nomads) but if you’re going to give me a BS 13 AP-Red Fury with Marksmanship, who is also a Specialist with Tactical Awareness for 30 points, I am going to drink all up and ask for more. One of those and a Daktari as a Corregidor Duo seems super sweet.

Coyotes (and Bambabots/Bambadroids)



This is another MI, Spec Trained Troop. Their gunfighting skills are BS13, Warhorse, MSV1, and the superb BS Attack (+1SD). They are also universally Forward Observers - a skill which will benefit from +1SD! This is a promising profile on its own merits, but the Coyotes are a bit of a complex unit. They have FD+4” profiles, each with a single Bambabot, a Peripheral (Synchronised). Alternatively they have FTO profiles without. Bambadroids are their own, Order-generating models, but you can only take 0-1 for each Coyote in your list. This is functionally similar to certain other units, e.g. Scylla, where there is a profile with a synchronised companion remote, and a profile with an FTO remote. RAW from the preview info, a Coyote with a Bambabot Peripheral still allows you to take a Bambadroid (they must be in the same Combat Group). This is attractive as the Bambadroids are very handy cheap-ish defensive and utility models, and are (Corregidor). Note that RAW they also don’t need to be in a Fireteam with their Coyote, so there are lots of possible combinations. 

Note that this is a Peripheral unit - the profile was displayed like this in preview information, presumably for clarity.

Bambas of both flavours are unarmoured, 1STR Remotes with Mimetism(-3), 360 Visors and PS6 chain rifles. In each case there is a choice between an AP Mine Dispenser, or a combo of D-Charges, Flash Pulse and Deployable Repeater. The differences are the Bambadroid (beyond being its own trooper) gets ECM Hacker(-3), Remote Presence, and a ranged weapon, with BS11, and so costs more points. The Bambabot 

The Synchronised Coyote profiles have a very cool niche as active-turn fighting specialists. While not conventionally that resilient - they’re 27-33pts for a unit where the controller is 1VITA - they can defend themselves with their +1SD, and in extremis have a boarding pistol and the Bambabot’s chain rifle. The multi marksman rifle, although the most expensive, is the most obviously attractive as an Active turn piece. The shotgun profile is relying on non-lethal solutions outside 8”; the hacker is versatile (a KHD with Upgrade: Carbonite), but similarly short ranged,  and vulnerable to enemy hackers without any marker state or even Firewalls. Its E/Mitter is a great weapon, but we prefer it in a Fireteam. Bambabots, as Forward Observers, have a situationally very useful form of BS Attack, and with the Coyote, give a relatively reliable way to activate mission Objectives. The AP Mine Dispenser is a valuable way to set up forks or lay down defences a good length ahead of what you need to protect, but bear in mind the BS10 bot shouldn’t expect to successfully lay 2 mines in a single Order. Our instinct is that the Flash Pulse/Deployable Repeater Bambabot profile is going to be more useful as the Peripheral for an Active Turn fighting Specialist, with the Bambadroid (see below) better suited for mine-dispensing. Situationally, the D-charges will be useful for objectives, and in some situations, just having a Flash Pulse makes the Bambabot an excellent speed bump ARO. But both options look eminently takeable.

Coyotes have an apparently new skill, Dodge(+3ARM). Now we don’t have the full rules text, but with N5’s language conventions, it seems fairly clear to us. If the model declares Dodge, they benefit from +3ARM in the resolution of the Order. Honestly that does make them a bit more resilient to things like template attacks. In most situations, you’d want to shoot, especially because of +1SD, but when you’re forced to Dodge, which are the most dangerous situations, they are effectively an ARM5 unit. As we understand it, you don’t need to actually pass the roll or win the FtF to benefit. Handy!

Instead of taking the Coyote with Bambabot , you could use a Coyote FTO, which in a Fireteam benefits from a powerful additional +1SD. We’ve seen that rules stack before, and units with +1SD natively, on top of +1SD from a Fireteam, are usually strong and popular. This is a major addition to Corregidor. Here, as well as the Multi Marksman Rifle, which is a 24” gunfighter that can defend itself against many gunfighting threats in Reactive, we have to call out the Killer Hacker profile. It’s a versatile hacker, not the best in the faction, but very useful for 0SWC, and of course it can benefit from a Tinbot: Firewall(-6). But the real prize is the E/Mitter. For 23pts, this offers Corregidor a really capable long ranged ARO, or a way to scare tough enemies into keeping their heads down. We expect Coyote FTOs to be a staple of Surface Division Fireteams.



Is the Bambadroid a good trooper on its own merits? It starts at 13pts, and we don’t see why you’d ever take the 16pt profile - an AP Marksman rifle is a decent weapon, but pointless on a BS11 remote, save the points and take the Thunderbolt. On reflection, we think the cheap AP Mine Dispenser profile is the best option. It will usually benefit from +1SD, and occasionally from an additional +1BS. It’s also likely to remain hunkered in your DZ, close to a Baggage bot reloading those mines. The FO profile is more relevant if you want to actually go out and do objectives, but there are a lot of other Specialists in this faction! Since Bambadroids have (Corregidor), they look like a pretty great choice to buff up a gunfighter in a Duo, watching its back and throwing out some mines as they advance, or as part of a larger team that stays back in the DZ.

Bambadroids are also 0-1 in Armoured Fireteams, so they can form a Duo with an Iguana (without any +1SD bonus). This doesn’t offer any synergy which leaps out at us, but who knows. Musterkrux: Coyotes? Stop. You had me at ‘Person wearing a Beret on top of their helmet/facemask while also rocking a Poncho’. My winner is probably either of the KHD profiles, they’re well priced as KHDs that also affect Hackable-Non-Hackers (which is the funny of saying: TAGs, HI and Remotes) while also having Warhorse and BTS 6. There’s a bit of skill bloat in there but the core package is solid enough. As far as Bambadroids are concerned,  I think the Forward Observer with a Chain Rifle (love my DTWs), D-charges and Deployable Repeaters is my winner here but they’re all eminently serviceable.

Shifta Insertion Group



This is a very useful support unit. It’s a Wildcard (up to 2 per team), with no purity tags. It brings Strategic Deployment (+4”), which is great for any teams including mission specialists and/or 24” guns. They’re also Sensors and Specialist Operatives. What makes this work particularly well is their defensive and utility kit at a reasonable price. The best profile might be the 17pt version with E/Marat and EM Minelayer. That’s a wonderful tool for stopping enemies careering into your DZ, and can also pivot into mission objectives or revealing enemy camouflage. The more expensive EM minelayer with a Thunderbolt(+1B) is slightly less optimised, but could potentially make use of Triangulated Fire. The Thunderbolt Paramedic we see less of a role for. But the most interesting profile is a 21pt Chain of Command model. That’s right, CoC in Corregidor - what a time to be alive. This keeps a good defensive loadout of light shotgun, flash pulse, and E/Marat, and adds a Discoballer. A fantastic piece of utility. 

The Shifta CoC seems like a natural choice when taking either the Territorial or Brigada Lieutenant options. While it’s more expensive, and arguably more risky, it frees Surface Division from the long established, boring method of Alguacil Lieutenants and decoys. We can foresee late games where the original Lt is dead, and the newly-promoted Shifta flings out Discoballers to enable a last-gasp Objective run. The E/M minelayer is just easy to slot into any list. I expect to include 1-2 Shiftas in most Surface Division army lists. Musterkrux: I agree, they seem like very solid Fireteam Filler. My favourite there is probably the cheapest one, as they’re still a specialist, still give your Fireteam +4 Inches of Deployment (Strategically) and a being a Minelayer means you get to contribute to the Fog of War that the rest of your CJC camo pieces will enjoy. Also, those cat-ears on the helmet scream Black Panther and I am 100% here for it: Yibambe!

Genghis: Beyond not suiting my personal aesthetic preference, I worry that cat ears on my models might give people the idea I'm one of those Nomad players. But to each their own.

Bearcats



This is a HI drop trooper; the closest existing analogue is probably the Liu Xing. Being a ‘light’ HI, in that they’re 1VITA and NWI (Shock Immune), is actually an advantage in many missions - when you assault the enemy DZ, you don’t want to risk leaving an Unconscious trooper which your opponent can score Classified Objectives from. You still get the important durability of taking 2 Wounds before you’re done. The other real benefit is that 6-2 Move with Super-Jump (Jet Propulsion). Flying 8” on every fighting Order is superb mobility. If you can bring a Bearcat in near or inside the enemy DZ, you can often take a fight with every successive Order.

As fighting pieces, these are mostly very short ranged, built to offer a good template fork within 8” using their B+1 Boarding Pistol. That is part of what keeps their cost low, and should be perfectly manageable given their deployment and mobility. Since every single profile is a Specialist, it also takes nothing away from their utility for Objectives. I personally think the Killer Hacker profiles are the most interesting. Warhorse solves some of the issues of a hackable unit operating forwards, but a Bearcat still can’t penetrate a hacking net - very common in the enemy DZ - without threat of a run-ending Carbonite ARO. Being able to blast through hackers with Trinity (and an inbuilt Firewall(-3)!) is fantastic. As with the Hellcats of old-Corregidor, these profiles are true Classified Objective machines: HI, Veteran Troops, Hackers, with D-Charges, that can easily reach the enemy DZ. Beyond the hacker advantages, this is the only type with a +1B Boarding Shotgun, which is a step above the AP SMG for purely offensive use. The SMG/Adhesive Launcher Rifle KHD seems like more of a finesse piece, for those who don’t mind being a bit less effective in all-out assault, if it gives them a chance to shoot a tough target in the back.

The Paramedic has some synergy in-faction, especially since it comes with a Deployable Repeater - a good choice for a second Bearcat. The Engineer profile doesn’t jump out at us so much, but remember that Surface Division lack Tomcats or Carlota - this might be the best insurance for a TAG-centric list. Remember that you can take a Zondbot for it, which starts on the table in Disconnected state. That telegraphs the choice, but can vastly extend its usefulness as an engineer. The Spitfire profile might seem like a compromise, but it is still a Specialist and on a tough, mobile platform - it might actually have the highest ceiling of any Bearcat, since it can operate at much higher ranges, while still applying template forks up close and completing Objectives. Any of the Bearcat profiles can also consider starting on the table, and the Spitfire might be the best at that. Flying 8” per Order out of your DZ can sometimes immediately start laying plunging fire onto enemies trying to hide Prone.

Bearcats are potentially the best all-out offensive unit for Corregidor in the box. All of those we’ve discussed above let you build utility, defend, play for Objectives, or take fights against offered AROs. Bearcats are what you use to smash through the enemy lines and get at their soft underbelly. Awesome. Musterkrux: As a known and even notorious Combat Drop Enjoyer, as soon as you hand me a drop trooper with 2-Vita or NWI I’m already in heaven. While the KHDs are my big winners here (Cybermask for the marker state, D-charges for Classifieds, and great weapon loadouts), the lure of a Combat Drop/Parachutist Deployable Repeater in a Nomad faction is real. Chef Kiss, clear winner of the Nomad releases in this cycle. No notes. 

Genghis: Yeah, I'll be honest. If you run into me at the table, and I deploy 14 models, I do have a Bearcat. If I deploy 13 models, I have 2 Bearcats. I don't even care if you know it, you can't stop it.

Vigilantes



These are Camouflage Infiltrators, which is a useful, powerful, but relatively expensive archetype. You have to pay for that kit, but ultimately they can win games on Objectives. They also have Counterintelligence, which is a nice boon to the faction, but harder to fit in than cheaper support units like Tunguska’s Puppetmaster. For 25-20pts, they are all Specialists of one stripe or another, while still being a little fighty at BS12, ARM3, and decent weapon sets. This is the jack of all trades problem. Vigilantes don't particularly shave any costs, so end up doing a few things OK, nothing very well. 

We do like the unusual combinations in their profiles. The regular Hacker being the cheapest option, with Sensor, is a lot of utility in exchange for much more limited lethal weapons. The Killer Hacker might not be as aggressive, or as Tinbot-able as some competing options in Corregidor, but it still starts in the midfield under camo and can Surprise Attack(-3) Trinity enemy hackers.

The AP minelayer with Specialist Operative offers defence and an unhackable Specialist, but the SWC can be hard to find in Corregidor. If you can spare 1pt instead, the Armed Turret (Combi Rifle) could see play. It’s not the most impressive turret in the game, but worth setting up over a midfield Objective in the late game. The FO is the cheapest unhackable profile, with the best FtF roll inside 8”, and the only long range weapon in the E/Mitter. 

Our core concern with Vigilantes is whether players will want to pay the premium over other Specialists that can join Fireteams. It’s hard to see them being taken before the significantly cheaper Moran Masai, which compete as midfield Specialists. Time will tell if these are embraced. Musterkrux: Mild disagree on that final point, I think I’ll be finding the points to upgrade the Masai I would be taking in CJC lists into Vigilantes. Though, someone needs to tell these guys that while anorak/hood might be bringing some rad drip, the loss of peripheral vision is going to get them killed one day.

Updated Unit: Alguaciles



The 3 Alguaciles included in the Mazebreaker box are all Alguacil Gatta, with (BTS=3), which is functionally not that important. The important thing is these are supplementary profiles to the existing Alguacil profiles - no word yet on whether those profiles will receive any kind of tweaks, or confirmation on whether the Gatta profiles are for Surface Div only. As 10pt models without any Specialist skills, these are simply optimised, representing marginal gains over that maligned rifle line infantry archetype. They’re better at the passive, reactive role we expect for line troopers, because they have stronger ARO weapons. 

Our concern is that this still doesn’t make them terribly attractive for list-building. Critically, there doesn’t appear to be a Lt option with these weapon loadouts (we’d have expected it to be previewed if there was). That means you can’t decoy an Alguacil (or any other) Lt with them, inclusion has to be based solely on their merits as cheap Fireteam filler. We don’t see the appeal, when Surface Div at least is confirmed to have rather flexible Fireteams, and options like the 13pt Bambadroid and 14pt Daktari are (Corregidor) as well. In terms of using your 15 slots, these don’t seem as handy as similar-cost Diablos or Jaguars. Perhaps there is some role for them in building up level 4-5 Corregidor Fireteams, but we really need to see the full Army update to judge that. It will depend on whether other Alguacil profiles, and ARO-suitable profiles like the Mobile Brigada ML or Wildcat HRL, are updated to be competitive. Musterkrux: I was preparing to put CB on blast for dropping another three Combi-Rifle Alguaciles sculpts onto CJC players but I guess they got me on a technicality. Well played, CB, well played. That said, I agree that the lack of a viable Lt in an Operations box is a mild concern.

Genghis: What do you mean? Just run the Territorial as an active Lt and 1VITA gunfighter, it will be fine! (It actually might be fine, if you took the Shifta as CoC).

Updated Unit: Bandits



Well this is unexpected! Another Bandit update. They were altered in 2025, partly to remove their Booty skill, which revealed their identity under camouflage, and arguably, if you were a certain kind of RAW-enthusiast, actually forced them out of camouflage if you rolled for it. But they gained massively in that update, becoming a competitive staple in Corregidor, because they gained Infiltration (ReRoll). This made one new profile, the Minelayer with Shock Mines and Deployable Repeaters, one of the very best in the Sectorial. It allows starting the game (75% of the time) with a substantial area of the enemy DZ already under your hacking area. This is quite oppressive in a faction which is (was?) highly incentivised to build into hackers and Guided Missile play. The hacks, and follow-up missiles, can rain down from the first Order of the game, if you’re playing first and pass that re-rollable check on a 10. It’s not bad going second either. You can risk the roll to pin your opponent down on their starting line, if you think there’s a suitable position and a lack of enemy hackers to reverse the situation. Or, you just deploy the Bandit conventionally, with either a Repeater or a Shock Mine in the midfield - it’s still a useful light assault piece and can use Camouflage to get a Repeater where it needs to be, if you’re willing to sacrifice it. 

Honestly, we were expecting a profile update to remove that potential negative play experience from the game, and perhaps give out some interesting new stuff to compensate. But no, the update here simply alters the BS Attack skill from (+1B) to (+1SD); the Bandit gets 1-2pts cheaper as a result. Now some readers may already be spluttering about +1SD being the hot new tweak given to under-performing units, this is nepotism, etc etc. While it is often a preferable skill to +1B because it works in the Reactive turn, bear in mind that Bandits are not firepower units. Bandits ideally hide under Camouflage until they place a repeater, they blast into something with a shotgun/template/melee fork, or they are forced out by Discover/Sensor. If they’re firing shots in ARO, that’s usually not ideal. It may keep them alive for an Order or two longer sometimes. It does partly close the gap between the Minelayer and other profiles, since all the others have an Adhesive Launcher Rifle, which can at least threaten tougher units in Reactive. But there’s some anti-synergy with Bangbomb(+4). Both those skills are to improve survival AROs, and you can’t use both, and neither help against templates. The most important downside of losing +1B is that the Bandits’ boarding pistol template is now only B1; this means they are seldom good bets for trading upward in template forks. 

Overall, this change is pretty minor. What’s most surprising is that it keeps, and even cheapens, the standout, notorious use of the Bandit as a hacking area projector. The Minelayer was the only competitively-relevant profile before, and that looks to still be the case. We will have to wait and see with the full Army update whether that hacking network capability in [either flavour of] Corregidor has changed at all. Musterkrux: I’m sure there’s an argument for including the other profiles now but if you start talking to me about Bandits being 1-2 points cheaper I’m going to think about Minelayer profile being 25 points, suddenly feel dizzy for a few moments and then collapse. Please call an ambulance. 

Surface Division Fireteam Structure



This is a simple set-up of Corregidor Fireteams (normal soldiers), Correctional Fireteams (convicts and guards) and separate smaller teams for Iguanas and Geckoes. There is a lot of freedom in the tags though, since (Corregidor) and (Correctional) are so widespread. 

The Wildcards list is interesting. Daktaris and Lupe retain (Corregidor). The former at least will still be helpful in Corregidor teams, but it also keeps alive the current technique of pinning Lupe and a Dak to a Gecko, to unlock the +1SD bonus (and provide specialists and vision control) for a budget price. Old favourites like the Vostok FTO, Jazz FTO and Valerya remain Wildcards but don’t contribute to the team level, and the same is true for the new Shiftas - we see all of those units as extremely valuable for teams. The Vostok is still one of the only options for a resilient, 0SWC gunfighter short of a TAG, the hackers benefit enormously from Brigada Firewalls, and Shiftas give great defense and Strategic Deployment. 

Tsyklons, as previously mentioned, don’t feature in the Corregidor rosters at all, and are similarly absent from the Fireteam chart. This might be one big omission, but we will note that being in the roster, but excluded from Fireteams, would doom them to (further) irrelevance. Much more interesting is the presence of Stempler Zonds - not FTOs - in the list as 0-2 Wildcards. This raises the hope that Surface Div can pin the Tactical Awareness Stempler profiles into Fireteams - multiple PanOceania Sectorials have shown how useful that combination can be. 

Within the teams themselves, Correctional Fireteams are now slightly more forgiving as Jaguars and Diablos team well together. But we don’t expect real changes to those profiles, so Diablos will still effectively require a Lobo in their team to work well. It does make the teams more resilient - they’ll keep a bonus longer after taking casualties - and it opens up Lobo/Jaguar Duo teams if you just want reliable vision control. 

Corregidor Fireteams are the biggest change, not just because they contain the fresh princes Territorials, Coyotes and Bambadroids, but because of the (Corregidor) tag and ability to form Duos. This makes it easier to achieve +1SD on firepower pieces, and even opens up a real possibility of a Sixth Sense ARO piece in Corregidor. We expect a lot of lists to play around with a collection of say 5-8 ‘linkable’ units in the main Combat Group, which could offer 2-3 combinations of Fireteams, decided at deployment. E.g. if you take a Territorial, 2 Coyote FTOs and a Bambadroid, it is viable to deploy them as a single, Level 4 Core, or a pair of Duos, or a Level 3 Haris, with the fourth model joining some others. This is something we tried in practice games and it was a lot of fun. 

Deep Space Division Fireteam Structure

We don’t know! The roster for Deep Space is completely unchanged, but our fairly confident guess is that their Fireteams will look extremely similar to Surface Division’s, but with EVAders in place of Coyotes/Territorials, and Gators sitting with Iguanas in the Armoured Teams. Tags like (Correctional) and (Corregidor) will almost certainly be expanded to a similar level. That should make list building much easier. 

Genghis: I would take a bet that Wildcats, which conspicuously lack (Corregidor) in Surface Div, will get it in Deep Space, and so will EVAders. I think it’s probably that Alguaciles and Brigada will also get it. That’s a much more compelling set-up than Corregidor has right now. But it won’t make a huge difference to the faction unless those units also get some tweaks to make their firepower profiles more compelling. Musterkrux: I’ve said my piece regarding the split, so I’m just going to nod and point at Genghis here.

Example Post-Mazebreaker List

We have already drawn up our first attempts (expecting they may change slightly if existing units are altered) and played some practice games, so here’s a suggestion, if you want to try a proxied game in the immediate future:

Group 1: 9 Regular, 1 TacAware
  • Mobile Brigada - Multi Rifle, Firewall(-6) - 33
  • Jazz FTO - 22 | 0.5
  • Territorial - AP Red Fury - 30 | 1.5
  • Coyote FTO - Multi Marksman Rifle - 26 
  • Coyote FTO - Killer Hacker, SMG, E/Mitter - 23
  • Bambadroid - Thunderbolt, AP Mine Dispenser - 13
  • Daktari - 14
  • Shifta - Discoballer, Chain of Command - 21
  • Salyut Zond - 8 
Group 2: 5 Regular, 1 Irregular
  • Bandit - Minelayer - 25 | 0.5
  • Moran Masai - Repeater, Combi Rifle - 17 | 0.5
  • Moran Masai - Repeater, Boarding Shotgun - 15 | 0.5
  • Vertigo Zond - 16 | 1.5
  • Transductor Zond - 7 
  • Bearcat - Killer Hacker, Boarding Shotgun (+1B) - 30 
Total: 15 models, 300pts, 5SWC.

Note: either the Brigada or Territorial can be the Lieutenant, reader’s choice.

That list features the classic Corregidor Repeater network and GML play, with the DepRep Bandit - we’re sorry, but he was in the box and we just couldn’t help ourselves. That capability is largely dependent on Jazz, but she is at least under a Tinbot Firewall(-6). The intent here is that there are 8 models, all in Group 1, which can go in Corregidor Fireteams, and 6 of them are (Corregidor). The only real default would be the Brigada Tinbot supporting Jazz and the Coyote killer hacker. Then there are multiple sensible ways to format either a 4-model Core team and two Duos, or 2 3-model teams and one Duo, or even a 5-model Core and 3-model Haris. That teamed element has 2 active firepower pieces in the Territorial and the Coyote MMR, a good ARO in the Coyote E/Mitter, and a lot of utility with the AP Mine Dispenser and Discoballer, as well as Specialists almost everywhere. In Group 2, the Bandit drops Repeaters or stage-dives a soft target, the Morans can play Objectives or set up Koala forks, and the Bearcat provides a packaged assault piece to kill enemy hackers (or anyone it can approach with its shotgun) and take a key objective.

What are the weaknesses of that list? It only has 2 offensive gunfighters, which are both 1VITA and only effective to 24”. They will depend heavily on the Discoballer Shifta, or hacking/GML plays, to avoid lockout. It’s not got that much resilience either -  only 2 models have a second wound, so it can’t brawl as well as some lists. Nonetheless, it looks fun enough to try out.

Genghis: I left McMurrough out of the list, just out of a desire for novelty. He could simply replace the Coyote hacker or Bandit.

Conclusion: What Are We Excited for in Corregidor Surface Division?

Well, this is a healthy handful of new units. Territorials, Shiftas, Coyote FTOs and Bambadroids all have clear uses in Fireteams. They’re good units and their roles seem to target some of the weaknesses, or we might say mediocrities, in Corregidor’s current roster. The Bearcats are a wonderful unit, joining McMurrough as the mobile edge of the Sectorial. Coyotes with Bambabots are a genuinely new unit dynamic in the faction which we’re excited to play with. Vigilantes might be the weakest, but they’re still not bad, they are just a jack of all trades unit that pays a relatively high points cost. Even in that case, it at least gives faction access to Counterintelligence. We particularly like the emphasis, across all this stuff, on widespread Specialists. Corregidor have often been noted for strong Objective-oriented play in the game’s history, and these profiles can sustain that, even in the absence of Hellcats, Tomcats and Carlota.

On that note, our only real negative about these additions is the restriction on combining them with certain existing units. We’ve already broken down our complaints from an organisational, presentational perspective. If, as we assume, players can select one Surface and one Deep Space list for a tournament, there’s not much impact from the inability to combine each Division’s exclusive units. If your two favourite models in the faction are the new Coyote and the EVAder Feuerbach, you might be understandably miffed, but it’s not that bad. The real negative is that Corregidor will remain quite predictable in its camouflaged and ‘off the board’ potential, when it could have been opened up. As mentioned above, when opponents see you deploy 14 models in Surface Division, they know that a 15th must be a Bearcat. If you deploy camouflage markers outside your DZ, and don’t have any Irregular Orders or Koalas telegraphing Bandits or Morans, opponents will know if it’s a Sombra or a Vigilante, depending on your Division. 

Moving on from that gripe, it is clear that Surface Division lists will play quite differently to our current Corregidor efforts, with a competitive role for almost all the new units. We expect at least a bit of uplift to Deep Space as well via Fireteam changes, so it’s a good time for all the Corregidor players.
