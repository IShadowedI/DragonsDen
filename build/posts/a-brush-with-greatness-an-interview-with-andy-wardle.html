A Brush with Greatness: An Interview with Andy Wardle

May 13th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Welcome back to A Brush with Greatness! I have something a little different (and very special) for you today. Rather than talking about my own painting, I had the opportunity to pick the brain of one of competitive painting’s brightest stars, the incredibly talented Andy Wardle!

Andy Wardle is a prolific competitive painter with an extremely impressive amount of hardware in his cabinet, including many of the coveted Golden Demon trophies and one of the highest honours that a miniature painter can receive: the Slayer Sword.

As one half of the Cult of Paint, Andy also produces a ton of high-level painting instructional content both on YouTube and Patreon. Thank you for joining me, Andy! 



What was the first model you ever painted/how did you come to miniature painting?

My very first models were Airfix and Gundam, but my first Warhammer model was a Space Marine bike. I went into Toymaster looking for a new Airfix, but I saw the Space Marine Bike and was completely fascinated by the aesthetic. It felt like a forbidden grown-up’s toy back then, and I wanted it! I miss that child-like feeling of imagination, instead of the jaded, critical eye I have now as an adult, haha!

Outside of miniature painting, which artists do you admire?

Hayao Miyazaki is the biggest inspiration for my miniatures. I also love a lot of the Magic: The Gathering Artists. I try to follow as many as I can on Instagram to fill my feed with inspirational fantasy art. This gets me more excited than miniatures.



What are your inspirations within the miniature painting sphere?

I guess I answered above, as fantasy art really helps me visualise what I want to do with my miniatures. As for miniature painters, I think colour palettes are what I admire the most from other artists. I really enjoy Marc Masclans’ work because of his taste in colour. Thinking about the balance of colour schemes is most important to me.

How has the competitive scene changed since you started competing?

I think the levels of complexity that have become “standard’ or almost expected are quite crazy now. For example, you would only see ambient reflections on the very top pieces. Now, it’s almost an auto-include, and you see it across all levels of painting. With the flow of information, people are discovering these things sooner on their painting journeys. I feel like I’ve been growing alongside the growth of the competitive scene, and it's nice to be part of it in that way. For example, I have wanted to really start making much more elaborate scenes and striking compositions, for my own fulfilment. These elements seem to be more essential in being successful now, too.



If you could change one thing about competitive painting, what would it be? 

The perception of people who don’t enter them, or really understand them. I read a lot of commentary online that isn’t true or wouldn’t work, which is quite frustrating. There are a lot of nuanced things around the painting meta/scene, which, if you aren’t heavily involved in it, you may not agree with me. For example, I have seen many people wanting all Golden Demon entries to be on gaming bases. Now I see this viewpoint, if you are a gamer, you want to relate to it more. But for me, I would hate to spend 300 or more hours on a figure, only to have to glue it to a little plastic disc. I love to present my pieces on the nicest piece of wood I can find, which complements the composition I’ve agonised over. So I get the viewpoints of other people, but I wish some of these views on competitive painting would change.

When you’re not painting Golden Demon and other comp entries, what do you work on?

I used to paint armies, but I’ve stopped that now to just do display miniatures at all times. I like to paint more relaxed pieces for our Patreon and YouTube channel. I don’t really paint anything that isn’t content or for a show, but it’s what I enjoy, so it doesn’t ever feel forced.



Getting into the nuts and bolts of your creative process, how do you decide what your next competition piece will be? What typically sparks inspiration for you? Do you see a model and think “that piece would be useful for this project”, or do you come up with a general scene first and then find models to match that scene?

It always sparks from the tropes, environments or characters I enjoy. I love pieces that have a human figure and an animal, so you’ll see many pieces from me like that. It's not that deep, really. I’ll play games like Castlevania and think “vampires are cool”. Then set about making the coolest Vampire I’m capable of. You’ll see a Vampire from me this year…

Sometimes a new release gets me excited, but more often it’s scenes I want to create in my head. Right now I’m enjoying demons and dragons, so I’ve been brainstorming various ideas for those. Eventually, after trying a few things out with rough kit bashes, etc., I start to complete projects. 

I had the idea for Glutos about a year before I actually started, but I built it and left it sat on the desk for 12 months. My “Gone with the Wind” diorama was part of a much larger diorama I started 3 years prior; I just decided to condense that idea down to something smaller and more focused.

So I guess to answer your question, I am constantly thinking of ideas, maybe trying a few out. I like to leave the ideas floating for a while before really going for it on them.



What do you look for in a model when selecting it for a composition?

I mean nothing in particular; it’s just what works for the project. But I do think long and hard about the figure selection. It's a big deal to me because every model takes so much time, I don’t want to start something unless I think it’s really great. I probably only do 2-3 figures per year, so it has to be really good in my eyes. Part of the joy, though, is modifying and making figures your own. So, so long as it’s possible to change any elements I don’t like, any figure is up for discussion.

In your recent diorama with the Jade Wizard and Spirit Wolf, you made some pretty significant changes during the process. Is that how you ordinarily work? Do you plan everything first and then stick to it, or are you more of a free-flowing improviser?

I mean that project started as a massive griffin, so absolutely free flowing, I guess. I think projects organically evolve. I think it’s more important to be prepared to completely change if a project isn’t working. For example, I stripped the paint from the wolf after it was finished. It simply felt too “safe”, and it didn’t excite me, so it probably wouldn’t excite the judges or others either. I think miniature painting can be a lot of problem-solving, and I don’t know how many problems I will come across during a project. I have clear visions in my head before I start, but sometimes they just don’t work. I think this is what takes the most time, changing and repainting things when you can’t get it right.

How do you know when a piece is finished? 

When time is up! I don’t think I’ve ever finished a model. I've just stopped painting it out of boredom, or the competition is the next day. Perhaps with the exception of Elrond. I think I could paint that figure better now, and it’s not perfect, but I think I did the best I could at the time on him. Other projects, such as Glutos and The Jade Wizard, I’m annoyed at myself for rushing them. I would add more to those pieces if I could turn back time and start earlier. My main aim moving forward is to spend longer on projects, to really let them reach their potential, and so I’m more satisfied at the end.



What do you do when you hit a point where the story you’re trying to tell with an entry is blocked by composition or technical challenges? For example, keeping a very dark scene readable or dealing with a light source that may be a complementary colour to a material.

I think if you plan for the main things to work in your scene, and prioritise them, it shouldn’t matter if it doesn’t work so well for all of the materials, for example. To be honest, I have never really come across this problem. Sometimes, there are compromises, I guess, but so long as the bigger picture works, I am happy enough. When I am building, I try to really imagine how it will be to paint, to try to avoid things like this. I love planning, not in a formal sense, but just constantly thinking about how it’s going to be. Maybe that’s why I haven’t had these issues.



The column I’m writing is about my aspiration and attempt to become a competition-level display painter. What advice would you give to an aspiring competitive painter starting their journey?

If you can get paint on the model, you are competition level. Don’t wait to start entering; the sooner you start, the sooner you’ll start enjoying yourself more. Talk to other painters there, 9 out of 10 people are going to be more than happy to give you feedback, help you or just discuss painting. It’s not as cliquey as maybe people think. Just don’t be a dick and you’ll have a great time. 

As for painting, just paint what you like, how you like. When I first started, I saw the pieces I liked the most and just worked towards that. Miniatures don’t have to do anything other than look cool, so just try to make them look as cool as you can, to your taste. If there is something you feel you can’t do, ask others. Being open to feedback and not being defensive will take you so much further. I’ve witnessed people getting excellent feedback from great painters, yet they are really defensive and don’t take it in. Surprise, surprise, these people don’t achieve what they want at competitions. I’ve also watched people soak up information like a sponge, take on feedback and grow as a painter. This is what I love to see.

Thank you so much for your time!

Andy Wardle can be found at the Cult of Paint on Youtube and at andywardle_artwork on Instagram.
