Tabletop Battles Reviews: Journal Tactica - The Battle of Tallarn Part One

May 2nd, 2026 via Goonhammer
Thanks to Games Workshop for sending us a copy of the Journal Tactica to review.

Background

Andrew: As it says on the cover, the focus of this book is the Battle of Tallarn. Known from early Heresy lore to be the largest armored battle of the entire war, and one of the largest tank battles in Imperial history as a whole, the Battle of Tallarn encompassed months of combat between Loyalist and Traitor armored hosts following the virus bombing of the formerly verdant world of Tallarn by the Iron Warriors. While the battle has existed in Warhammer 40000 lore since early editions of the game, it was first explored in detail in John French’s Tallarn series of novellas, and later at the strategic level in the Legions Imperialis supplement The Devastation of Tallarn (link to the TTB review of that book here). Battle of Tallarn Part One focuses on the early parts of the battle, and the forces and field commanders that took part in it.

Solar Auxilia Leman Russ. Credit: Andrew_N

The lore section starts with a description of the planet of Tallarn prior to the Heresy; a verdant and lush world used as a storage and supply planet for forces taking part in the Great Crusade. To help maintain the long-term viability of the planet, the Imperium built its Crusade storage and supply depots underground, something that would become vital to the survival of the planet’s people and fighting forces in the battle to come. As the Crusade progressed and the front lines moved further away from it, Tallarn moved in role from a frontline supply depot to a rest and storage facility, acting as a long term holding facility for military assets and a rest stop for Imperial Army forces too worn down to rejoin the front lines. This was the state Tallarn found itself in when the majority of the Iron Warriors fleet jumped into the middle of the system and unleashed a hellish bombardment of virus bombs on the planet, followed by an armored invasion of the now-hellish surface. Only those fortunate enough to be working in one of the existing bombardment-proofed shelters below Tallarn’s surface, or quick enough to get to one once the bombardment began, survived, swearing vengeance against the traitors and rolling out to meet the traitor armored forces with tanks of their own. 

The profile on the loyalist combatants of Tallarn starts with an explanation of the command structure and origin Reborn Cohorts of the Solar Auxilia. Composed of a mix of Great Crusade veterans that had been left behind when their crusading Auxilia cohorts and Army regiments had moved on, and native Tallarn volunteers, these large armored formations made up the majority of the loyalist forces fighting against the invasion of the Traitors. There is a small mention of loyalist Astartes forces, but in a rather surprising move for an otherwise Astartes-centric game system, the “puny mortals” take the fore in the loyalist order of battle of this book. 

Solar Auxilia Carnodon. Credit: Andrew_N

Before moving to the traitor forces section in the lore, the Journal gives readers a couple of pages of color plates of vehicles from Astartes and mortal forces on both sides, including an always-fun description of an Alpha Legion vehicle in the colors of another legion, in this case the Imperial Fists. 

The Traitor Forces profile in the Journal starts with a table of organization that would not look out of place in a military history textbook, providing a snapshot of the organization of the IVth legion’s forces in a specific region of the planet, including Legion subformations, Imperialis Auxilia support forces, and detachments from Traitor Mechanicum Taghmata, Titan Legios, and Questoris Houses. These forces make up the antagonists of the later lore deep dives - the 84th Grand Battalion. Further lore explains how First Captain Forrix of the Iron Warriors had command of all traitor ground forces on Tallarn and his reorganisation of the legion forces for the battle caused some…friction…in the legion’s ranks. This friction between Legion formations is described as having been further exacerbated by some of these formations trying to enact Forrix’s orders, while others worked to gain Perturabo’s favor by aiding in his search for the mysterious Cursus.

One of these forces, known as Scorn Two-Six, is the focus of the last part of the lore section of the book, known as the Battle of Sector Rho 18/19. Scorn-Two-Six is described as having become complacent, regarding any seemingly “dead” tank they come across as a wreck. They are rewarded for their complacency with some of those erstwhile Solar Auxilia wrecks coming alive and knocking out multiple of their vehicles, even as the human crews inside relegate themselves to slow deaths from the hostile atmosphere of Tallarn seeping through as they lie in ambush. Calling for reinforcements, the Iron Warriors fight on, finally driving back the loyalist forces, only to then find themselves being driven back by surprise reinforcement from an Iron Hands aircraft squadron that happened upon the battle. This kind of piece-meal grind of attrition of uncoordinated, disparate forces, really illustrates the haphazard and brutal nature of the Battle of Tallarn as a whole; it was simply a multitude of tank hordes slamming into each other, with the last tank running claiming victory.  

Mission Rules

The Narrative Mission in the journal allows players to play out the Ambush at Rho:19, the battle described in detail in the lore section. Like narrative missions in other journals, the mission design reminds me of missions in historical wargames, with a focus on the forces present at the battle and more asymmetric objectives. This mission has 3000 points of Solar Auxilia vehicles and walkers (with an allied detachment of up to two Astartes Xiphon Interceptors) attacking a same-sized, all vehicle force of Iron Warriors, which is split into equal sized patrol and reaction forces. The deployment map is an ambush map, with the attackers on both long sides of the table, and the defenders starting on one short edge and trying to make it to the other short edge. The victory conditions are simple: If any of the defender’s patrol force survives to turn 4, or the entire non-flyer attacker force is destroyed, the defender wins. Otherwise, the attacker claims victory. 

The attacker can also place Disguised Wrecks (A new rule) at deployment, representing their vehicles playing dead as the defenders roll by before surprise attacking them. These markers can then be activated using a new reaction, Ambush Fire, a free reaction where these erstwhile wrecks can fire at unsuspecting defender units as they move by. The defender gets a special rule, Hour of Reckoning, where their reaction force can enter the game en masse on a reserve roll of 4+, or automatically at the start of turn 4.  

This mission seems like it would be a lot of fun to play - ambush missions can be pretty swingy if not executed right, but the special rules for both the attacker and defender give some flexibility to both sides to snatch victory from the jaws of defeat if they feel on the ropes towards the end of the game. If players remove the army composition requirements (outside of perhaps the Defender splitting their force), this mission could easily be modified to accommodate any mix of armies. The Disguised Wrecks rule also screams for a special terrain modelling opportunity, and the book calls out modifying the mission to be used with other armies by mentioning that a player should have model and army-specific wrecks to use to represent their ambushers.  

White Scars Xiphon Interceptor. Credit - Soggy

Armoured Spearhead Mission Pack

We get quite an extensive new mission pack here for fighting armoured battles. If anything it’s too extensive, using far too many words talking about things we already know, like how to choose a 3000 point army, using up space that might have been used for two scenarios. So we only get two scenarios but we do get a paragraph of text, and a table, telling us how to choose between them.

There are some actual rules mixed in all of this. Firstly, you have to take more Vehicles than non-vehicle units. There’s also a new Apex Detachment containing three Armour slots, all of which are Prime. You can only pick one of two Prime Advantages for these tanks:
  • Hardened Systems lets it make Repair Tests at the start of the turn as well as the end.
  • Suspensor Web Stabilisers makes it count as Stationary for the effects of Heavy - though notably not Ordnance.
Malcador Infernus. Credit: NotThatHenryC

This is bit weird in my opinion. It doesn’t combine well with the ability of Command Tanks (which we’ll cover later) to take two Auxiliary Detachments of tanks. This Apex Detachment notably is not a Tercio, which is a problem for Auxilia. It’s better for Legionaries, who can take some Hardened Kratos that are very hard to shut down. Suspensor-equipped quad-las Predators - maybe with pintle Multimeltas - could be fun too.

We get told to set up only a fairly small amount of terrain. This makes sense for moving vehicles about the place but means the battlefield will be an extra-unfriendly place for infantry.

The Primary mission in both scenarios is to kill enemy vehicles. You can score 5 points if one of your vehicles drops an enemy one to 0hp and it doesn’t explode, 3 if it does explode, 2 if it dies to a glancing hit and just 1vp if something other than a vehicle kills it. There are bonus points for killing big vehicles and more for killing Super-Heavies or Knights.

The first mission, Clash of Iron, uses a really weird deployment map. Each player has one of the long sides of a 6x4 board. Vehicles, which can include Transports full of assault troops, get to deploy in a band only 6” deep, just 6” from the centreline. Other units deploy behind the vehicles in the normal Dawn of War set up. As well as the “kill tanks” Primary we have First Strike (5) and Giant Killer (1) for Secondaries.

This is really weird. For one thing, the deployment zone is too narrow for lots of 30k vehicles to deploy in, except perhaps sideways on. A bigger problem is just that setting up a load of tanks just 12” apart is going to result in a very brief game, won by whoever goes first and/or has the most Melta Kratos. 

You do get to put stuff in reserve, which you may be forced to do for some of the bigger units that won’t fit in your DZ. That’s a way to keep some of your army safe at first, though the downside is they’ll have less effective firepower on the move.

The other mission is Reconnaissance in Force, which looks a bit more fun. You get First Strike (1) and Last Man Standing (9) - for which only Vehicle units are counted. Here we play with the long table edge towards players again. We have 18” deep Deployment Zones but can only put Vehicles up to 12” from each side of the board. So Tanks go in the corners and other stuff in the middle.

This is a bit more like a straight up tank battle, which is fine. Better than the first mission, anyway.

If I’m honest, I don’t think 30k is actually the best “engine” to use for tank battles. Switching to all tanks strips away things like the entire Assault Phase and also removes all Objectives, as Tanks can’t do them. An IGOUGO system with lots of shooty units and not much terrain sounds like a problem. But that's entirely a matter of my opinion and, if tank battles are what you want, here you go.

Kratos Heavy Assault Tank. Credit: Rockfish

Toxic Environment

This is a horrible place that has lethal amounts of poison and/or radiation. You can choose to designate some Difficult Terrain as Toxic. Any unit that enters, leaves or moves through Toxic Terrain is subject to the really horrible Necrotic Annihilation rule. Interestingly it doesn’t trigger if you stand still so you can potentially set up a shooty unit in a radioactive scrap pile and they won’t have any problems unless they move.

To resolve Necrotic Annihilation your unit takes a Toughness test. Pass and the unit gets Stunned, which isn’t all that bad as they may well recover in the end phase. Fail the test though and every model in the unit takes a S4 AP2 D1 hit.

This means that, as you’d expect, tougher units are much less bothered by Toxic Terrain.

Cohorts Vagus

The Cohorts Vagus are random robots that have no human oversight for some reason. They wander about and occasionally turn up in battles to help one side or other and now you can include them in your force.

Vorax Battle-Automata Maniple. Credit - Lyle

The way this works is that you get a single, potentially massive Additional Detachment. It’s not an Allied Detachment so doesn’t count towards your 50% limit on allies. In a way they aren’t part of your army at all, just a bunch of robots that have unexpectedly turned up to help.

The detachment is potentially huge. There are three slots each for both types of Castellax and Vorax, plus a slot each for Thanatars, Vulturax and Domitars. In theory you could bring 12 maniples of robots if you wanted, though at that point maybe you’d be better off just admitting that you were a Mechanicum player.

Every slot in the Detachment is Prime, though the only advantages you’re allowed to give them are specific to Cohorts Vagus. There won’t be any paragons of metal here. There are four advantages to pick from and all your units have to pick the same one, so in effect it’s like picking a Legion Tactica for your whole robot Detachment, not Prime Advantages for individual units. 

All four options grant Expendable (1) and Cortex Corruption - a new rule saying nobody can join these units and they can’t be targeted by friendly Cybertheurgic Rites. They each have the following additional special rules:

Battlefield Orphans

These are robots that survived when their squishy human friends didn’t. They get +1 WS and BS to a maximum of 4 but -1T. They always begin the game in reserve and at the end of the game they’re removed as casualties, to represent them moving on in search of more enemies. 

This is a solid option for units like Castellax that start with WS/BS3. Having some experienced but damaged robots turn up over the course of the battle will be narratively very cool and definitely useful.

Castellax. Credit - Soggy

Misplaced Reinforcements

Having been delivered to the wrong address and/or to people who were already dead, these robots start the game with their factory settings of WS/BS2 but add +1 at the start of each of their controller’s turns, to a cap of 4. They also all have Infiltrate (9) and must deploy within 12” of the enemy or else each suffer an automatic wound.

This is a bit of a gamble. If you go first you’ll be able to unleash a load of walking metal on the enemy. This will be great with things like flamer Castellax or Domitars letting off templates that don’t care about BS. But if you go second your poor robots will be stranded in midfield with WS2 during the enemy turn. Make sure you don’t set up near things like Terminators and Dreadnoughts if going second.

Corrupted Programming

These are robots that have seen too much and gone a bit wrong. They have Fear (1), explode on a 4+ and suffer Cybertheurgic Feedback in both their own charge phase and their opponent’s if they haven’t declared a charge and aren’t locked in combat.

This is a tricky one as it’ll be hard to avoid feedback in the first couple of turns. The best approach might be to take numerous small units as that way you probably won’t lose anyone to feedback. A Thanatar would probably be happy to stand back and ignore being forced to make a 2+ save now and then, though in that case it wouldn’t really benefit from these rules.

Malign Influence

Someone has mucked about with the programming of these robots. They hate everything apart from other Automata. They’re allowed to shoot at friendly units and if they don’t shoot or fight anything at some point they take Cybernetic Feedback at the end of the turn.

This is weirdly fine and maybe the least impactful option. Robots have decent range so you can probably find an enemy to shoot at or, failing that, maybe a tank of your own to harmlessly shoot with small arms fire. Most of the time you’ll be able to deploy your robots normally and use them however you like.

Should You Take a Cohort Vagus?

Sure, if you want. There are already loads of ways to bring robots for most factions, either through allies or things like the Auxilia Iron Cohort. This is another. You have an upside that you don’t have to pay the “tax” of bringing Mechanicum commanders for your robots, set against the fact that those commanders are actually really useful. 

Cohorts Vagus won’t get to use Cybertheurgy at all, which is a disadvantage compared to an Allied Detachment. That said, it’s a lot of words that you won’t have to read and understand if you want to keep things simple. There are other advantages that robots from the Cybernetica sub-faction get, like help with Reactions and scoring, which you’d probably miss more when running a Cohort Vagus.

Mechanicum Thanatar. Credit - Soggy

Cohorts Vagus have great narrative potential. I think they’d pair up very nicely with Shattered Legion or Blackshield forces, for example. I like the idea of a Detachment of Loyalist Battlefield Orphan (or maybe Corrupted Programming) robots that had killed their former masters when they turned traitor, for example. The Detachment of Misplaced Reinforcements that arrived years after the battle they were intended for is another fun option. Maybe you could have a Detachment of Malign Influence automata and also the Infocyte Assassin who had hacked their programming and made them switch sides.

Command Tanks

We get command “tanks” for both the Auxilia and Legions. They’re really mobile command stations for senior officers rather than true tanks. They fill High Command slots and both have the relevant trait to give you an extra Reaction. They unlock Apex Detachments if you want but can instead be used to access lots more Armour.

Dracosan Command Tank

Solar Auxilia Dracosan. Credit: andrew_n

This is a quite interesting new High Command option for the Auxilia. It’s a 200 point Dracosan with BS4, 9HP and a flare shield but no transport capacity at all. Instead it has a few special rules to help your army.

The Command Vehicle (9) rule lets one of your other units take a Leadership or Willpower test on a 9 instead of their own stat, so long as it’s got a Nuncio-vox, Command Vox or Vox Interlock. That’s pretty good for units like the Rapiers, with LD6.

Tank Commander (2) lets you pick two Auxiliary slots for it instead of one. You can only fill slots in these detachments with Armour. You’re allowed to take Armoured Tercios for Solar Auxilia and two of those might well provide all the Armour you need.

The Command Dracosan’s other abilities include Cohort Supreme Command, granting an extra Reaction. It also has the Armoured Warfare rule that Command Leman Russes get, granting Auto-repair to nearby Armoured Tercio units. It has Auto-repair 4+ itself. You can stick the usual upgrades like a Demolisher, pintle weapon and two HKs on it. The HKs are quite nice with BS4.

This is all good news for an Auxilia Legate. Instead of standing in the mud waiting for a super-human lunatic to come and chop him up, he’s relatively safe inside a tank, far away from harm. The only thing you’re missing out on is a Command Vox, but any of your Command slots can bring one of those.

Spartan Prometheus Command Tank

The Command Dracosan has quad heavy bolter sponsons that it can’t swap for lasers, which isn’t great, and an Augury Scanner instead of its hull guns. You'll have to convert something for this, though you can probably magnetise it so you can swap back to a normal Spartan at other times. It’s got an impressive 12HP. However it has a capacity of just 8 and its Command Transport rule means it can only transport a unit that includes somebody with the Command rule.

Like the Dracosan, the Spartan has Command Vehicle (9), though that won’t make as much difference, since Legionaries have better morale than Auxilia. It has Tank Commander (2), letting you bring a couple of Armoured Support Detachments. It’s still an Assault Vehicle and gets Auto-repair 4+.

Unfortunately this is rubbish. 420 points is far too many to carry 8 models. You do get BS5 but that doesn’t at all make up for paying this much for a brick with the firepower of a couple of 40 point Rapiers. I suppose it’s a way of getting your extra Reaction without much danger of giving up Price of Failure, as nobody will bother trying to kill the thing until all your actual threats are gone.

Tank Commander feels a lot less valuable in a Legion force than it is for Auxilia. Legion forces tend to want an Apex Detachment and don’t especially struggle to unlock enough Armour slots. This would certainly be useful for tank battles, except that the Spartan itself is a dead weight in a tank battle, where those Heavy bolters will be useless.

Legionaries could probably do with a real tank in a Command slot, as they have in Legions Imperialis. The only rationale I can see for giving us the Command Spartan is that the new kit has come out and they want to give us a reason to buy one. They haven’t succeeded in doing that, except perhaps for rule of cool reasons. Have your Praetor get out of his tank and punch things, like he’s supposed to.

Revised Solar Auxilia Units

Rapier Section

Solar Auxilia Quad-Laser Rapier Battery. Credit: Andrew_N

This is an unchanged unit, except that now we get the option of upgrading to Mole Mortars. We were a bit confused that they’ve removed the reference to quad launchers having both blast and shatter shells, but those are the standard option for the gun so nothing seems to have changed.

Mole Mortar. Credit: Jellymuppet

Mole Mortars are pretty good. They are Barrage weapons without Blast, which we haven’t seen before. Essentially they simply ignore Line of Sight, which is great. They have 36” range, fire 2 S7 AP3 D2 shots and have many special rules: Heavy (D), Armourbane, Barrage (0) and Pinning (1), plus a new rule: Subterranean. This rule alone has four bullet points saying you can’t use cover saves or damage mitigation against it, it always hits vehicles on their side armour, it can’t target flyers and it can’t kill anyone on an upper floor.

Mole Mortars are a significant addition to the Solar Auxilia arsenal. They’re a threat to all infantry, except perhaps 2+ saves, and to lighter vehicles like Rhinos. 36” range with no need to see their targets makes for a potentially quite oppressive unit, especially if someone takes multiple units of one to cause loads of pinning tests. 

Charonite Ogryns the May GHO 2025

Charonite Ogryn Section

As with the Rapiers, the main rules for this unit are unchanged but now we have different weapon options. Now, Charonite Ogryns come with two Charonite claws, which have the same profile as before. They can replace one or both of their claws with three other options for a few points, giving them an option of which weapon to use. 

I think you’ll always want to keep at least one claw because they’re good. S7 AP3 D2, Breaching 5+ is a no-nonsense powerful weapon, scary with 5 attacks on a charge. It’s a high bar for the other options to replace.

Charonite bades are worth considering, as a 5 point upgrade. They’re only S5 AP3 D1 with Breaching 6+ but they also have Reaping Blow (2). Getting up to 7 attacks for a charging Ogryn would be great but unfortunately these guys are Bulky (4), meaning a full unit counts as 12 models. Not many enemy units are going to count as outnumbering this, so Reaping Blow probably won’t trigger often.

Charonite Crushers look much more useful, in the right situation, though they’re 10 points pre model. You lose a point of Initiative and 2 attacks, ending up with A2 or A3 on the charge. In exchange you get S10, AP2, D2, Armourbane and Armour-breaker (4+) - letting you roll to do an extra point of damage to vehicles on a 4+.

Charonite Shredders are a bit of an odd one. Base initiative and attacks, S6 AP4 D2 with Rending 4+ and Shred 4+. They’d be very good against something with multiple wounds, low toughness and a 4+ save but I’m not sure such a unit really exists in 30k - or at least not commonly enough to invest in these when the standard claws will do the job perfectly well. 

Personally I’d either just stick to claws or mix in a few crushers. Unfortunately the kit doesn’t want you to do that and only gives you one claw per model. Maybe give blades to some of them or just count blades/shredders as claws. Nobody is going to mind, but on the other hand you’re also not going to lose many games because you spent 5 or 10 points on blades that end up not mattering.

There are four Charonite Ogryns in the new box, though initially only three in the unit. Adding a fourth model is worth it if you have the points available but three is fine too if you don't. These guys are going to struggle to cross the board so they're probably best used to counter-charge and take out other people's elites as they stage towards your lines.

Final Thoughts

We like this journal. It’s got a good amount of meaningful content for several factions and some different missions that everyone can play, to make a change from the core book ones.

In terms of content it’s definitely Auxilia players who gain the most, with useful new models. Plastic rapiers make the army somewhat more affordable too, as they’re a staple unit that was previously only available in resin. Giving Charonites a tool to break tanks may also mean they have more utility. 

Legion players can probably skip this book unless they want the scenarios. The command Spartan is not a good unit unfortunately, which is a shame as it’s a cool conversion opportunity. There’s also not much here for Mechanicum players, except for a way for other people to use your robots.

We’ve seen several of these Journals now and the format seems quite successful. It’s a big change from the huge black books of 1st edition but it seems to work on its own terms. We look forward to seeing what comes next.
