Heroes of the Black Library: Maleneth Witchblade

May 9th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Across the grim darkness of Warhammer 40,000, the Mortal Realms of Age of Sigmar, and the battlefields of The Old World, a ceaseless conflict rages between steadfast champions of order, terrifying architects of ruin, and everyone caught in between. Heroes of the Black Library is a deep dive into the incredible characters that make these universes so rich, from the tabletop to the bookshelf and beyond. Join us as we explore their bloody, larger-than-life tales, proving that in an eternity of carnage where there is no peace, the line between an inspiring hero and a monstrous villain is almost always forged in the fires of war. Happy reading!

Hello and welcome, folks, to the next installment of our brand-new series: Heroes of the Black Library! Each will feature one of the heroes (or villains!) that have been brought to life in the pages of the Black Library, and today we'll be looking at Maleneth Witchblade, Daughter of Khaine, agent of the Order of Azyr, and (reluctant) companion of the legendary Gotrek Gurnisson.

Maleneth's trajectory from companion to a hero carrying a book in her own right is a fascinating one, as it bridges the gap in evolving strategies for both Games Workshop's fantasy property as well as the Black Library's audio format itself.

And it all begins right here, with a little audio drama we call...

Realmslayer (2018)

Quick history lesson for those who have only come to the hobby in the last decade. Back in 2014, Games Workshop decided it was going to nuke the Warhammer Fantasy Battles game setting that had long defined its fantasy footprint and replace it with something new. Something modern. Something... called Age of Sigmar.

This isn't the place to dive into it, but if you're curious as to what happened don't worry, we've got you covered. Suffice it to say that the world that would become known as "The Old World" was going away, and a completely new one introduced in its place.

At the same time, the sun was beginning to set for the once-popular audio drama format. You wouldn't have known it then, with amazing talent like Brian Blessed and Catherine Tate lending their voices to Warhammer stories, but before long the high production costs relative to audiobook narration- and the advent of Warhammer+ and it's focus on visual storytelling- would usher in the end of an era.

Into this time of great transition was born Maleneth Witchblade. An assassin of Khaine that had joined the Order of Azyr (sort of like the Inquisition in 40K, but for the Cities of Sigmar), she was created to be one of Gotrek Gurnisson's replacement companion as he adventured through the Mortal Realms.

"The original intention back when we were filling out the cast of Realmslayer," noted David Guymer in an interview, "was that Gotrek could, Dr. Who style, have many different companions that he could alternate between depending on the story and who was writing it."

Whereas Gotrek's classic companion, Felix Jaeger, was an amiable sidekick, Maleneth was envisioned for as a bickering foil. He a warm-hearted poet, she a cold killer. The chemistry between the two came alive in the recording studio, with Blessed's Gotrek and Penelope Rawlins' Maleneth playing off one another splendidly. Rawlins, in fact, had been Guymer's choice for the role after he'd heard her in the 40K audio drama Titan's Bane.

Realmslayer was very well received, helping many Old World veterans make the jump into the Age of Sigmar setting. The Realmslayer audio drama would eventually be adapted into a novel with Realmslayer: Legend of the Doomseeker, and its popularity also secured Maleneth's place alongside Gotrek- at least for awhile.

Image credit: Games Workshop

The Bone Desert (2018)

Released as part of the Black Library's Novella series, The Bone Desert marked Maleneth's print-story debut. This isn't just a cosmetic difference; being a character in a prose novel rather than just voices of dialogue in a script allowed author Robbie MacNiven to really develop the character and spend more time 'getting inside her head.'

Part of how he accomplishes this is focusing on the cool competence of Maleneth when it comes to strategy and battle. Gotrek's always been a battle-loving hothead, it's part of his indelible charm. The humor with Felix was that he'd invariably be dragged along for the ride when he'd much rather be doing almost anything else, ever the noble but reluctant hero.

Maleneth is a far more proactive combatant than Felix ever was, and The Bone Desert brings the fun tension up from the classic "push and pull" of the original duo into a "yoke with two horses" model as Gotrek and Maleneth butt heads over the most effective course of action.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Ghoulslayer (2019)

With the next Gotrek and Maleneth novel, author Darius Hinks enters the scene. "The idea with different authors writing Gotrek," he's noted, "is that people can either enjoy the new stories as standalones or buy them all and follow a larger narrative... It’ll be really interesting to read all the different takes on his character and I know the BL editors will ensure the disparate threads pull together to make a story that grows with each book."

And with Ghoulslayer, Maleneth had come into her own. Realmslayer had introduced her, using the device of being a spy for the Order of Azyr to bring her and Gotrek together. The Bone Desert had expanded on the audio drama, establishing her as a point-of-view character in the greater space prose afforded. And now with Hinks at the helm, she had more space to stand on her own and move past sidekick status into a character that potentially could stand on her own in the Black Library.

Ghoulslayer sees the duo arrive in Shyish, the realm of the dead, hunting for Nagash, God of Undeath in the underworlds.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Gitslayer (2020)

Gotrek had always been a character of negative space from the very first time William King wrote him, largely viewed through the lens of his companion in much the same way as Watson's role to Sherlock Holmes. The challenge, then, is to make that companion character compelling on their own, and here Hinks takes the opportunity to send them to Chamon as Gotrek seeks to remove the Master Rune from his chest (a longstanding objective of his). For Maleneth in particular, this marks a turning point in her character arc.

To this point, Maleneth and Gotrek have had something of an adversarial relationship, even if Gotrek wasn't entirely aware of it. As a spy and assassin in service of the Order of Azyr, her mission has been to keep tabs on Gotrek with an eye to recovering the Rune from him once he died- and even at points considering hastening the event along herself, such as in this passage that evocatively shows just howe little regard she holds him in:

Maleneth ignored the voice, creeping closer to the Slayer, wincing at his stench. He was grotesque. A graceless lump of scarred muscle, bristling with porcine hair and covered in knotted tattoos. Even by the low standards of the duardin race he was primitive – like a hog that had learned to stand and carry an axe. He was shorter than the brigands he had carved his bed from, but twice their width and built like a barn. The stale, sweet smell of beer shrouded the bodies, mixing with Gotrek’s belches and stinging Maleneth’s eyes. She could see the dregs glistening in his matted beard as she leant closer, keeping her eyes fixed on the rune. The rune stared back.

 

Despite her loathing she hesitated, knives trembling, inches from his body. (from Ghoulslayer, by Darius Hinks)

In Gitslayer, that worm finally turns, and we see this clearly when Maleneth secretly sabotages the Kharadron machine that was going to extract the Master Rune, potentially killing Gotrek in the process. The assassin successfully pulls off her treachery, but is gripped by a sudden change of heart. "After all her thoughts of murdering him," writes Hinks, "she now felt a chill at the thought that she might have succeeded."

When he survives the resulting explosion, she's relieved- and begins to face the fact that she's come to feel a fondness for Gotrek over the course of their adventures.

‘Damn you!’ screamed Maleneth as she felt the dreadful bubbling sensation moving under her skin, spreading further up her arm. ‘Without my help you will never get the Slayer. Only I know how he thinks. You’re making a mistake. He won’t follow me. I betrayed him!’ The word ‘betrayed’ caused Maleneth to halt. Why did it sit so awkwardly in her mouth? What could be wrong with betraying Gotrek? He was a boorish, arrogant hog and he cared nothing for her or the Sigmarite cause. So why did she feel a rush of panic at the thought that she had betrayed him? (from Gitslayer, by Darius Hinks)

When later in the book we see both heroes consumed by a rapidly-spreading fungus and Maleneth using her one cure on him instead of herself, it's not a deviation of what's come before. It's the fulfillment of it.

If there's a common knock on the Gotrek & Felix dynamic in the earlier stories, it's that over time it seemed to devolve into a familiar pattern. Gotrek chases his doom, Felix the reluctant hero is dragged along, they prevail, and Felix laments his life choices. Next book, new doom.

It's certainly reductive, but Maleneth sidesteps the concern entirely. She does grow and develop across their adventures, and we'll see the ultimate expression of this in the next book- their final one together.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Soulslayer (2022)

The "season finale" of Hinks' turn at the wheel, Soulslayer concluded his trilogy in a big way. As noted earlier, Maleneth is a member of the Order of Azyr, tasked with spying on Gotrek and recovering the Rune. Of course, as the stories have unfolded that mission has been compromised somewhat by her growing fondness for the dwarf. When the Order dispatches a Witch Hunter to demand Maleneth fulfil her duty to eliminate Gotrek, Maleneth chooses instead to dispatch the emissary.

The arc comes to its climax with heartbreak, as- spoiler alert- Maleneth ultimately sacrifices herself to save Gotrek and their Fyreslayer allies from an overwhelming force of Idoneth Deepkin.

Just before the Idoneth reached her, Maleneth looked up and saw Gotrek. The storm had almost taken him. He was breaking apart, his body fragmenting in the wind, but she could still see how furious he was. He was pointing at her, howling and cursing. He was outraged that she had robbed him of his chance to reach Dìolan, but there was also another emotion in his face. Behind the rage there was something even more powerful, a terrible sorrow.

 

She realised that, rather than trying to reach Dìolan, he was now trying to reach her, trying to save her.

 

Then he was gone. The storm vanished, leaving no trace of the Slayer.

 

Maleneth turned to face the army, baring her teeth in a brutal smile, her pulse quickening in anticipation of the coming bloodshed. Arrows thudded into her, puncturing muscles and shattering bones, but her bloodlust was so great she barely noticed. As the front lines reached her she leapt forwards to meet them, stabbing and lunging, howling Gotrek’s name as Idoneth blades sliced into her body, spilling her blood into the cold, unnatural air. (from Soulslayer, by Darius Hinks)

In discussing his return to the franchise, David Guymer noted, "I honestly thought my time with Gotrek was done, but then I wrote the novelisation for Realmslayer and just fell in love all over again. It reminded me of the unexplored ideas I’d had for Gotrek and Maleneth after Blood of the Old World, and found myself inspired with all new ones while reading Gitslayer and Soulslayer (and cried along with every other reader at the end of the latter)."

But those tears didn't fall forever...

Image credit: Games Workshop

Shade of Khaine (2025)

By this point, Maleneth has had a terrific history in the Black Library, and author Evan Dicken, writing in the afterword of the Special Edition talked about being "terrified" to follow in its footsteps. As he noted in an interview on Warhammer Community:

We have followed Maleneth through a bunch of novels and short stories, so there’s not only a lot of history to contend with, but the fact that readers (myself included) have a big investment in the character. I wanted to put my own spin on everyone’s favorite Khainite assassin; but I also wanted Maleneth to be recognizable to fans of the Slayer novels.

Gotrek had a part to play in Dicken's story, but never actually appears in the story. Writing in our review, I noted that "Dicken really sticks the landing here, finding just the right amount of Gotrek that lets him loom large as a figure of great significance to Maleneth without overstaying his welcome. He feels like an old friend, woven tightly within the tapestry of her life and given to fond remembrance."

Maleneth's amnesia (stemming from her aforementioned brush with death at the hands of the Deepkin) allowed Dicken to pare down the character and focus on the tale he wanted to tell, while simultaneously making this a remarkably new-reader-friendly entry in the canon.

Ultimately what Shade of Khaine showed, though, was how far the character had come. Originally conceived as just one of a rotating 'bench' of companions for Gotrek, she'd grown and matured into a main character able to stand all on her own.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Other Appearances

Maleneth has shown up in a number of short stories over the years. A trio of them by Darius Hinks, The Neverspike, Death on the Road to Svardheim, and The Crown of Karak-Khazhar appear in the recent Gotrek & Maleneth Omnibus alongside all three Hinks novels as well as The Bone Desert. It also includes The Dead Hours, a 2020 short story by Guymer.

We also saw her somewhat recently in last year's Black Library Celebration with A Point of Pride, part of the commemorative short story subscription.

Ultimately, if you're looking to discover Maleneth Witchblade you are well-served by starting with Realmslayer, diving into the omnibus, and topping it all off with Shade of Khaine.

Image credit: Games Workshop

From Paper to Plastic

While our previous featured character, Minka Lesk, has been incarnated in plastic twice, this far we only have one miniature of Maleneth. Image credit: Games Workshop

Shelf Candy

Those looking to decorate their collections with Maleneth have a few options to choose from, though given how tightly connected she is to Gotrek Gurnisson some blending of the theme will be inevitable.

For her on her own, of course, we have the Shade of Khaine Special Edition with its terrific purple cover with gold foiling.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Then there's an absolute gem of a box set from 2022, the Gotrek Gurnisson Collection.

Yes, it's Gotrek-themed, but it contains the Hinks trilogy (Ghouslayer, Gitslayer, and Soulslayer) that form the main Gotrek/Maleneth story arc.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Image credit: Games Workshop

For a single-volume decorative piece, don't overlook the Realmslayer: Legend of the Doomseeker Limited Edition from 2023. I love the cloth/leatherette mix on the cover, and the color is fairly consistent with the box set so they'll pair together wonderfully.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Finally, the hispter's choice award goes to the Realmslayer Script Book Limited Edition.

Image credit: Games Workshop

If we're treating Realmslayer like a comic-book hero and placing a high value on her "first appearance," you could hardly do worse than celebrating her debut with the Realmslayer script. But this thing is much, much more than just a script book as it includes:
  • Behind-the-scenes photos of the recording
  • A one-page introduction by Gotrek voice actor Brian Blessed as well as a full intro from David Guymer
  • The Saga of Gotrek Gurnisson, a history of Gotrek & Felix by Nick Kyme
  • Annotated script with notes from the story's author, editor, and producer
Sure it's heavy on the Gotrek focus, but it remains a milestone for Maleneth as well.

Moving from the paper to the metal, back in 2020 Games Workshop offered a Daughters of Khaine collectible coin for in-store purchases. I'm always a fan of these as they're fairly easy to obtain at a relatively low cost compared to other, premium collectibles- and they look terrific in a coin holder alongside your books.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Unfortunately, there's not a lot of merchandise out there for one of the more marginal factions and characters, but if you also appreciate decorating the walls of your study, Warhammer Art has a special edition of Ghoulslayer's cover art still available (only 247 copies left at time of writing). It's shown here as framed, but you can also get it flat or in canvas.

Image credit: Warhammer Art
