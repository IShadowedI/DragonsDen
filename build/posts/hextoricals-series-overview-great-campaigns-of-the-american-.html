Hextoricals Series Overview: Great Campaigns of the American Civil War

May 11th, 2026 via Goonhammer
This is the first in a series of articles that will explore several hex and counter/board wargame systems, starting with some of my favourites. They will attempt to both be intros as well as general reviews of the systems in question.

What Is "Great Campaigns of the American Civil War?"

Great Campaigns of the American Civil War (GCACW) is a series of operational-level games set within the war that split the United States of America apart during the 19th century. The series itself has a long and storied history, starting with Lee vs Grant: The Wilderness Campaign of 1864 in 1988; although this game is not technically part of the series, it was made by the same designer (Joseph M. Balkoski) and shares many of the same concepts and rules that were later popularised within the series, with some die-hard fans arguing that it’s in some ways superior to the latter offerings of GCACW.

The first game in the series proper was Stonewall Jackson’s Way, released in 1992 by one of the most famous wargame publishers at the time, the Avalon Hill Game Company. Rather than just modelling the battle of Second Bull Run, the game attempted to model the entire campaign, which meant that depending on the individual decisions of the two opposing generals, the battle itself might not even be fought in the same location as the historical outcome. From there, further games were released to model additional campaigns, including the Antietam, Gettysburg, Shenandoah, Chancellorsville and Peninsular campaigns.

Unfortunately, in 1998 the Avalon Hill company was bought by Hasbro Games, who laid off most of the staff; although there was another game in the pipeline, the future of the series was uncertain. Thankfully, Multi-Man Publishing (MMP) managed to license the rights of the series from Hasbro and published Grant Takes Command in 2001, a game focusing on the Overland Campaign. From there, MMP continued to publish games and finally released some much-needed western theater offerings along with remakes of the older Avalon Hill games, usually bundling two games together in a single box. The latest game in the series, Thunder on the Mississippi, modelling the siege of Vicksburg, was released in 2024, and there are plans to release further games in the series in the future, with rumours pointing at a remake of Stonewall in the Valley being the next game in line.

The Confederates retreat from Centreville during the 1st Bull Run campaign. Credit: Riccardo Fabris

Game Rules

As previously mentioned, GCACW is an operational-level game with unit counters mostly representing divisions, although they can go as small as regiments. The map scale is also about 1 mile per hex, and is consistent across all games in the series. As such, battles which are the entire focus of other games are usually resolved in a couple of dicerolls, and the game aims to cover not just the main engagements but also the smaller skirmishes and advances that characterised each respective campaign. Scenarios in a game are divided into basic scenarios, which represent pivotal moments or battles of the campaign, and advanced scenarios, which feature more complex rules for logistics and supply and usually focus on the entire campaign.

The way the series achieves its aims is through an extremely interesting and dynamic rule system that has one major ethos: uncertainty. The game adds this ethos at every possible step: you can never know when a random event like rain or command paralysis will scupper your plans, or if your units will even be able to react and block an opponent threatening a flank march, or even if you do manage to activate them, if they will move fast enough to get there in time. Even when your troops do manage to get into position, they might fail to attack or only attack piecemeal. Every step of the way, the rules will seem to be against you, until the moment when fortune smiles on you and your enemies are sent skedaddling away from your victorious forces.

The Union crosses Bull Run and attempts to flank the entrenched Confederates. Credit: Riccardo Fabris

The core of the system is the action cycle: each turn (which represents a single day of the campaign), the players will roll for initiative, and whoever wins the roll will get to activate a single unit; once that activation is over, initiative is rolled again until both players pass, ending the action cycle. Each unit is ordinarily activated to march but unlike other systems, there is no fixed movement allowance for each individual unit. Instead, you have to roll a die and whatever you roll is how far the unit can move, although this can be modified through leaders or force marching your troops, albeit at a cost. Yes, you saw that right; this series uses roll-and-move as a base system within the rules. Units can be activated like this several times, but will build up fatigue levels up to a maximum of four, and might become disorganised or even lose manpower if pushed too far.

Attacks can be done in one of two ways: either on the march through the expenditure of movement allowance, or by activating a leader and calling an assault. The latter is the only way to bring more than one unit in an attack, but is potentially dangerous since you need to roll a leader check, and this might prevent the attack from unfolding altogether, or only send a fraction of the units earmarked for the attack, something that can lead to catastrophe. Combat itself has a host of modifiers for terrain, flanks and artillery support, and uses a combat result table which discourages large attacks unless you have an overwhelming advantage.

At first glance, the above system seems crazy, since it feels so random and there’s so much potential for low rolls to screw you over, but in practice it works incredibly well and leads to riveting stories of daring flanks, last minute maneuvers and desperate charges.

Strengths

I’ve always felt that games that represent a single battle or engagement can be a little limited in scope. Unless they attempt to simulate the experience of the battle rather than being historically accurate (something wonderfully espoused by Guns of Gettysburg by Rachel Simmons), the focus is usually on portraying the key moments of the battle; the results can be different, but the starting set up is largely the same.

What I like about GCACW is that due to the scale of its maps, it gives you a very large open canvas that you can explore however you want. One of the very first games I ever played was Here Come the Rebels, which models the Antietam Campaign. My Confederate opponent decided that he would set up defensive positions on the Monocacy River and entrench the city of Frederick. My Union forces eventually managed to push through after some inconclusive battles, and chased the Rebels past South Mountain, at which point the game was over. There was never an engagement anywhere near Antietam Creek or Sharpsburg, and both me and my opponent got to write, through our shared decisions, our own slice of alternative history.

The starting set up for Roads to Gettysburg: the battle could happen anywhere! Credit: Riccardo Fabris

It’s this potential that really elevates this game. As well as that, the sheer uncertainty that is present within the game really allows the players to immerse themselves in their roles, and truly add an incredible level of historicity to the game. Delays, unexpected attacks, lost orders and the constant fog of war, all elements which plagued the ability of Civil War generals to enact their plans, are all deftly and elegantly modelled by the initiative system.

Finally, the games are surprisingly playable considering its size and scope. The maps are quite large, but unit density is quite low, especially in comparison to operational level World War II games. Most of the game is spent moving units across the board, which doesn’t require fastidious checking of the minutiae of the rules. The initiative system also means that, unlike I-GO-U-GO systems, you won’t be waiting for ages for a chance to act, unless the dice really hate you. The snappy cadence of the game means that you will always be on the edge of your seat, desperately waiting for the chance to react and save your army from defeat.

Weaknesses

Although it’s a cliche at this point, one of the major strengths of GCACW is also one of its major weaknesses. Randomness will certainly rub some people the wrong way, and sometimes victory will just feel out of your hands through no real fault of your own. You will get into situations where your opponent gets activation after activation, and the only thing you can do is sit and wait while your army is methodically dismantled in front of you, with no possibility for you to react. If you are easily annoyed by swingy dice roll systems, I would recommend staying away from GCACW.

Although the game is easily played by only two players, the advanced scenarios do require a long time to play so you will need to keep the game set up for more than one day or even multiple weekends if you wish to see a campaign go to completion. Although the basic scenarios are much shorter, they also tend to be far swingier, with single battle results largely deciding the entire scenario. I am not a big fan of the basic scenarios for this very reason; the strength of the game relies on the big advanced campaign scenarios, where each individual dice roll has far lower impact.

Another weakness of the game is that each individual unit in a game has a whole host of potential status effects that can be applied to it, ranging from the different levels of fatigue, to being disorganised, out of supply, exhausted or even demoralised. These are represented by either extra counters or flipping the unit or manpower markers and are constantly changing throughout the game, which can be extremely fiddly with tightly-stacked units. The games do have vassal modules, some of which feature all-in-one counters that make it far easier to assess the status of a unit at a glance, however.

The Battle of Catoctin Mountain, which replaced the Battle of Gettysburg. Credit: Riccardo Fabris

The series also tends to be a table hog: most of the games require two maps at least and therefore will need a large empty table in order to play. None of the maps are mounted either, which means you will have to contend with annoying bumps or will have to buy some plexiglass in order to keep the maps flat and prevent them from shifting.

Although in practice the rules for the game are rather simple to play, they still tend to be quite a chore to read, with many exceptions (I still regularly need to check how woods affect zones of control and marching, for example). The advanced scenarios can also seem rather daunting due to the extra rules for reinforcements, supply and other campaign-specific requirements.

Where to Start

There isn’t a designated starter game like there is in many other series, so for GCACW I would honestly recommend whichever campaign interests you the most, or whichever game you can find at an affordable price; MMP doesn’t really do many reprints of older titles, so unless the game was released recently, you will be forced to use the secondary market (usually at a mark up).

If I was really pushed to give a recommendation, I would suggest either Hood Strikes North, since it’s one of the few single map campaigns of the series, or Roads to Gettysburg II, since it features both the Gettysburg and Antietam Campaigns (two of the most pivotal battles in the war), and the advanced scenarios have some of the easiest supply rules present in the series.

My Own Opinion

I am absolutely in love with this series, and I try to play it every chance I get. I have a deep interest in the American Civil War, but I’ve always been disappointed that most of the games featuring this conflict tend to be tactical-level games, a scale that has never really interested me all that much. When I was introduced to GCACW I felt that it had everything that I wanted from an ACW game and I had finally found an operational-level ACW game that really spoke to me.

I have extremely fond memories of the various sessions I’ve played, creating stories that I still cherish to this day. The game excels at creating stories, where individual leaders succeed beyond all expectation or fail miserably, and I think this is something that a lot of wargamers (both miniature and hex and counter) look for in their games. Everyone wants to experience moments where a flanking maneuver or a brave charge secured victory, and GCACW has this in droves. If you have any interest in the American Civil War, I would heartily recommend the series as a whole.
