Tabletop Battles Reviews: Conquest Last Argument of Kings - The Lost, The Found, and the Lost Ancestor Models and Rules

May 12th, 2026 via Goonhammer

Building the Miniatures

Starting with the Lost Ancestor, this is a resin character kit and very much follows the usual expectations that come with that material. The components are fairly large and chunky, which immediately makes the assembly process feel more forgiving than some of the finer, more delicate resin kits out there. There’s the standard prep work involved—cleaning up mould lines, a bit of scraping, and assembling with superglue—but nothing here feels overly demanding.

What really stands out is the design of the model itself. The smaller, grounded figure is the armoured host, while the true “Lost Ancestor” manifests as the larger, flame-like entity erupting from the back and rising above it. That contrast between the solid, grounded armour and the expressive, almost spectral figure above gives the miniature a real sense of narrative even at the build stage. It’s an eye-catching sculpt and feels like a genuine centrepiece model as you’re putting it together. Overall, it’s a smooth experience for resin—straightforward, not overly fiddly, and well within the comfort zone of anyone who’s worked with resin before. For that reason, it lands at a solid 8/10 as a resin build.

Moving on to The Lost and The Found, these are plastic kits and share a very similar assembly process. The core bodies are essentially the same, with the main distinction coming from their loadouts—The Lost featuring their signature flame elements, while The Found are equipped with their more mechanical, cannon-based components.

The simplicity here is genuinely refreshing. These are not overly complex kits, and that works in their favour. The parts are large, the joins are intuitive, and the overall construction is quick and satisfying. While the sprue quality is generally decent, there are a few areas with more noticeable mould lines than you might expect. That said, because these are larger, bulkier models, a bit of sanding and cleanup goes a long way and doesn’t feel like a chore.

Dweghom Ancestor painted by Josh Gagel

Instructions are clear and easy to follow, and importantly, there’s very little in the way of fiddly or frustrating assembly. These are sturdy, “brute-style” miniatures, and they behave accordingly during the build.

One limitation worth noting is pose variety—it’s fairly minimal, so you won’t get a huge amount of differentiation between models. More noticeably, there’s an ongoing issue when ranking them up on their stands. The bulk of the models, particularly their arms and weapon elements, means they can clash with each other when placed side by side. This often requires a bit of angling and adjustment to get them to sit neatly together, which can be a minor but consistent frustration.

All things considered, these kits deliver exactly what you might want from a straightforward plastic build: quick, solid, and relatively stress-free. As a result, they come in at around a 7/10 for the build experience.

Overall, if you’re looking for a clean, uncomplicated assembly process—especially compared to more intricate kits—the Lost Ancestor and the Lost/Found units are a great choice.

Painting the Models

My approach to painting both The Lost and The Found was largely the same, built around a straightforward slapchop method that let me get strong results fairly efficiently.

I started with a black undercoat, followed by a dry brush of Mechanicus Standard Grey, and then a lighter dry brush of Pro Acryl Titanium White focused on the upper edges. This worked particularly well on the larger surfaces—especially the stone elements around the cannons on The Found—giving me a strong value sketch before applying colour.

From there, I used a green ink over the stone sections, which came to life thanks to the slapchop base. It created a natural-looking highlight and contrast with minimal effort, and the same approach worked well on the shoulder details across both units.

One challenge I ran into was the lack of clear definition between materials—particularly where the skin meets the stone elements. At times it was difficult to tell where one surface ended and another began, which made the painting process feel less intuitive than it should. When you’re trying to work out what you’re painting rather than how to paint it, it does slow things down, and that was probably the weakest part of the experience.

Outside of that, though, the process was really enjoyable. The metallics were a particular highlight. I used Balthasar Gold with an overbrushing technique to build up a rich copper tone on the pipework. Before that, I placed thinned white into the recesses, followed by a glaze of fluorescent orange (AK Interactive), creating a convincing internal glow. The brighter tones showing through the gaps beneath the metallic layer worked really well.

For the cloth, I used Flesh Tearers Red contrast, which sat nicely over the slapchop base and gave a strong, saturated finish. Skin tones were handled with Guilliman Flesh, again relying on the underlying dry brushing to provide definition.

For The Lost, the flames were the key feature. After the dry brush stage, I base-coated the flames in white, then built up from fluorescent orange in the recesses through deeper reds like Mephiston Red, finishing with a darker, smoky edge using a near-black tone. A mix of wet blending and dry brushing helped create a sense of depth and heat.

Dweghom Lost painted by Josh Gagel

The same approach carried over to the Lost Ancestor, where the large flame element becomes the focal point. The smaller armoured figure beneath was painted more traditionally, using Leadbelcher for the armour and Retributor Armour for details like the beard rings, providing a strong contrast between grounded metallics and the more supernatural flame above.

To finish everything off, I created a lava base using Mordant Earth. Underneath, I painted a mix of reds, oranges, and yellows to simulate molten lava. I then applied a thin layer of PVA glue and allowed it to fully dry before adding the crackle paint. This step is key, as it creates larger, more dramatic cracks, revealing the glowing colours beneath and selling the lava effect.

Overall, the painting process was efficient and visually rewarding. The Lost Ancestor stands out as a centrepiece and comes in at around a 7.5/10, while The Lost and The Found are slightly held back by unclear sculpt definition, landing closer to a 6/10.

On the Tabletop

The Lost

The Lost function primarily as a support-focused melee regiment. With Clash 3 and 5 attacks, they can contribute in combat, but without cleave, their damage ceiling is limited. Defensively, they’re solid enough with 6 wounds, Defence 3, and Resolve 4.

Where they shine is in their support abilities. Their draw event grants Relentless Blows to nearby units, while their Stride aura provides +1 March to infantry within 8". Combined, these buffs can dramatically increase both speed and offensive output.

A standout combo is with Wardens. Boosted by the Lost and supported by a character with the Crown of Ushkalos, Wardens can reach March 8 with Impact hits, effectively behaving like fast cavalry. It’s a powerful and entertaining interaction that gives infantry a surprising threat range.

However, outside of this kind of synergy, The Lost struggle to justify their 180-point cost. They are heavily dependent on supporting other units rather than standing on their own.

The Found

The Found take on the ranged support role, with Volley 2, Barrage 3, and Range 14. Their output is decent, and Arcing Fire gives them flexibility, though it’s often redundant due to their size.

Their Battery rule, reducing enemy March after dealing damage, is situational. Where they really improve is through Elemental Power Markers, boosting them to Barrage 4 and Range 18. At that point, they become a much more serious threat.

At 160 points, they are reasonably costed and fit well into shooting-focused lists. However, they rely on resource support to reach their full potential, meaning they perform best in armies that can consistently generate tokens.

Dweghom Found painted by Josh Gagel

The Lost Ancestor

The Lost Ancestor is a flexible hybrid character, combining combat ability with spellcasting. With Clash 3, 5 attacks, and Cleave 2, he can contribute in melee, and his Wizard (6) gives access to multiple spell schools.

His most interesting interaction comes from combining Lethal Demise 6 with the Bloody-Minded mastery, allowing him to always die fighting. This results in a significant burst of damage when he’s removed.

However, he suffers from being outclassed by more specialised options. Characters like the Tempered Sorcerer or Steel Shaper perform similar roles more efficiently and at a lower cost.

A classic Jack of all trades, but master of none. If you want a good wizard, bring the Steelshaper/Sorcerer, if you want a good combat character, bring the Raegh and buff him up.

Unit Ratings

The Lost – 4/10 A niche, combo-dependent unit. Strong in specific builds, but underwhelming and overcosted on their own.

The Found – 6/10 A solid ranged option that becomes effective with proper resource support. Well-costed and flexible.

The Lost Ancestor – 5/10 A flexible but unfocused character. Usable, but often overshadowed by more specialised and cheaper alternatives.

Final Thoughts

Overall, these are fun and characterful units, even if they’re not the most competitive choices available. You’ll likely see them more in casual or experimental lists than in top-tier tournament play.

The Lost and Wardens combo is a clear highlight—fast, aggressive, and genuinely entertaining. The Found are also worth exploring, particularly in token-heavy lists where they can maximise their ranged potential.

The Lost Ancestor, while interesting, is the hardest to justify, given the strength and efficiency of alternative characters.

From a hobby perspective, though, these kits are a success. They’re easy to build, enjoyable to paint, and perfect for experimenting with fire effects, glow, and lava basing. A good fluorescent paint and a high-quality white—such as AK Interactive’s Titanium White—make a big difference in bringing these models to life.

In short, while they may not redefine the meta, they offer a great mix of hobby enjoyment and creative gameplay—and that’s often more than enough reason to get them on the table.
