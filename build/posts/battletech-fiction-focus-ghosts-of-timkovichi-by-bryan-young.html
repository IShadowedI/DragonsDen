BattleTech Fiction Focus: Ghosts of Timkovichi by Bryan Young

May 15th, 2026 via Goonhammer
I swear I'm not Bryan Young's publicist, and I’ll have reviews of other BattleTech authors’ work ready soon, but, uh…

Today marks the release of Bryan Young's latest novel, Ghosts of Timkovichi, which you may remember from the preview we hosted last month! I’ve had a chance to read the whole book by now: Read on for my (spoiler-light) reactions!

Cover of Ghosts of Timkovichi, Credit: Catalyst Game Labs

Who Are the Ghost Dogs?

The Kell Hounds had a rough time of it in the Dark Age era, landing squarely in the crosshairs of the Jade Falcons’ then-Khan, Scorched-Earth-is-a-First-Resort strategist Malvina Hazen. Malvina decapitated their leadership structure, took their homeworld, Arc-Royal, and did her best to wipe them out entirely. In that chaos, black-sheep Kell Hound heir Callandre Kell ordered most surviving Hounds to go to ground, dispersing and disguising themselves, shedding their iconic Kell Hound colors and insignia to escape extermination.

At the time of Callandre's dispersal order, the unit which would soon become the Ghost Dogs were the Hounds’ Second Company, the Wolfhounds, based out of Galatea. The Ghost Dogs stories are fairly distinct in the corpus of BattleTech fiction for maintaining a Company-scale perspective throughout: Bigger picture than stories focused on a single Lance or Star (like Young’s Fox Patrol tales), but smaller and more interested in characters beyond the Great People of History than books which span interstellar empires.

The main characters of the Ghost Dogs stories are the unit’s leaders, who are…

Col. Hank Mallory: The archetypal getting-too-old-for-this hero, a father to his men. Very conscious that one of the leaders he’s mentoring will succeed him when he retires.

Maj. Ilsa Escalera: XO and Alpha Lance leader, Ilsa’s logistical expertise is what keeps the Ghost Dogs fighting as they work to establish a new reputation for themselves. She's the usual sounding board for Hank’s worries.

Cpt. Raven Davis: Bravo Lance leader, Raven is professional and dedicated. She's relatively straitlaced compared to the rest of the Ghost Dog crew, but does a good job of rolling with whatever schemes the crew cooks up.

Cpt. Rufo Nguyen: Charlie Lance leader and agent of chaos. Rufo's out-of-the-box thinking and tendency towards impulsive decisions in the heat of the moment would make him a poor fit for a regular military unit, but he's right at home with the Ghost Dogs.

There's a pretty decent-sized cast of recurring characters beyond those four, but most of them are little more than a name, a physical description, maybe one or two memorable personality traits, and a handful of speaking lines. Given the scale (and word count) of the stories, that makes sense, but it does risk making some deaths more a statistic than a tragedy - particularly after Charlie Lance shifts from an all-mech composition to combined arms, with almost none of the tankers and infantry even getting named.

Kell Hounds Wolfhound. Credit: Jack Hunter

Who Aren't the Ghost Dogs?

My initial exposure to the Ghost Dogs was through the Star League Discord server, and I really only knew them as “the trans flag mercs.” Their out-of-universe genesis dates to a (now well-past) period in which the then-moderators of the BattleTech Reddit server were deleting posts of mechs painted in Pride flag color schemes. My understanding is that the Ghost Dogs’ colors (dominated by desaturated pink and light gray, with light blue and white accents) were chosen as a way to give painters a non-bannable excuse for painting Trans Pride mechs. It's a canon color scheme, after all!

Unfortunately, this led me, as a transgender fan, to assume that the Ghost Dogs were an unabashed Blue Hair And Pronouns mercenary force. I wasn't able to back the Mercenaries Kickstarter campaign (which featured an advance release of the Ghost Dogs’ debut novella, Giving Up The Ghost, as a backer reward), so to me the Ghosts existed only vaguely, at the edges of my awareness, until the short story “Way of the Ghost Dog” (since collected in the Tales of the Inner Sphere anthology) was released as part of Catalyst’s monthly free fiction program.

At the time, I was terribly disappointed. The paint scheme is basically a running gag in “Way” (with the pirates they're fighting assuming that they must be a merc company themed around salmon, as in the fish); Hank still openly dislikes it at that point, and I got little indication that anyone in the Ghost Dogs was queer. One of Raven’s thinly-sketched lancemates is nonbinary, and later novella Let Slip The Dogs of War saw queer-coded punk-rockers Gilchrist and Taylor join as the leaders of the Dogs’ armor and infantry, but it wasn’t until a running subplot with a civilian love interest in Ghosts of Timkovichi that the reader actually spent any significant time with a trans character in a Ghost Dogs story.

I went back and actually read all the Ghost Dogs stories from the beginning while prepping to review Ghosts of Timkovichi, and once I was able to set aside what I wanted the Ghost Dogs to be, I definitely found myself appreciating the stories for what they actually are. Just, if you’re a queer reader, I caution you not to expect them to be something they aren’t. (Also, go read Russell Zimmerman’s serial novella Violent Inception.)

How Did They Get Here?

Ghosts of Timkovichi is the fifth Ghost Dogs story, following…

Giving Up The Ghost, a novella covering the Wolfhounds’ transformation into the Ghost Dogs. It probably would’ve been possible to rush through the plot points covered in Giving Up in a shorter story, but the novella length really lets this book shine as an introduction to the team. It’s pretty much all stage-setting, but well worth a read.

“Way of the Ghost Dog,” a short story found in the Tales of the Inner Sphere anthology. I liked “Way” much better when I re-read it after Giving Up. It’s brief enough and action-packed enough that it doesn’t have much time for proper character introductions, and I definitely think it hits better if you already know the cast going in. It does have a fun framing device where the start and end of the story are Hank’s meeting with the Dogs’ contact with their employer (their “Mr. Johnson,” if you speak Shadowrun), with the middle an extended flashback to the actual job they were hired to do.

“Extraordinary Rendition” is a longer short story found in issue 21 of Shrapnel, Catalyst’s quarterly BattleTech magazine. I’d say it’s probably the best standalone piece in their saga if you want a quick sampling of the Ghost Dogs, just a fun bunch of outside-the-cockpit intrigue on Solaris, after the Ghosts are hired to (extra-legally) extradite a war-criminal-turned-Solaris-jock.

…and Let Slip the Dogs of War, a novella showing the Ghost Dogs defending a planet in the Free Worlds League from the Marian Hegemony. This one is an outlier among the Ghost Dogs stories in that it's neither an ensemble piece nor really focused on Hank. It's firmly centered, at least emotionally, on Rufo Nguyen and the Marian commander, Legatus Quintus Liberalis, struggling down parallel paths of grief after suffering battlefield losses which cut to their hearts.

Aside from Hank’s focus on nurturing his subordinates to pave the way for his retirement, the main thematic throughline of the Ghost Dogs stories is that they are constantly punching up against foes better-positioned, tougher, and/or more heavily armed than they are, winning through guile rather than simple skill at arms. Their company runs light, with a trio of 70-tonners (two Periphery-chic Hounds and a single glass-cannon Warhammer C2) as their heaviest assets. Rufo, Raven, and Hank himself all run in 35-ton Wolfhounds, which Hank favors for their ability to rapidly reposition across shifting battlefields. They always start off on the back foot and have to scheme their way into coming out on top, which makes for engaging storytelling! Everybody loves an underdog.

Lyran Commonwealth Wolfhound. Credit: SRM

The New Novel

I ran into a few formatting issues the last time I tried to use the spoiler-warning block I’m accustomed to, so let me say this the old-fashioned way: This review will contain some spoilers regarding themes and premise-setting events in Ghosts of Timkovichi, but will steer clear of major plot twists and/or emotional gut-punches.

Now then…

Ghosts of Timkovichi sees the Ghost Dogs recalled by the resurgent Kell Hounds and employed by Callandre Kell - now Grand Duchess of a fledgling state called the Arc-Royal Liberty Coalition - to return to Timkovichi, the world where the Hounds’ previous commander, her uncle Evan Kell, died along with countless other Kell Hounds, civilians, and Clanners when a frustrated Malvina Hazen turned to orbital bombardment to seize the planet. They’re tasked with facing off against both the skeletal remnants of Timkovichi’s Jade Falcon occupiers and the better-equipped forces of its would-be liberators from the Lyran Commonwealth in a bid to grant the planet’s people the right to self-determination.

The elephant in Ghosts of Timkovichi’s room, the plot point I can’t actually discuss the book in any detail without mentioning, is thankfully something which the preview chapter hosted on Sarna already introduced, so go read that quickly if you want to see how this next bit is introduced in the book itself.

Okay, ready now?

Cool.

So, more than anything else, at its emotional core the story of Ghosts of Timkovichi is a story of fatherhood. It’s a story contrasting Hank’s easy, practiced mentorship of the Ghost Dogs with his strained, uncertain relationship with his decade-estranged biological son. To the book’s credit, there’s plenty else going on, and proper attention is given to other developing plot threads and emotional throughlines, but the central question is “What happens when the commander who’s ‘a father to his men’ doesn’t know how to be a father to his actual son?”

Ghosts of Timkovichi is far from the first action-adventure sequel starring an aging male protagonist to introduce an estranged son to up the emotional stakes (see Mutt Williams in Indiana Jones and the Crystal Skull or David Marcus in Star Trek II: The Wrath of Khan, for instance), but between the prose format, the length of this book (Young’s longest to date), and the author’s skill as a storyteller and experience as a father, that thread develops better here than it might in an overstuffed movie. We see Keith Mallory grow from an opaque enigma, seen primarily through the point of view of a father who doesn’t know or understand the man he’s become, into a leader, character, and peer in his own right, and I’d say it’s a very effective emotional journey.

One of the main forces working against Hank as he struggles towards trying to reconcile with his son is the trauma of being on Timkovichi itself. We find out in this book that Hank is one of the precious few survivors of the bombardment of Greywalk, and Timkovichi is basically a planet-sized trigger for his PTSD and survivor’s guilt. I called Let Slip the Dogs of War an outlier in my introduction and I stand by that - Ghosts of Timkovichi is less laser-focused on the theme of grief, spending time with a broader cast and making space for more emotions - but there’s also a clear throughline across Let Slip, Ghosts, and previously-reviewed Fox Patrol novel Outfoxed of Bryan Young grappling with the heavy psychological toll war takes on warriors. It’s compelling material, and it’s refreshing to see him handling stuff like this in a wargaming setting with therapy-informed nuance.

The less-interpersonal, more-political/military aspects of the book refreshingly bring the partisan resistance of Timkovichi to the fore. The locals are vocal and active self-advocates for their own liberation to a degree I’m not sure I’ve ever seen in a BattleTech story before. Troubadour and newsman (and propagandist, power broker, spy, etc.) Tom Frost, the heavily Tom-Waits-inspired figure introduced in the book’s prologue (previewed on this very site!), is an absolute scene-stealer in his intermittent appearances, but other locals also play significant, memorable roles as leaders, organizers, protestors, and freedom fighters. From Ukraine and Palestine to Minneapolis, the resilience of occupied peoples seems like an ever-more-relevant topic in our modern world, and it’s good to see a planetary populace in a BattleTech story consistently portrayed as actual political actors who have the largest stake in the conflict, not merely a prize to be won.

The opening sequence of the novel sees the Ghost Dogs [recruit / bond / adopt, delete according to your own cultural bias] a trio of young Jade Falcon sibcadets, but I’m happy to report that their subplot is neither a retread of Alexis and her sibkin’s adoption into Jiyi’s Falcons in Young’s A Question of Survival / Without Question duology, nor is it a mirror of the uneasy Spheroid bondholder / Clan bondsperson relationship in Philip Lee’s Letter of the Law. They’re used more to contextualize the Jade Falcon garrison on Timkovichi, and to underline the desperation of the Ghost Dogs’ position on the planet when they inevitably find themselves forced to deploy captive Clan children to help fill out their numbers. I would’ve liked to have gotten to know the Those Two Guys of the trio better, but they follow the trend in Ghost Dogs stories of characterization thinning out the further you get away from company leadership, an unfortunate but understandable cost of the story’s scale.

Rufo gets significant subplot focus again here, which might be a bit surprising after his near-starring role in Let Slip, but delightfully he’s back in full shenanigans mode, offering broadly-lighter point of view chapters full of madcap antics and a cute budding romance with a local civilian, providing a nice palate cleanser. His story isn't all fun and games, though, and he's certainly not immunized from the rising tension, stakes, and costs of the latter stretches of this book.

And the stakes do rise. The Ghost Dogs are warned going into the situation on Timkovichi that a significant contingent of Lyran reinforcements is expected to arrive some time after they make planetfall, and I don’t consider it a major spoiler to say that yes, they sure do show up and they sure do pose the biggest problem the Ghost Dogs have ever faced. As I’ve said, they’re used to being underdogs, they’re used to fighting against long odds, but Ghosts of Timkovichi keeps ratcheting the danger higher, ultimately threatening the question… What happens if the Ghosts run out of tricks? What happens if they’re actually forced into a straight-up fight?

I know Young’s been accused of being a soft touch before, largely on the back of the positivity of early Fox Patrol stories, but he absolutely does not shy away from the human cost of war in this book, and I respect and am horrified by the results in equal measure.

Antagonist PoV chapters are somewhat more limited in Ghosts of Timkovichi than in most BattleTech fiction, used sparingly but with significant impact. I was impressed overall with how Young handled point of view in Ghosts, with most narrators having a very distinct voice (though Ilsa and Raven stood out a bit less due to their relatively no-nonsense attitudes). Narrators also come and go with the demarcated parts of the book in keeping with rhythms of the plot and story (of particular note, the entirety of Part 4 is seen through the eyes of the Falcon and Lyran occupation forces’ commanders in a sequence of escalating dramatic irony). It’s a well-crafted tale.

Wolfhound 2X Variant. Credit: Porble

Final Thoughts

I must acknowledge that I am specifically and personally vulnerable to some of the parent/child relationship heartstrings pulled in Ghosts of Timkovichi, but I found it the most emotionally impactful piece of Bryan Young’s BattleTech work that I’ve read. In broader BattleTech canon it’s probably rivaled only by Violent Inception in terms of “How many feels did this give me?”

Beyond that, I thought it was a well-crafted tale which both used its returning characters well and rapidly endeared me to its new additions to the cast. I do really wish some of the “little guys” in the unit got more attention, but I understand why they don’t. This book is definitely long enough as it is.

As I alluded to in my intro, the pink-blue-white stylings of the Ghost Dogs really don’t do anything for me without any thematic weight to back up the aesthetic (shout out to my girl, Clone Trooper “Sister,” for keeping it real), but I’ve come to admire them as the Kell Hounds / A-Team hybrid they really are.

I do think the build to Timkovichi across the previous Ghost Dogs stories, or at least Giving Up The Ghost > Let Slip the Dogs of War > Ghosts of Timkovichi, is kind of necessary for this book to land with full impact, but it should still be enjoyable as a standalone piece.

I’ll warn you to get your tissues ready if you’re at all emotionally susceptible to a fraught parent/child relationship, but Ghosts of Timkovichi is well worth a read regardless of whatever pain it might surface. Good book.

Next time you see a book review from me I’m planning to travel back in (publication) time to one of the other “Fortunes of War” novellas which came out of the Mercenaries Kickstarter alongside Giving Up The Ghost, Jason Hansa’s A Skulk of Foxes. I look forward to seeing you then!
