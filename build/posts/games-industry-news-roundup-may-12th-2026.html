Games Industry News Roundup- May 12th, 2026

May 12th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Here at Tabletop Battles, we know that it’s hard to keep track of all the news happening all the time in the games industry. So much is always going on, all over the place, all the time. With games of all sorts, and their related media, being an informed gamer can feel impossible some days. 

That's why every week, we help you stay in the know by rounding up five of the biggest recent stories in the gaming and gaming-related pop culture sphere in the Games Industry News Roundup. Our trusty news boy, Dan “Swiftblade” Richardson, is right here at the very real, and cool TTB newsdesk with the scoop.

Mantic Teases Warpath Global Campaign with 2026 Roadmap

Credit: Mantic Games

Mantic Games pulled back the curtain on its big plans in 2026 for the Warpath universe of games last week in a roadmap, including a swathe of new releases and even a global campaign. 

For Summer 2026, Mantic is focusing on digital STL releases on its webstore and Mantic Vault. Alongside this, Mantic is partnering with MyMiniFactory to launch a new 3D printing tribe for its skirmish level game in the Warpath universe, Firefight. Through a monthly subscription, fans of Firefight and 3D printing will receive a host of STL files usually focused on a certain faction. The service has two tiers: the basic recruit tier and a premium commander tier, which includes extra files like tanks and walkers to go alongside the infantry models included in the recruit tier. Both the recruit and commander tier include a welcome pack of Enforcer miniatures at no extra cost, ensuring anyone who joins the tribe will have a fully playable firefight force from the jump.

In the fall, Mantic promises a big event that will shake up the Warpath universe and will cross over between all three of the Warpath games (Warpath, Firefight, and Epic Warpath). This major expansion for all three games will come alongside a global campaign that Mantic promises will “shape the future of the Warpath universe”, and will potentially allow players to decide the very fate of the galaxy right from their kitchen table. Alongside this release, Mantic will round out the Epic Warpath faction releases with wave 2 of releases rounding out the existing faction rosters, and wave 3 adding new factions entirely.

Finally, for winter 2026, Mantic is promising new heavy hitters for several of their games. Highlights of these marquee releases include the introduction of the Nameless faction into Firefight with all new plastic miniatures, as well as new vehicle super heavy units to shake up the skirmish format and provide terrifying new threats to the game.

The Firefight Tribe is live now on MyMiniFactory, and at time of writing Mantic hasn’t given any further hints or details as to what the global campaign will be about later this year.

Asmodee Purchases Rights to Times Up!

Credit: Asmodee

Asmodee is continuing its aggressive push to grab up as much of the board game industry pie as it can, with its most recent acquisition of popular game Times Up! planting the company flag on the shore of the casual party game market.

Times Up! is a party game created in 1999 by R&R Games, and plays similarly to charades. The active player is given a card with an idea, concept, or person and their team must guess what is on the card correctly in time, with each round the active player being allowed to give less and less information to use as hints for their team. The game has multiple expansion releases, with each focusing on a different theme for the cards contained inside.

Asmodee is no stranger to Times Up!, having published and distributed the game on R&R’s behalf since 2005 through Zygomantic, the studio responsible for many of Asmodee’s other social game titles such as Werewolf of Millers Hollow and Dobble. According to Asmodee’s press statement, the purchase of the full IP rights for Times Up! is a reflection of the company’s “commitment to investing in brands with strong global recognition and long-term development potential” as the social game industry continues to expand.

“Time’s Up! is a unique brand with enduring appeal across generations,” said Alexandre Alonso, Studio Head of Zygomatic. “Its simple concept, social energy, and replayability make it a cornerstone of the social and party game segment, and it’s been here for almost 30 years. We are excited to integrate it fully into our portfolio and shape its next chapter with a clear ambition: modernize the range, expand internationally, and ensure it remains a must-have title for every family.”

No information has been shared about the price Asmodee paid to acquire the full rights for Times Up! from R&R, only noting that this purchase “will not have any impact on the company’s financial performance.”

EVE Online Team Rebrands, Partners with Google for AI Testing

Credit: Fenris Creations

The team behind popular massively multiplayer Space Combat/Microsoft Excel simulator Eve Online, Crowd Control Productions (CCP), announced last week that the company is officially rebranding as Fenris Creations and partnering with Google DeepMind for AI research and testing. 

The rebrand comes shortly after Fenris’s former parent company, Pearl Abyss, sold Fenris back to the company’s CEO for $100 million plus $20 million in cryptocurrency tokens. As part of the statement from Fenris regarding its rebrand and newly found independence, Fenris Creations clarified that there will be no change in the team or leadership structure at the studio, and they will continue operations as normal in their home studio in Reykjavik. 

When asked by Gamesindustry.biz as to why the company decided to change its name from CCP after decades in business, CEO Hilmar Veigar Pétursson cited the confusion between Crowd Control Productions and the Chinese Communist Party.

"It is an interesting journey of the brand collision between Crowd Control Protections and the Chinese Communist Party," Pétursson told Gamesindustry.biz. "That is a growing collision. It's a fight I'm not going to fight. I'm not going to fight the Chinese Communist Party over who is the CCP. Obviously, the Chinese Communist Party doesn't think of itself as the CCP. That's a Western construct. They refer to it by a Chinese name.”

"But in the English-speaking world, we have increasingly had problems. I have seen reporters write stories about us where we are literally referred to as 'the other CCP'. We have CCP employees going to the US and being questioned over the name. These are not big things, but I can sense where this is going. We were already making all these changes. It's a good time to do it; if we don't do it now, will we ever?"

Alongside the independence and new studio name, Fenris announced that the company is partnering with Google Deepmind to “explore areas including long-horizon planning, memory, and continual learning, using EVE Online as a uniquely rich environment for study.” The AI will be run on an offline version of EVE online, separate from the influence from any of the game’s active playerbase. As part of this partnership, Google Deepmind purchased a minority stake in Fenris Creations.

“EVE is built to endure - and it only works if you’re willing to keep pushing into the future,” Pétursson writes. “This transition gives us direct ownership, clear accountability, and the independence to invest in worlds that grow over decades.”

Psychonauts Development Team Unionizes

Credit: Double Fine

Another video game development team to organize and unionize its workers for protection in an increasingly unstable industry. This time, it’s one of the most storied development teams in the gaming industry: Double Fine Productions.

Founded in 2000 by industry legend Tim Schafer, the studio gained worldwide notoriety for its first game, Psychonauts. Empowered by their critical and commercial smash hit, Double Fine went on to develop and produce a number of other popular titles, including a sequel to Psychonauts, the heavy metal adventure game Brütal Legend, the more experimental title Keeper, and remasters of both Grim Fandango and Day of the Tentacle. Microsoft purchased Double Fine in 2019 as a first party developer for Xbox, following the development of Psychonauts 2.

Aftermath reported that Double Fine filed a petition to unionize with the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) on May 7th in collaboration with the Communication Workers of America (CWA). 

The CWA told Aftermath in a statement that “On May 7, the workers at Microsoft studio Double Fine Productions announced their decision to form a union with CWA to preserve and extend the studio’s commitments to creative excellence, diversity and inclusion, and worker quality of life. In tandem with requesting voluntary recognition from the company, workers have also filed an election petition with the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) to secure union representation. We appreciate that Microsoft has taken a neutral approach and agreed not to interfere in any way with worker’s rights to organize unions.”

Despite the union neutrality agreement Microsoft made with the CWA and NLRB expiring late last year, Microsoft appears to maintain its neutral stance on labor organization within its development studios. Said neutrality agreement, made in the wake of Microsoft's historic acquisition of Activision Blizzard, allowed for thousands of employees across multiple subsidiary studios to unionize, including multiple teams at Bethesda, Zenimax, and Blizzard, and begin negotiations with Microsoft leadership over union contracts.

The new union at Double Fine includes all of the workers currently employed there, including both regular part time and full time workers, for a total of 42. Double Fine or the CWA hasn’t made any statement yet as to when it plans to present a contract to Microsoft and open negotiations at this time. 

D&D Beyond Rolls Out Weekly Content Drops as Hasbro CEO Pushes for Live Service Game Model 

Credit: D&D Beyond/Wizards of the Coast

Games as a service has long been a buzzword in the digital games realm, but it looks like now it's creeping its way onto tabletops, as last week D&D Beyond announced a new feature that will give subscribers new weekly content for Dungeons and Dragons games.

This program, called D&D Beyond Drops, is designed to provide subscribers at the hero or master tier levels with new weekly content for use in the home campaigns of Dungeons and Dragons. These include things such as new spells, feats, subclasses, one shot adventures, and even virtual tabletop maps and assets. To kick off the new program, D&D Beyond is releasing a pack of 500 new assets, including 125 maps, 250 reveals, 10 stickers, 5 new feats, and 5 new spells. Following this initial release, new encounters for DMs to use in their campaigns (alongside their VTT assets) will be released to subscribers, with a larger monthly drop of new items, spells, feats, and other rules that don’t neatly fit anywhere else in the D&D release cycle.

This constant drip feed of new content is familiar for players of live service video games, which provide players a consistent drip feed of new content in order to keep them playing indefinitely. This model is both wildly successful in the industry, producing mega-hits like Fortnite and Destiny, but in recent years is more commonly responsible for multi-million dollar disasters such as Anthem, Evolve, Skull and Bones, Concord, and most recently Highguard.

Despite this, Hasbro CEO Chris Cocks is enthusiastic about the idea of modeling Dungeons and Dragons after live service games, telling GamesRadar in an interview that while books will always be an integral part of the D&D experience, a piecemeal release schedule makes more sense with a large number of players using digital platforms to play the game.

“We can start to release components or aspects of that book over time, and you don't have to buy everything all at once,” Cocks says. “You can buy chapters or segments of it over time. That makes a ton of sense to me.”

Cocks assuages those who doubt the bite sized releases and want bigger, all at once expansions typical of D&D through splat books and campaigns, saying “that said we will still have big moments. We will still have like, 'hey, ta da, here's a huge campaign.' You can expect there'll be more around that, both from us and from all the creators in the world that can leverage a platform like D&D Beyond to share their content as well."

While subscribers for D&D Beyond Drops will have access to the program’s entire back catalogue, there aren’t any plans as of this writing to eventually release this content to the wider playerbase of the game, and sharing Drops content with those outside the hero or master tier levels is restricted on the platform.
