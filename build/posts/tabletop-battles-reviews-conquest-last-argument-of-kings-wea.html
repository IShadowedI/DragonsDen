Tabletop Battles Reviews: Conquest Last Argument of Kings Weaver Courts Update

May 5th, 2026 via Goonhammer
A rare mid-cycle update from Para Bellum has brought the poor, sad, lonely children that are the Weavers back into the contest! From the beleaguered release back in late 2025, the Weaver Courts have struggled to gain the tracking and momentum that the likes of Yoroni had. New model fatigue? Hatred of trees? Many reasons were thrown around, but the obvious and honest answer was that they just sucked!

BUT NOT FOR LONG! The wood elves Weaver Courts may now have their time in the upper-middle levels of the odd meta that is Conquest. Gone are the seasons being a fruitless (eyyy) clip on, and seeds being nigh impossible to have when you need them. An almost-full faction overhaul has hit, and there's some fun to be had!

Oberon - Credit: Rachel

Starting with the Seasons: These are now a constant source of special rules, shenanigans, and overall fun times. Most of your army is now tied to your warlord's season, barring some unique season-specific units, so pick wisely as you’ll be seeing a lot of it! My personal favourite is winter for the army-wide dread, but a sprinkle of characters to add in a little seasonal spice is highly recommended! Alongside winter’s chill, you’ve got the shocking heat of summer, the speed of spring, and the morale-breaking fall of autumn. All the rules are useful, and it feels very satisfying to have spring give you the leg up on arrival, followed by a charge aided by the shock summer brings; you really feel like a force of nature. You can still mix in (on the whole) characters to give units multiple seasonal tags to extend your benefits for some real combination play.

Seeds and Blooms are under a whole new system too, with your own units now collecting them rather than stacking them on your opponent. Between this and the Bloom's change, the bookkeeping aspect is a lot easier, but nice seed tokens/dice are a must, or you will forget them, and you will be sad. With three now being the max, you can spend one-to-three for a variety of effects. This is the new Bloom mechanic, and its effect are all done as draw events (these can be used in addition to others, so fear not fluid formation moths!) One seed gets you a few cheeky extra attacks or barrage being a nice way to boost some poke damage, two is always your Warlord-specific Bloom, and three allows just about any unit to benefit from the current seasonal bonus. The seeds themselves still stack up after a volley or clash action, but there are plenty of extra little ways to rack them up early, so these are a nice expendable recourse to push up the power of your units. Not good at keeping units alive (and oh boy, do Weaver units like to die)? Fear not! Your seeds can be transferred to any, still living, friend within 12".

Now, I mentioned your second-tier Bloom was tied to your Warlord, and I'd be remiss not to talk about them more. The same selection of four heroes are at your disposal; one shooty, one choppy, one magicky, and one that's really trying to be support. The Mhor Sidhe (choppy) and Rhun Sidhe (shooty) are tied to autumn and spring respectfully; the wizard (Seanchas) and Tao Sidhe have some choice of seasons. The Warlord traits all give your unique Bloom, and a supercharged version as a once-per-game proc. The traits themselves are all very solid, with the ability to gain some much-needed cleave, a heal, reroll sixes, or deadly blades and shots if you ambush your prey. At a recent five-round tournament, I took a Winter wizard to give my mainline troops dread during winter months as well as her always active ability to redistribute seeds at the end of each round, and a nice additional free spell per turn. The dread is nice to limit some output against your units, and always having seeds where you need them was incredibly helpful, though don't get too confident; even with dread, your units are low wounds and usually max E2, so they'll drop to anything other than poke. I'm curious to test further using other Warlord traits, with spring giving some minor healing and a nice speed boost, or autumn Warlord cleave as a Bloom, making them the most aggressive lord.

Rhun Sidhe. Credit: Rachel

Now for the meat and veggies of the update: Units.

Although the majority of the units have had only minor changes and still fulfill the same battlefield role within the army, the tweaks to Blooms and seasonal effects have made them have real potential. You, like all good Nords players, will have to utilise speed and mobility to get good engagements. You do not want to be taking head-on fights with others; with limited cleave and armour-piercing, you will struggle to cut through even the most moderately of armoured foes.

Starting with the light regiments, you have two options here: the quick, squishy Kern Skirmishers, capable of some descent mid-longer range poke, or the beautiful Will-o-Wisps. I think most armies, be it casual or competitive, will be running a couple of Kern units. They have good enough output to harass the enemy or plink away at objectives, and enough speed to give a solid reinforcement line early doors. The downside to this is their pitiful D/E1 and low resolve, with no loose formation to help against counter fire to boot, so they are not winning any prizes for durability. The other option is the 100 point Wisps. I want to love these, I really do; they are so pretty and cute and lovely. But I've just found them very underwhelming. They are okay as a road block, but with 4 attacks and clash 1 they will still die to a stiff breeze and will have output almost nothing in return. For 25 points more, the Kerns have solid ranged damage and will sit in the way almost as effectively. The thematics of the swarm leading the charge, a volley of arrows flying through them and into the enemy is a really fun image, but very lacklustre in practice.

Medium regiments are where the heart of the army lies. A really good mix of infantry, brutes, and the bestest moths you ever did see! As of the date of release, a few units are still to be released and a couple of rules yet to be edited (sorry Gallowglass, you don't get all seasons) so I will focus on what is out. The ents Living Trees are currently the only reliable anvil in the list, so a unit or two of them is a solid starting point. With some vanguard and constant season effects, they can get up the board speedy for those early zone steals, and D3 with shield keeps them in the fight. Damage output is a little lacklustre, owing to the no cleave and poor Clash stat, the constant inspired means they can use their unique rule to pass out seeds like champions. A cheeky hero, such as a brutish Mhor Sidhe, is a very nice way to sure them up also. Gaining R4 and some extra attacks with cleave and terrifying makes them a hard hitter. (I also like giving them the decay relic as watching your opponents units off themselves to a chilly tree always makes me giggle.)

The other infantry options hail from autumn and spring alike and offer a nice mix of blender chopping and some disciplined rank and file troops respectively. These both fill a nice niche in the army and will see good play each, especially now the points have had some tweaks. Most infantry can take the same officer that dishes out 1 auto wound per activation, which is a lovely little way to finish off those peaky high defence/tenacious units (I assume, I forgot about them in 5/5 games played at the last tournament and remembered just in time for the next game to start, and for me to promptly forget again).

The summer unique unit of Scaile Dancers have had a lovely glow up; they gained double time, as well as extra attacks and evasion during summer. Mixed with the right lord and relics, these can turn into a vicious blender unit that has fly.

Three finished-ish Scaile Dancers. I'm not entirely happy with the wings, and I haven't based them yet because I'm waiting on some nice marshy foliage to arrive. Credit: Rachel/PB

Now, my favourites... the Moths. These little nuisances had more people mad at insects than you thought possible. The more melee-focused version have had their bombing run supped up to be a flat 3X the stands in your regiment, so a damn good chunk of damage for flying over someone with a move 8, fluid, flying cavalry piece! But the joy of the army are the shooting Moths. They may not have fluid, but my oh my does their output make up for it. Surprisingly easy to manoeuvre owing to fire and advance, they easily get into enemy flanks, where their full opportunist rerolls, and additional AP(1) really shine. Three shots a Moth seems low, but when you hit with almost all, every turn, they can really put down a couple of stand per action. This then causes the enemy to fear coming towards you, or rush you to desperately tie them into combat and overextend. A big MVP in a local worlds event in the UK, the Moths are a staple of all lists for me. Though still be careful; they have been dropped from five, to four wounds, and it is VERY noticeable.

The as-of-yet unreleased units look to add even more variation and, in my opinion, really help the faction with the few areas it struggles in currently. With Wyrd Cavalry helping deal with armour, and the gallowglass being a very solid anvil and damage piece.

Credit: Rachel

The heavy slots are limited as of now, with only the two dragons -- sorry, "Draics" -- and the Gemred knights. Both dragons seem rather solid, one focusing on monster clashes and raw melee output, and the other being a more utility, poke and holding unit. I ran the fire-breath boy and he did me well, even if the lack of any terrifying still baffles me. His slight self-heal is nice; given that all heals in the army seem to be "non monster" only, it helps with some sustain, though I feel "fly trap mouth" is likely the slightly stronger option, with terrifying 2 and opportunist. Both can be mounted by one of two heroes, which is always a fun conversion to make too!

The Moose Knights are an odd one. Their impact damage is nothing to be sniffed at, 4 at brutal 2, but down to 6 wounds at D3 I found they didn't hold the line as well as I was hoping, and you don't really want to be sending things off unsupported with this army; trading blows and units is not your friend. Saying that, they are currently the only reliable cleave and my unit of 3 plus hero did pull some solid work.

New off the press are the second Gallowglass unit, a D4 brute with fly, potential cleave 3 and a beautiful buff beetle vibe, they will likely become a staple for a lot of players.

 

Weaver Courts Coill Draic. Credit: Rachel

How They Play

With only a week or two from release, it's not 100% certain where the new-look Weavers will sit into the meta but, from early testing, they will be happy competing with the best. The tough fights come from any high damage factions, such as SK, as you are a low wound count army with little in the way of defence against magic and counter-fire shooting. In these match-ups your positioning will be paramount, and using the flanks and your speed will be the way to win. High defence is, as mentioned, a bit tough. With little to no cleave D4 feels like a mountain, but with good positioning, shields can be ignored and, eventually, those bricks will fall. I found myself often relying on my skirmishers and trees to hold a centre zone, and going hard into one or both flanks to then push back in and surround the enemy. At the end of the event, I had come 5th, falling short of two Sorcerer Kings, a Nord and a very nice 100 Kingdoms player. I'm excited to see what the coming month brings and what fresh unit will be shown off to bolster the ranks!

Trout's army for a World's event - Photo by Blitzbear
