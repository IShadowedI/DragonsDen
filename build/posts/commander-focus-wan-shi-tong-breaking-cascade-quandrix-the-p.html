Commander Focus: Wan Shi Tong, Breaking Cascade, & Quandrix, the Proof

April 30th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Secrets of Strixhaven has arrived, and with it, a new set of five Elder Dragons for our favorite format here, Elder Dragon Highlander. These are always destined for popularity, and indeed, all five of the school's newest takes on their Founder Dragons sit at spots 1-5 on EDHREC's podium of popularity for the set. Coverage of SOS centers around these backbreaking buildarounds, and as someone whose domain largely rests in the underexplored, what on earth am I doing talking about Quandrix, the Proof today? It's not the interaction between her ability and the Suspend Spells, like Ancestral Vision—that's already known, and over half the Quandrix decks out there are on the package of Vision, Crashing Footfalls, & Inevitable Betrayal to Cascade into with their cantrips. That's also true here, but is a pedestrian aspect of the deck. Instead, I'm far more focused on the fact that we have Green's Creature tutors, need to recycle very specific Sorceries, and have access to Blue's interaction lineup. This isn't just a Quandrix list, as hidden beneath a banal Simic value Commander is something I've wanted to cover in full for a long time: Wan Shi Tong, All-Knowing, as our 'Secret Commander'. In exploring that version of Quandrix, I've arrived at what I believe to be her most cohesive, powerful, and fun 99 that I've yet seen on the internet. And trust me, with how popular this Elder Dragon is, that's a big sample size. Join me as I reinvent the wheel, and take Quandrix, the Proof from Simic value to something truly unique.



"Put the Exiled Cards on the Bottom in a Random Order"

Let's first talk about Cascade, because it's worth knowing the specifics on its resolution as we discuss the thrust of this deck. When you cast a Spell with Cascade, you place above it on the stack a trigger, which tells you to exile cards off the top of your Library, until you hit something with a lesser mana value. You have the option to cast it, then those remaining exiled cards are put back on the bottom of your Library. Unlike so many effects, this triggers cards which care about cards moving back into the Library, namely Wan Shi Tong, All-Knowing (hereby referred-to as WSTAK) & Dutiful Knowledge Seeker, and if you 'hit' those cards with Cascade, they see the remaining cards during the trigger's resolution return to the Library, too. Casting cards that move game objects also gets a bit funky with Cascade, as playing a Sylvan Tutor with Quandrix onboard sees you flip cards until you hit one with mana value 0 (the Suspend pieces), then search your Library for a Creature and put it on top. In short, you Cascade before cards move, and that's especially relevant with the core gameplan for Quandrix: Abusing the ever-loving hell out of free Spells.

Quandrix's actual text reads closer to 'Your mana value 1 Instants & Sorceries gain one of the following effects at random: Create 2 4/4 Rhinos with Trample, Draw 3 cards, or Grab the best Creature from target opponent's Library'. This is derived from how Cascade works with Spells that have mana value 0, as while you can't traditionally cast something like Ancestral Vision, Cascading into it skirts around that restriction. This metaphorical 'added text' only exists as long as you have at least one of those three Suspend pieces in your Library, however, which means Quandrix is incentivized to play a profound wealth of ways to reset those cards. Conveniently, this is both returning cards to the Library (thanks, WSTAK), and many of these effects are mana value 1. If you cast something like a False Mourning or Salvage targeting a Suspend Spell, with at least one remaining in your deck, you can cycle through these effects a number of times. That sequence, of putting 1-2 of these pieces back with a Dwell on the Past-style effect, only to Cascade into the remaining, not only racks up two WSTAK triggers each time, but both refills your hand and fills up your board. The more of these cards we're on, the better we can chain them together, never running out of text to tack onto our 1-cost Instants & Sorceries.

Built with Two-by-Fours

Crackship or Commander Deck, you decide.

The crux of Quandrix is clear, with us looking to jam 1-cost Spells followed by ways of resetting the yard, but we cannot forget that our Commander is a whopping 6 mana. She's strong, yes, but comes online later than the vast majority of Commanders in the format. There's some silver lining, in that Quandrix herself instantly recoups value by way of having Cascade, but one other facet is important to her function: Priority. See, we don't actually want to get to 6 mana, but instead, 7; due to Cascade being a cast trigger, we retain priority after Quandrix herself resolves, given she has no enters effects. Therefore, we're guaranteed to launder at least one Cascade trigger onto a Spell, even if it's a Sorcery, in the face of her imminent removal. We certainly can afford to play her with 6 mana available, so long as there's a free Spell in hand like Gitaxian Probe or Mutagenic Growth, but by & large the aim is to have a mana spare for protection or enablers. Luckily, being in Green, we have access to the most consistent, resilient way to count to 7: Traditional Land Ramp. Cards like Skyshroud Claim are akin to real world physical media, going from expected and even banal, to out-competed, but now regaining some nostalgic allure in a world of increasingly efficient Artifact removal. Simic has an expansive suite of options to turbo out a ramp Spell that finds two lands for 4 mana, given the wealth of Three Visits/Growth Spiral-esque effects present. Our ideal curve is fairly simple, going from a 2-mana ramp Spell, to a 4 mana ramp Spell that finds two Lands, to making our fourth Land drop, and having 7 mana available. It's by no means radical to suggest a curve like this, but the fact these ramp pieces still accrue value once we have plenty of mana, by being given Cascade with Quandrix, helps them feel loads better than they'd otherwise be.

That's a sensation which rings true of many things in Quandrix, where pieces that are reasonable (if not a bit old-fashioned) hold special luster in the context of everything else going on. Case in point, our interaction package: Unsubstantiate, Remand, and their ilk. Being able to return specifically Spells to hand, as opposed to just countering them, does a lot for us. Not only do they have the option of bouncing something back if Cascaded into (Hitting a Narset's Reversal off of a Ranger's Path feels tons better than hitting a Counterspell), but in putting that Spell back in hand, we can cast it again for another Cascade trigger. Doing something like Brainstorm into Remand—both from hand—still gets us two Cascade triggers, and leaves Brainstorm available once more to dig for another Suspend piece. Remember, those are also WSTAK triggers, too. Not to be a D&D Bard, but in the context of wanting to slam Quandrix, little beats options that do more than one thing at once; she excels at multitasking, and that goes for the Spell lineup as well.

"With WSTAK on the Stack..."

Blue morning, Quandrix day

With so many ways to reset the strongest part of Quandrix, and ramp into her, how are we truly breaking this list via its Secret Commander? First and foremost, we need to find the Bird. The great news is that Green as a color has the lion's share of Creature tutors, here being Sylvan Tutor, Nature's Rhythm, & Time of Need for the main ones, and some more flexible options we'll cover in a bit. Sylvan justifies itself primarily in so being a 1 mana Spell, and Time of Need is quite efficient, especially given we're on more Legendary Creatures than just WSTAK to nab as needed. Rhythm though, that's perfect for ramping on 2 into Dryad Arbor (fulfilling that portion of the curve), and Quandrix having 6 power means she can Harmonize out WSTAK from deck on her own, with no mana needed beyond 4 {G}s. Using Quandrix as a path to WSTAK also bodes true in the case of Vivien, Monster's Advocate, a monstrous card in this list. Quandrix herself is 6 mana, meaning we can downtick Vivien to get both our Commander & Secret Commander in one fell swoop, but that effect to cast Creatures off the top is a huge get; we're not only shuffling our Library a ton, but also placing cards from the yard back on top, so we can turn those into soft tutors if Viv is on the table. At her worst though, she makes pretty good blockers, or 3/4ths of a Rhino per turn.

Even in spite of all our shufflers however, there will come times when you find the Suspend pieces in your hand. Not to worry, we don't intend to wait very long, because there's a reasonable stretch of ways to get them back where they belong: The Library. First, in terms of ways to hardcast them, there's Jace Reawakened—Jace can Plot any card of mana value 3 or less from our hand, including the 0-cost Suspend Spells, where we can cheat them from exile on a later turn. As Foretold is similar, giving you the chance to cheat larger and larger Spells, but you'll always be at 0+ counters from the get-go. A better opportunity is in the form of Brainstorm, though, putting cards back on top of the Library, and Sylvan Library provides a similar degree of control over what stays in hand. At worst though, you can always just discard and reshuffle them, via things like Formidable Speaker; there should never be a point during the game where you're without access to Cascading into at least one of the three options, unless you've met some severe hate.

Fair in Spite of Itself

In spite of the core gameplan being to launder 'draw 3' or 8/8 worth of Trample onto the board from Spells that cost ≤1 mana, I've intentionally kept the win conditions of this list fair. That seems a peculiar word to associate with the above outcomes, but what I mean by that is the intended lack of strict combos—no Thassa's Oracle, no Altars to translate tokens into mana. The mana you see, barring free Spells, is what the deck has access to, and without Haste enablers like a Craterhoof or Concordant Crossroads, we're forced to untap with our massive board to deal lethal damage. For someone so combo-brained, why on Arcavios would I do such a thing? Simple: This recalls the format's namesake, 'Elder Dragon Highlander', and with my so rarely drawn to cover literal Elder Dragons I wanted to push for a battlecruiser-y win condition where I could. If you want combos, I've got literal dozens of other articles; that said, we are running one teensy, tiny option, being Basalt Monolith & Mesmeric Orb. This is a callback to my original coverage of WSTAK, where I identified (correctly) that the best way to win with the bird was by emulating "four horsemen", a banned Legacy deck. The long and short of that is by infinitely twiddling a Monolith with Orb on the board, and a shuffler in deck (here Gaea's Blessing) we can generate an infinitely large Dutiful Knowledge Seeker or Murktide Regent, or infinite Spirit tokens via WSTAK. Still, these are combat wins, and require the relevant pieces to lack summoning sickness, so I've deemed it within bounds.

Cardboard boxes, the cowards, the fools!

One of our 4 mana ramp pieces, The Water Crystal, also puts a fairly reasonable clock on opponents, turning Mesmeric Orb into 5 cards milled per permanent untapped. Critically though, because our deck is looking to make Rhino tokens & steal the best opposing Creatures, we do intend to win via combat most of the time. Having mill as an alternate path for players who have managed to loop a fog, that's just a bit of insurance. A great example of how the deck manages to slot in an aggressive win effortlessly amid its core gameplan is Arixmethes, Slumbering Isle. We're already on Time of Need to find WSTAK, most of the time, but in this scenario it's also a mechanism to find our 4-mana ramp, which similar to something like a Skyshroud Claim puts us up two mana. We're able to stay on schedule, and eventually turn that pivotal, highly accessible ramp piece into a genuine threat after casting a ton of Spells...something we do naturally. So much of what you do flows into itself, cascading in the non-keyworded sense from one part of the strategy to the next. You don't need to drop everything and rush to a combo, because your path of play will innately make your board bigger than those of your opponents, with little they can do to stop it.

Example Decklist: I Saw Quandrix Kissing Wan Shi Tong

Onto the pet cards here, undisputed champion of which is Rod of Absorption. At time of writing, a mere 2 decks on EDHREC are on this card for Quandrix, which baffles me. Rod allows you to 'store' Instants & Sorceries cast by any player under it, spending mana into the Artifact to shoot off any number of equivalent value or less. The thing is, Rod at just X=0 or X=1 allows you to cast 1-3 of your Suspend pieces plus a cantrip, or potentially means of resetting them, giving you the opportunity to double up on huge effects like Ancestral Vision for a meager cost. Another card in only 1 listed deck on EDHREC? Mystic Speculation. This is a repeatable 1 mana trigger for Quandrix, at the lowest cost for a Buyback spell with mana value 1—you can use it to loop through Suspend pieces, or just set yourself up to draw the right cards with other effects. The name of the game in this list is consistency, and not needing to hit more 1-cost Spells ensures you're about as consistent as can be expected, even if it's gently taxed.

Then, there's Anurid Scavenger, a rather funny Frog that cycles back a card each Upkeep, and blocks decently. By no means is this the most powerful card in the world, but it's consistent, and repeatable, with no extra mana needed to pay into an ability (in contrast to, say, Dutiful Knowledge Seeker). The last pet card here I want to highlight is Tamiyo, Inquisitive Student//Tamiyo, Seasoned Scholar, a card which flips readily, and has a number of perfect targets for its -3 in this list. We run a whopping 9 targets here that are 1-mana Green Instants/Sorceries, so more than most any deck she could be included in, flip Tamiyo is always extracting maximum value. For a card which sees consistent play in a number of competitive 60-card formats, that's notable indeed.

Decklists are kept updated, and may change with set releases.



Currently, this list rests in the nebulous space known sometimes as 'Bracket 2.5', or 'Game Changer-less 3', a rather popular niche for play in both paper and online. That sweet spot allows you to slot into either side of the curve, between those potent 'technically 2's, or with folks that swear their upgraded precons are Bracket 3. Ultimately, in my experience testing post-Secrets of Strixhaven, this is rapidly becoming the most widespread power level in Commander. What that means for the Bracket system on the whole, it's hard to say, but if you wanted to slot in proper combo tools, it wouldn't be difficult; things like Altar of Dementia turn infinite WSTAK triggers into a win, and you can do something similar with four horseman-ing a Narcomeba with a shuffle trigger in Library. This list can play combos, or indeed, something like a Thoracle line...but because two of your key tools are about putting bodies on the board, namely Crashing Footfalls & Inevitable Betrayal, pivoting to combo so quickly seems against the grain of what Quandrix wants to do.

Proof Positive

Lucas Graciano brings us another amazing dragon, following Murktide Regent (also in this list!)

It's rare that I cover a Commander with such popularity, and moreover, with the confidence I've done so in this article. The delta—the difference in performance—between this and your average Quandrix deck is likely higher than nearly any other Commander I've covered; from testing this behaves as a well-oiled machine, and operates with a consistency that I've been pleasantly impressed by. Quandrix here is an entirely different beast than her most obvious point of comparison, Imoti, Celebrant of Bounty: Where the former aims to cast and reload a minimal package of Cascade targets, the latter is trying to chain a broad swath of them together, one into another. The difference is down to control, not in play, but in deckbuilding. Imoti's design implores you to try and run table from mana values ~8 to <5, whereas Quandrix gives savvy players the chance to 'know' what their theoretically random Cascade triggers will hit. It's the divide between Mass Polymorph and Reality Scramble, exploded out to the Cascade archetype as a whole. Suffice to say, I prefer the latter, having covered a Volo Scramble list as my very first Commander Focus on the site.

There's something special about running a big 6 mana Dragon in the Command Zone though, and I say this as someone not generally motivated by the thematic allure of Magic. Playing a real Elder Dragon feels good, it provides a sense of awe and power that something like a random party member in Final Fantasy can't (Love you, Gau). You're given a wide berth when you rock up with something like Quandrix, the Proof, and while this is undoubtedly more a spellslinger deck than strictly stompy—though the former is just means to the latter—I've had a ton of fun bashing heads in with herds of Rhinos and opposing threats regardless. Battlecruiser Magic isn't what it used to be, as you do now need some mechanism to 'break' the game in order to stay relevant, but Quandrix brings that feel back alongside her finest companion, Wan Shi Tong. We're more than halfway through the schools of Strixhaven now, having covered Witherbloom in my Lluwen article, and Lorehold via Gau, so next week we'll be making a splash with Muddle, the Ever-Changing and making use of some creative rules surrounding copiable values. You don't want to miss it.

Until next time, the proof is in the putting (cards back in the Library).
