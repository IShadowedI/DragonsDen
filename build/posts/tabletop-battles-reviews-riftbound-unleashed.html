Tabletop Battles Reviews: Riftbound Unleashed

May 8th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Editorial note: Riot Games and publisher UVS provided us the starter decks and box of booster cards used in this review. Specifically, this review set consisted of the Vex and Vi starter decks, a vault box, and a box of sealed boosters from Riftbound: Unleashed, which is the third set released after the game debuted at the end of 2025 with Riftbound Origins.

Thanks once again to Riot and UVS for providing these for our review.

Last week, I introduced you all to Riftbound, the League of Legends themed card game through the Spiritforged set, which was my introduction to the game as well. This week, building on that, I was able to spend some time with the newest set, Unleashed, which releases today, May 8th! If you didn’t get a chance to read our review of Riftbound, I suggest doing so as I spend a good amount of time going over how the game works; this week, I’ll be focusing specifically on Unleashed, the new mechanics and cards, and talk a bit about how my feelings on the game has evolved after being able to spend about a month working from one set to another and seeing how the game seems to be evolving along those lines. I’ll state that Riftbound is a fast paced and interactive TCG that I’ve fallen quite smitten with, and encourage you to check the review for more on that!

Riftbound Rundown

As a quick refresher, Riftbound is a TCG primarily made for 2 players, but can scale to 4, set in the League of Legends universe from Riot Games. If you’re unfamiliar with League, the game that started it all is a quick paced Multiplayer Online Battle Arena (or MOBA) that has grown in popularity over the last nearly twenty years to become a gaming cultural staple. Riftbound is the latest spin-off of the League universe that takes the familiar Legends (the characters players are able to select from in League) and the perhaps less familiar universe setting that the game takes place on the planet of Runeterra. You really don’t need to know anything about how League of Legends plays or even who any of the characters are to enjoy Riftbound, but if you are a fan, there are a lot of fun little touches that stick out in the flavor text. Like most TCGs, flavor is exactly that, so whether or not you have ever queued up for League of Legends in your life shouldn’t impact your ability to enjoy Riftbound.

Your Legend determines your deck colors (here Green and Purple), as well as providing you a unique effect.

In Riftbound, players select a Legend to base their deck around, and each player has a 40 card Main Deck, a Legend, Champion, and 3 Battlefields, 12 Runes and an 8 card optional Sideboard to build around. Unleashed doesn’t change any of that, which sounds odd to praise, but I really love that the game is digging in on the specific format and victory conditions that make the game engaging and interactive. I was slightly concerned that there might be attempts to ‘shake up’ that formula early on, as I’ve seen various other TCGs do, often to their detriment. Thankfully, the team at UVS seem committed to building a solid foundation that means that new players can jump in to the newest release of Riftbound, construct a deck, and start playing with new and old players alike.

New For Unleashed

Of course, a new set often means new mechanics in a TCG, and Riftbound: Unleashed follows that trend with a new set of keywords: Ambush, Backline, Hunt, Level, and Predict. These build on the existing keywords of Accelerate, Hidden, Legion, Assault, Shield, Tank, Deathknell, Deflect, Ganking, Temporary, and Vision, and seems to imply that future sets will continue to add to the system and bring ways to shake up the game state based on those new mechanics interacting with the existing meta from previous sets. Although not a part of the set itself, Riot has also started to note the meta and ban various cards that were overly dominating, and with the rotation of older cards through bans and the introduction of new cards, that made me satisfied with the direction the game seems to be going on. I think bans, rotations, and updates make for a healthy game, and although sometimes players feel like bans rob them of their favorite toys, a healthy meta survives when dominating combinations are removed sooner rather than later.

Leblanc's Backline ability means she takes damage last, making her harder to kill.

Some of these new keywords provide some shake ups that seem much more minor than they actually are. For example, Backline states “This unit takes combat damage last”, which makes it the opposite of the existing keyword Tank, which reads “This unit takes combat damage first”. In Magic, combat damage is dealt by choice of the attacker, with keywords like First Strike sometimes determining how or when that happens. With Backline adding to Tank, Riftbound’s combat has begun to deepen as well, as cunning players can ensure that cards they want to protect can take damage at the end of combat rather than earlier, sometimes meaning that they survive; in Riftbound, if a unit is not defeated in combat, once that combat ends, all damage is erased from that unit, meaning that a Backline card can sneakily remain on a Battlefield simply by forcing players to deal damage to it last. Combining both keywords can even make for vexing situations in which taking a Battlefield through combat can be far more complicated than before.

Vex, meanwhile, has the opposite keyword, Tank.

Ambush is a new keyword that adds “You may play this unit to a battlefield where you already have one or more units as a Reaction”, essentially making it an instant speed deployment. Since players can take turns on combats, a player who potentially passes and then responds with an Ambush unit can now have more to do at Battlefields than simply placing units or casting spells, instead allowing for sudden shifts in the battle state due to the sudden appearance of a new card (and, potentially, the response options from their opponent similarly expands). In my initial review of Riftbound, I praised the way the game allows for instantaneous interactions between players, because something that makes TCGs sing for me is when players can interact with one another in ways that are not simply “I attack, you defend, you attack, I defend”. I like when the game feels like an actual, live standoff, and when players are able to take advantage of various tools to help them seek victory.

Predict should be familiar to players who are used to mechanics like Scrying in Magic.

Predict is a new keyword that allows players to filter their own decks, allowing them to look at the top card(s) of their main deck and then recycle (place on the bottom) any number of them, putting the remainder on top in any order. This is similar to effects like Scrying, except that you don’t permanently lose the card by discarding it into your trash; instead, you are able to reorganize the deck hopefully to your benefit. When I was playing with Spiritforged, I noted that many cards have various Draw effects, but I did make note of the fact that there was not a lot of deck searching or filtering, which Predict has now introduced. At 40 cards a deck and 3 max copies of cards per deck, some of that was a little less needed than in larger deck games, but Predict in my playtesting has worked out pretty well; it is a powerful ability, but it doesn’t feel like it completely defeats the purpose of proper deck construction. Instead, it really helped make various strategies a little easier to pull off by helping to ensure they happened when needed.

Master Yi wants Levels to activate abilities, but other cards like Poppy can use XP to draw cards.

The biggest new keyword is Level, which takes advantage of cards and effects that provide you with XP, and that XP can be used to Level cards for new effects. In Unleashed, that primarily happens through Hunt, which stipulates that when a unit conquers a battlefield (essentially, captures it that turn), they gain XP. This is a pretty obviously easy to expand on Keyword that I expect to see new variations of in future sets, as the current keyword is just one way to do it, but I am sure there will be more ways to gain XP as it does help investing in various cards; for example, Legend Master Yi really wants you to accumulate a lot of XP to get to level II and level III, which provide permanent buffs to all your units, and I would love to see how future cards can take advantage of this mechanic beyond buffing units.

New Legends, New Strategies

Unleashed also introduces 12 new legends to the game: Jhin, Rengar, Pyke, Vi, Lillia, Master Yi, Vex, Ivern, Diana, Leblanc, Kha’zix, and Poppy. If you’re unfamiliar with League of Legends, none of those names really mean anything to you, so the biggest thing here is that there are 12 new Legends across all color variations, allowing more experimentation with previous deck strategies in various color combinations and also allowing for new decks to use previously released cards. There is a pretty large amount of Legends still to come, but so far I really enjoy how distinct each one of them tends to be, even with very simplistic differences between them. For example, Poppy has the ability to gain XP by holding a battlefield, and then spending XP as a resource to draw cards, while Leblanc allows you to discard a card to create a copy of a card at a battlefield you conquer or hold. Each legend’s unique ability tends to guide you towards the type of deck you would want to work on creating (Leblanc, for example, wants you to make lots of token copies of things), but none of them are extremely restrictive, since you can also focus on cards in the color combination you want to use and experiment with that as well.

I'm a big Leblanc fan, so I'm all over this new set.

The two starter decks that we were sent, Vi and Vex, were a lot of fun to play, and I was happy to see that they felt as equally balanced as the previous set’s starter decks. I also was pretty happy to see that games between the previous edition decks and these also were fairly equal, although there was a bit of an advantage to some of the newer cards having mechanics the previous edition couldn’t quite take advantage of. Even with that caveat, the win/loss ratio was still basically 50/50, and with some light deck construction of new cards into the older decks, that became even more stable.

Seriously we love Evil Woman here.

Although competitive meta is going to swing towards certain decks and combinations that are likely to win more than others, I also have to admit that I appreciate that most legends have fairly minimal effects that manifest in interesting ways, rather than some Legends having game altering effects while others do almost nothing; in the simplest of terms, this means that players can pick who they like and base it on aesthetics or playstyle (or even both), playing into the color pie of the game and working mechanically. For example, I really love Leblanc and Vex, so I already started working on decks that take advantage of that, and both have varied playstyles that feel unique and competitive in my small sample meta. I think it will be interesting to see how the competitive scene changes with these new cards and combinations in coming weeks at the various regional qualifiers following the sett release.

Riftbound Rips

When I reviewed the first set, I was really happy and impressed with the game overall. I had some reservations going into it that it would be serviceable but maybe nothing special, but then I was deeply surprised by the fact that the game actually was extremely good and also far more interesting than I had initially considered. When I was able to take a look at Unleashed, though, I knew that Riftbound had the potential to be something really special in the TCG space, especially with the heavy focus by Riot on the competitive scene and focusing on players actually, well, playing the game! While working on this review and experimenting with the new set, I curiously took a look at YouTube to see what people were saying about Riftbound, as well as other TCGs, and I found that almost every other game except Riftbound was nothing but videos about pricing speculation and investing, while Riftbound was primarily, to my surprise, gameplay focused.

Unleashed is a fantastic set to get into the game with; the 2 starter decks are very fun (Vex is my personal favorite) and make excellent introductions to the game, and the new cards mesh very well with the current state of the game and I expect to see quite a few of these cards appear. I did also want to make a final mention about the “vault” box that we were sent, which reminds me of ETBs from Pokémon or Magic’s older “collector” boxes that came with a set of booster packs, tokens, and some storage, along with land cards. The Vault works much the same way for Riftbound, and if you can get one, I really recommend it, as it comes with a sealed deck of Rune cards for every color. Something I noticed while opening the booster packs we were given is that Rune cards are actually a lot harder to obtain than I expected, and I also never really got a full playset of them across colors, often getting 2 or 3 of one color and various singles of another. If you are in the market for a new competitive TCG, Riftbound: Unleashed is an excellent time to jump into the game, and hopefully the set will be available and easy to pick up at your LGS; I’m hopeful that future sets will continue to be more available to purchase at retail, and that as the game grows, singles will also become easier to come by and make deck construction easier and more affordable for players.
