Warhammer Underworlds: Thyrielle's Zephyrites

May 16th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Thanks to Games Workshop for providing Tabletop Battles with review copies of Thyrielle's Zephyrites.

It's another Warhammer Underworlds preorder day! Today, we are graced with Thyrielle's Zephyrites, an order warband consisting of a magical fox that also happens to have some aelves following it around. Games Workshop already released most of this information in a Warhammer Community post earlier this week, but we can still dig into the warband and (most importantly) show off the awesome models that Blake Law painted.



Thyrielle's Zephyrites join Myari's Purifiers as another representative of the Lumineth Realm-lords, one of the the Age of Sigmar factions of Aelves. They have the distinction of being the 50th tournament legal warband in second edition of Underworlds. That's a lot of warbands!

They're also labeled as a Mastery warband, which doesn't really give any insight into how GW thinks they should be played. It just means they're a little funky in the rules department. If I had to put them into the Strike/Flex/Hold archetypes, I'd settle on Flex. On the whole, they don't have too threatening of a damage output to be a pure strike warband. While they have some neat tricks that can help hold treasures, in the end they are just elves (mosty) on a single defense dice so they're not going to be able to park themselves on treasures reliably.

Fire fox! Credit: Blake Law

Model appeal is pretty subjective, but I have to say I'm a big fan. Thyrielle has a dancer's pose with cool little details; the wind effect coming out of the jar is a little wonky, but I haven't seen a better way to sculpt wind so I'll cut them a break. Both of the archers are fantastic sculpts. Anara is your classic hooded, sneaky archer type. I keep picturing an old Wood Elf Waywatcher from WHFB when I see the pose. Orieth rocks the blindfolded with a massive bow in a very confident, bad-ass pose. Tzul obviously rocks, as nearly every Underworlds critter model does. Not only is the concept of a magical wind fox a fun one, but the almost floating, ephemeral look that it has makes it fit into the rest of the warband qutie nicely. The only miss for me is Sirikith. There's something about the model that makes the head feel a little too big and I keep picturing Farquad from Shrek when I see him.

Thyrielle's Zephyrites warscroll.

We're going to skip ahead on the warscroll to The Living Gale since that's what so much of the warscroll is focused on. The short version is that at the start of the Action phase, you place Tzul in a hex with no fighter and no feature token. In the second and third rounds, you have to remove Tzul and place them in a different hex than the one they started the round in – this means you can't just park the fox in one hex for the entire game.

Tzul doesn't count as a fighter, so the opponent can't attack them, but the fox hex does count as an occupied one so no fighters can move through it. Tzul doesn't block line of sight, so it's not quite creating a blocked hex, but given how many ranged attacks this warband is packing it feels like being able to block movement while leaving firing lines open is exactly what they'd want anyway.

While Tzul can't be placed in a hex with a feature token, you can still place them in any other empty hex. The rules define an empty hex as "a hex that is not occupied or blocked" which means you can drop the fox into any hex that doesn't have a feature token, fighter, or blocked hex in it. You can choose to place Tzul into a stagger hex to block an opponent's Tunneling Terror or Canny Sapper, a waystone hex to deny your opponent the opportunity to access re-rolls, or even a starting hex to interfere with cards like Commanding Stride or warbands that can raise fighters into starting hexes like Spiteclaw's Swarm or the Sepulchral Guard.

Being able to place Tzul as an on-demand barricade is great and all, but that's only a part of what the fox does. The windblown effect is what turns on most of the rest of the warscroll for the Zephyrites. This creates a six-spoked star radiating out along the faces of the fox hex and any model, friend or foe, is considered windblown when they're in any of those lines.

Tzul placement is going to be the main skill-check with this warband. You have limited opportunities to place Tzul, and once you do you are locked in for the remainder of the round (barring a one-use ability we'll get to in a second). Predicting where the action is going to occur on the board and/or forcing your opponent into those places is going to cause the warband's power to fluctuate. Having windblown in effect for the majority of the game is a substantial boost in power; meanwhile, if your opponent is able to avoid your wind tunnels and keep you from benefiting from them it's going to be painful. It does wind up covering a fairly large amount of the board in total – ranging from 19 to 29 of the board's 85 hexes (assuming you don't get interrupted by a blocked hex). It's worth keeping in mind this is also every hex adjacent to Tzul, meaning you can create a nice bubble around some focus point and still cover roughly one fifth to one third of the board.

The first of the reasons you'll want to be windblown yourself is the inspire mechanic. You need to make a successful attack with a windblown fighter to access their upgraded fighter stats. Fortunately, this doesn't have to be specifically a melee or ranged attack, and it still allows you to use weapon upgrades or tricks like Improvised Attack. The uninspired accuracy of fighters in this warband are resoundingly "okay" so you'll be relying on luck or wanting to stack the odds in your favor to reliably inspire with things like rerolls, extra dice, or setting up flanked attacks.

Since we're already on the topic of flanked attacks, let's blow on over to Hurricane Aid on the warscroll. It's incredibly straightforward – enemy windblown fighters are flanked. This works for both offense and defense, both of which will be appreciated by these fighters. The magical Christmasland situation is to have your attacking fighter and the enemy target both be windblown, that way you can benefit from them being flanked and still inspire. That probably won't come up all that often but it will feel great when it does. There's also a tiny bit of extra synergy here with, unsurprisingly, Deadly Synergy – cards in that deck that care about fighters being flanked could appreciate this extra method of granting that status.

Zephyr Dance grants +1 save dice for windblown friendly fighters provided they have a move or charge token. This is a way to boost the mediocre defense stats of the elves, but the restriction means you can't just cluster your fighters around Tzul at the start of a round and benefit from the survivability boost on everyone. Since your fighters natively only have one or two dodge, Zephyr Dance isn't making them into impenetrable walls – they'll be boosted to respectable or above average save values at best, so don't get too cocky. It does nicely synergy with Hurricane Aid if you can swing it by granting both more dice and more successful faces on those dice if you can pull it off.

Zephyr Leap boosts the already solid movement of the warband. Getting boosted up to five move and fly (potentially to hop right over Tzul) can help ensure your elves can get where they want to be. Combined with the ranged attacks that most of your fighters have, this means the Zephyrites can threaten almost any hex on the board. There's some interesting push/pull here between wanting to start a move while windblown to get the Zephyr Leap bonus and wanting to end your move windblown to benefit from Zephyr Dance. I've also typed the word "zephyr" so many times that it's starting to look really weird.

The first of the one-use abilities is One with the Wind. This is your mulligan for placing Tzul, but it does require Thyrielle to still be alive and it is restricted to being used in your power step. The timing means you can't immediately benefit from Zephyr Leap, extra accuracy from Hurricane Aid, or inspiring your fighter but you could park Tzul next to a fighter who just moved/charged to boost their save going into the opponent's turn. I'm not sure how useful it would be to do this, but if you really wanted liked where Tzul was now and wanted them to be in the same hex in the next round, you could use this in your last power step to shift Tzul to another hex, then use The Living Gale in the next action step to put Tzul right back where you wanted them.

The final warscroll ability is Cyclonic Pull and it earns the distinction of being the largest theoretical push distance possible in the game. You are limited to pushing towards Tzul and using this in your power step, but it still can push a fighter across the entire board. The timing lines up with One with the Wind so you could blow both of these abilities in the same power step to get some insane single use repositioning of one of your fighters. I am absolutely going to sit on this ability for too long in multiple games as I wait for the perfect moment to use it only to panic slam it for a suboptimal play later on. If nothing else, you can use it to zip onto an Aqua Ghyranis token right before the end of a round.

Fighters of Thyrielle's Zephyrites

This warband has a pretty typical four-fighter bounty split of 2/2/2/1 where the melee-only goober Sirikith is the least valuable bounty-wise. All of the fighters possess a respectable move of 4 (both inspired and uninspired), which isn't much of a surprise given they're elves. Defensively, they're all on some number of dodge for their saves – so beware of enemy fighters who have ensnare!

Since WarCom already posted all the fighter cards in a far better resolution than my pitiful scanner can ever hope to match, I'm instead showcasing Blake's pretty models.

Thyrielle herself! Credit: Blake Law

Thyrielle is the only fighter who starts on two defense dice, meaning she's the most durable but that's not a particularly high bar. This is a warband that needs to play keep away as much as possible.

Fortunately, keep away plays directly into their gameplan because the leader is one of many fighters with a ranged attack. It, along with her melee attack, is fairly average in terms of accuracy but by going after a flanked (maybe via windblown) enemy, the success chances improve drastically which will help her inspire faster. She's also sporting double critical grapple on her uninspired attacks that upgrade to full grapple once inspired. I'll be honest, despite having played hundreds of games of second edition I have never seen grapple come into play in any useful way, but if any warband can take advantage of repositioning enemy fighters, it's going to be this one.

Archer number 1 -- Anara! Credit: Blake Law

Looking at Anara's attack profile is pretty exciting, but also weird. The exciting bit is that she has a range three attack with critical grievous, making her one of the only fighters who can potentially deal two damage from three hexes away. The weird part is that her uninspired three sword attack is going to trigger the critical runemark more often than her inspired attack since it changes to two hammers. It's not a huge shift in accuracy either way, but if you're factoring in the extra flanked results you'll be able to engineer with Hurricane Aid and the two-vs-three dice in fishing for critical hits, it genuinely feels like a downgrade to inspire her.

She also has no melee attack on her fighter card, so pay careful attention to other cards. Some objectives specifically require melee attacks while some ploys and upgrades only enhance melee attacks.

Given that no other stats on this fighter change upon inspiring, it makes me wonder if inspiring is required upon making a successful attack while windblown. I'm inclined to say yes, because the inspire box on the warscroll doesn't have a surge icon and that's typically the way optional abilities are coded in the rules, but who knows. This is definitely an FAQ worthy question and I've already sent it in. As a reminder, you can send questions to whunderworlds@gwplc.com to have them considered for answers in upcoming rules updates.

Orieth is archer number 2, not to be outdone by his teammate. Credit: Blake Law

Orieth is basically Anara but better. He's another fighter threatening two damage at range, but also has the (probably seldomly chosen) backup of a melee attack with grapple if you need to swing an opponent around into the foxy breeze. The biggest difference between the archers is that Orieth inspires to two dodge, making him the only other fighter in the warband that can be on two defense dice aside from the leader. That defensive boost along with his quite appealing three hammer ranged attack makes him a top candidate in terms of inspiring.

Very long spear, but still only range 1. Poor Sirikith. Credit: Blake Law

The final body in this warband is Sirikith, aka the most likely to be used as bait, aka most likely to get someone's iPhone to wake up in the middle of a game. Given that he's the only fighter without a ranged attack, he's going to wind up on the front lines. Unfortunately for him, he's only sitting on three health and never gains a second defense dice, so he's the squishiest of the whole group.

As sad as it is to say, he's also one of the heavy hitters of the group. The two archers only guarantee a single damage per attack (but do have the chance of critting for more), but Sirikith is always going to be bopping for two. That's not a ton, but it's something. Inspired, he even becomes pretty accurate and picks up grapple to shove the enemy around. I just don't know how many times he'll be able to take advantage of that inspired profile before getting pasted.

Deck Pairings for Thyrielle's Zephyrites

Credit: Jake Bennington

They're definitely not a warband that I'd feel comfortable plating onto tokens and daring the enemy to come to me, so I don't think pure hold strategies like Emberstone Sentinels or Nexus of Power is where I'd want to take them. Likewise, they're not exactly threatening to table the enemy with a plethora of one and two damage attacks, so (like many elf warbands) I'm more interested in exploring the flex / midrange / counterpunch style with them.

Of the 12 decks that we have for options, there are a few that stand out to me as possible pieces of this puzzle.

Blazing Assault is (in)famous for having some very easy to score objectives and they all work well with ranged attacks. You're not likely to get Annihilation with this warband, so you'll want to pair with another deck with some higher glory payoff objectives. In the power card side of things, Blazing Assault has outstanding movement tricks including but not limited to Lure of Battle, Sidestep, Illusory Fighter, and Duelist which will all greatly aid in ensuring fighters are being windblown by Tzul.

Countdown to Cataclysm feels like it would be rough in the objective department, but getting another shot at inspiring via Improvised Attack is pretty cool. Desperate Rage also gives the warband access to a three damage attack which it definitely would appreciate. Great Fortitude and Utter Conviction help to cover the frailty that the warband suffers from.

Wrack & Ruin offers a few ploys to help reposition enemy fighters with Confusion and Damned If You Do. Combine those tools with W&R's pings to help boost the Zephyrite's middle-of-the-road damage. There are also helpful defensive tools like Fireproof, Desperate Defense, and Unstoppable to shore up some elven weaknesses.

I'm still not sold on Hunting Grounds in general, but if any warband can make it work it'd be one that's full of ranged attacks. Given that you can stand in your own territory and plink over the line at opponents who don't want to engage you (and even grapple them into your territory on occasion), it's at least something I'd like to explore. I'm forever annoyed that you can't score Pinned! off of a grapple attack, though. Mind Your Step is yet another enemy push tool that can be used to shove your opponents into the vulpine wind tunnel, too.

With all that said, here's a Hunting Grounds and Countdown to Cataclysm Nemesis deck for Thyrielle's Zephyrites. It's probably not an optimal deck (that'd likely be something tried-and-true such as BA/PP or WR/CC) but it's at least something weird that could have some unique interactions with the new warband.

The Verdict

Now that we have fifty different organized play legal warbands, the pool is deeper than ever. The Zephyrites give me a little bit of an Ephilim's Pandaemonium vibe in that they're going to require a careful hand and forethought to get the most out of them. I can't wait to sit down across from someone who has figured out how to best place Tzul to get the most out of the warscroll abilities; I just don't think it's something I'm going to be particularly good at myself!
