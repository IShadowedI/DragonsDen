Commander Focus: Engines, Theft, & Nita, Forum Conciliator

May 14th, 2026 via Goonhammer
What is an 'engine', in Magic? I would define it as being a non-infinite form of repeatable value generation, that translates some game action or resource into another, but there's no formal definition. The point here is, you take one thing, shake it around, and you're left with a different thing of equal or slightly greater value—in theory, the more knobs and levers you need to fiddle with, the greater your eventual payoff. Commanders have in the past tended to serve as part of an engine, either giving you resources to spend (an 'enabler') or providing benefit for doing a game action, or otherwise perpetuating the cycle of your intended engine (a 'payoff'). This has changed in recent years, however, in that many Commanders are at least in part both enabler & payoff, all in one. Your Teval, the Balanced Scales, or Y'shtola, Night's Blesseds. They tell you what to do, and get you a little bit there on their own, as to demonstrate the core gameplay loop all on one card. They are engines in their own right, fully formed and thrumming. In contrast, what happens when your Commander asks much of you, with no clear singular vision for its enabler, or payoff? As it turns out, you land near the bottom of your set's EDHREC page, with scattershot recommendations that trend towards goodstuff; we're talking about Nita, Forum Conciliator today, and helping her hum with the rest of her modern peers. She'll be an engine yet.



"Whenever You Cast a Spell You Don't Own..."

Nita has a wealth of words in her textbox, and the number of inputs required is staggering, compared to most modern Commander designs. First, in terms of mana, she requires {2} to activate, with the chosen exiled Spell also requiring its printed cost, payable via any color, and beyond that, there's need of a willing sacrifice as well. Your opponents of course need a legal target, and therefore must have cast, discarded, or milled a relevant Instant or Sorcery at an earlier point in the game, although even if you'd like to use an Instant at its intended speed, Nita requires you activate her preemptively, as she's only available at Sorcery-speed. So, you're paying full price for the spell, plus {2}, plus a body, and it can't be something you've chosen to include in your own deck...what's the payoff? A +1/+1 counter on every Creature you control...after having just sacrificed one. I don't mean to scare away potential readers in diagnosing Nita's problems, as she's no doubt a cool card (as we'll soon see), but it's fairly obvious as to why her popularity has remained low since the release of Secrets of Strixhaven. Theft is a popular archetype, but within it, Nita at first appears to ask too much and provide too little; she's a demanding Commander that doesn't explicitly push your gameplan forward, unless you intend to care about your board getting bigger, one cast at a time.

Nita does need to defenstrate Creatures to do it, though.

That's where Cryptic Trilobite steps in. As one of my personal pet cards, seeing the {2} in Nita's activation and that her payoff was likewise a +1/+1 counter, Trilobite pulls upon several synergies with Nita as an engine piece. That got me thinking, if Trilobite was so good, what of its peers? Well, both Hangarback Walker & Marketback Walker similarly cost {X}{X}, and accrue value via +1/+1 counters...and they're even great fodder for Nita's sacrifice requirement! When we're spreading counters around, if so many of them are of this ilk, Omarthis, Ghostfire Initiate allow us to double dip, and floats into a similarly large board, not unlike Hangarback. All of these creatures have mana value 0, and we're lucky in that tutoring small bodies is something White has profound access to, between both Ranger of Eos & Ranger-Captain of Eos, and if we're incentivized to do that, our eyes turn to some of the best 1-drop Creatures in Orzhov. You can see how extrapolating out from a single, highly synergistic option can open the floodgates for building out a list, and there's even more lessons to learn from Trilobite, regarding its ability to skirt the mana requirements for Nita to get going.

Workhorse Ahead? Do We Have To?

See, Trilobite's effect to remove a +1/+1 counter and add its specific mana is an activated ability, and far from the only way to turn Nita's trigger into spare mana. Workhorse & Crystalline Crawler do similar things, although at a 1:1 ratio of counters to mana, with the upside of being freely available. In their own way, each reduces the effective requirements of Nita's activation from {2}+the actual spell's cost by 1-2, but what if every Creature you controlled could do so? Enter the linchpin of our list: Agatha's Soul Cauldron. This card, at time of writing, doesn't even appear on Nita's EDHREC page, a shock to my system given its clear use case. While it's nice to grow your board after paying for a Spell out of the opponent's yard, we don't intend to play fairly with Nita, at least not entirely. While Soul Cauldron is often just used to launder activated abilities onto one Creature at a time, here we're making use of the fact that all your bodies with +1/+1 counters benefit from the cards tucked inside the cauldron. Suddenly, you're able to reduce Nita's effective cost to just the sacrifice, and requirement of a relevant piece to steal from an opponent's graveyard, and that's quite the appetizing proposition. Not only that, but via Hoarding Broodlord or Gonti, Night Minister we can further discount the amount we're actually paying for stolen options, or going wider and yet wider with Appa, Steadfast Guardian. The fact Nita puts the targets in exile first is a lifesaver, as you might be able to tell.

A thoroughbred of sin.

In a similar fashion to seeing White make tutoring for Trilobite a breeze, being in Orzhov makes finding Soul Cauldron fairly painless. There's Artifact tutors, like Delivery Moogle, or those which key off its Legendary status, such as Search for Glory, but Black carries with it the benefit of omnitutors—Diabolic Intent & Beseech the Mirror are great here, and special mention belongs to Grim Servant for being (at worst) a Ranger of Eos-like. We're sort-of building backwards here, finding cards which allow us to flex our Color Identity to the greatest extent, while using Nita to fill gaps in our gameplan. Speaking of which, while it's been brushed over thus far, let's not forget that Nita does in fact give us access to opposing cards, stealing Green ramp Spells, Blue on-stack interaction, etc. It's not especially efficient nor reactive, but you do get an expanded toolbox of options, especially in terms of removal. With knowledge of Commander players' preferences, Nita uses the common thread of players' enjoyment of cards like Beast Within or in-color tutors to get away with far less interaction in the 99 than we'd otherwise need.

Betrayal at House on the Mill

If the only Spells we're stealing are those opponents have already cast, that's a huge limit on our options, and without a dedicated spellslinger deck in your pod, you'll be running out of potential hits rather quickly. Therefore, on top of every other spinning plate thus far discussed...we're a mill deck. Mill isn't necessarily a win condition for Nita, though in testing it came up a couple of times, by way of opponents already churning through cards. Still, in lacking Blue, there's not a wealth of options available to us at efficient price points; some of the best options are cards you'd immediately think of, like Mesmeric Orb, or Altar of the Brood, as means of seeing more cards from opposing Libraries in our hunt for worthy pieces to pickpocket. Mindcrank allows us to translate the power on our board (often supplemented by Nita's +1/+1 counters) into mill, and as an added bonus pairs with Bloodchief Ascension for one of the deck's only obvious combos. The mill portion of this strategy also includes two of the coolest inclusions within the context of the deck, being Feral Ghoul & especially Spoils of Evil. You've likely seen Feral Ghoul, debuting in the Mothman precon, but here it doesn't just act as a great counter sink for Nita, nor merely good sacrifice fodder—we're flexing those Rad counters to mill opponents into cards we can steal. Spoils meanwhile loads us up on Colorless mana once a player's been suitably milled, counting two card types we're not actually ever exiling. That Colorless mana works double duty for us, given Nita allows us to spend it on any stolen Spells, regardless of how rigorous their mana costs might otherwise be.

The other portion of our mill package is held within our manabase, shockingly, by way of the Locus Lands. We've covered that package before on Commander Focus, but here it's being used for two main reasons: Colorless mana is as useful to us as White or Black, most of the time, and in a pinch, we can dig for steal-able options via Trenchpost. This is a contentious package admittedly, as it's rarely efficient to jam mana into Trenchpost over and over...until you consider the fact that it's an activated ability, and we can therefore funnel our Trilobite counters into it. Furthermore, being in Orzhov, we get to play Weathered Wayfarer to find these pieces (or indeed the redundant Land combo of Cabal Coffers + Urborg, Tomb of Yawgmoth), which is itself a 1 mana Creature. The maps too, both Expedition & World, make an appearance, as well as the everpresent Tezzeret, Cruel Captain. That said, while I have a tendency to squeeze every last drop of efficiency from my manabase, this is an ancillary component to the list; you could cut away to just Urborg + Coffers and have a great time.

Our Wincon, Comrade

Imagine we were called 'Crankhammer'.

Balancing the ratio of theft to counter payoffs, to those which enable Nita's mana intensive gameplan, is far from an easy thing. Nita asks quite a lot of you, and is a Commander you're not often happy to slam as soon as you have the mana to cast her. She's a capstone to a gameplan that's already going, the accelerant and glue to those disparate synergy pieces which make up the 99. As mentioned, the only 'hard combo' in this deck is Mindcrank + Ascension, and mill is rarely a viable path to victory, so what are we doing to win? The answer, funny enough, is more often the question we ask to our opponents. Using cards like Praetor's Grasp & Apple of Eden, Isu Relic, we can steal away combo pieces from opponents who look to be running them, and coupled with our ample mana and ability to nick tutors from the yard, our wins arise most often from coupling pieces we have, with those of our opponents. Not just that, but playing the table police while you put together play lines on the fly is a ton of fun—jamming one opponent's Heroic Intervention into anothers' Blasphemous Act isn't that far-fetched, and indeed was how one of my test games concluded. That said, if worst comes to worst, you can always exile a Saw in Half to your Hoarding Broodlord, and get both pieces of the Mindcrank line in one go; it still takes a few turns to come online, as this is ostensibly a high 2/low 3 in terms of Bracket, but accessing it is easy.

Example Decklist: Nita, Forum Moderator

Theft decks are a tough nut to crack, in terms of diving into card choices. So many of your best pieces, in things like Cruelclaw's Heist or Abstruse Appropriation, are only acceptable because of the mana rebate we can often get from Trilobite effects. Were we unable to offset the cost, in terms of their mana by way of translating Nita's counters to pay for them, I would be unimpressed. Stealing opposing cards tends to come at a premium, outside of the very best options, and on top of that you're effectively just 'drawing' cards you chose not to put in your 99. Nita makes that a bit better, in giving you a more surgical means of taking the best cards your opponents are playing, but charges you through the nose to to so. And yet, in actually playing this list, you feel incredibly clever. That sensation is one worth chasing, loading up your Seance Board to cast an opposing haymaker, all while politicking to double dip on the best opposing removal to deal with pairs of problems. There's a really cool core going on, with all the {X}{X} Creatures, but without the flexibility afforded by Nita, even compared to other Orzhov +1/+1 counter Commanders, it would feel lacking.

It's worth highlighting a few of the more generic (though impactful) cards we're on, given this is the first time I'm covering Orzhov/Silverquill on Commander Focus. Outside of a scant few scenarios, it's hard for me to think of a deck that doesn't play the pair of Cauldron Haze + Afterlife Insurance, in exactly these two colors. While the former has some added upside with Nita, in that she removes the -1/-1 counters, both make your board incredibly sticky when wipes abound.

Decklists are kept updated, and may change with set releases.



If you wanted to take Nita to a higher bracket, you could, but this list places her at a cool 'Bracket 2.5', the Game Changer-less B3. Indeed, if you did play with opponents that had Game Changers, she might feel even better, with you stealing a Jeska's Will or similar, and theft as an archetype in the format tends to scale well. After all, given every other card you're casting is one found in the opposing 99, the better those stacks are, the happier you'll tend to be. Still, in powering up Nita, you're forced to play more combo pieces, and likely lose some of the coolest aspects of her identity as a player-steered midrange pile. More often than not, you'll just pack enough combos that rely on each card type, as to make better use of the increased density of tutors across the board. While your power might scale well, your interest likely won't, and Nita could find herself in the position of the table police with answers in each graveyard. While you can get around parts of her cost, she remains inefficient in taking things all by herself, so a high B3/B4 Nita would feel more like 'Orzhov midrange-control that sometimes casts its Commander to duplicate opposing tutors'. If that strikes a chord with you, by all means, give it a go!

Trilobites the Dust

Leesha Hannigan keeps Trilobite weird, as it ought to be.

Nita is quite an interesting engine, at the end of the day. Yes, she asks plenty of you, but also provides the necessary colors to surmount those issues, and enables a really cool subtheme alongside Agatha's Soul Cauldron & the Trilobite-style effects. While you can fold to opponents disrupting...exactly that, by removing Soul Cauldron too early, you have the capacity to wield their own protection or recursion against your foes, even if it is all at Sorcery-speed. The pervasive worry that's interwoven itself throughout this article though, that comes from having tested this deck a fair bit; you can't exactly play a theft list against itself, so I saw firsthand how people were reacting to what I was doing, and how I was doing it. On the scale between Don Andres, the Renegade & Tergrid, Nita falls somewhere in the middle, leaning towards the former. You do rely on mill, finicky interactions, and touching opponents' cards...but to fairly interesting ends, and a payoff that reads at first to be extremely fair.

I'm of two minds in recommending Nita, because for those who already enjoy theft, she's a breath of fresh air with a ton of unexplored potential. For players not used to riffling through opposing yards a dozen times each turn, however, it can lead to definite decision paralysis, and with a lot of your mana generation being in the form of dice you're constantly rotating between 1, 2, & 'off the card', the amount of moving game pieces can really slow the game's pace. Nita isn't strong enough to make misplays something you can readily recover from, either, so taking deliberate steps throughout your turn (and only your turn) is also a factor; I almost think being available at Instant-speed would have not just made the card more interesting, but also led to better pace of play. Instead of taking a long personal turn, you'd in its place be considering your options while other people play, like a more 'normal' control pile. Regardless of how I personally feel about her design, though, Nita is a blast to crack open in terms of solving the puzzle behind all of her inputs & outputs, and wielding your opponent's Spells against them feels thrilling once you've gotten set up. Don't just take my word for it, give her a go!

Until next time, I Nita get out of here.
