Tabletop Battles Reviews: Carnage Unending, a Warhammer 40,000 Anthology

May 8th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Image credit: Games Workshop

One look at Warhammer Community recently will tell you that we're homing in on Warhammer 40,000's 11th Edition and a Return to Armageddon, and the Black Library has been doing its level best to follow suit. Books like The Freebooterz Code by Justin Woolley, an Illustrated and Annotated Edition of Nate Crowley's Ghazghkull Thraka: Prophet of the Waaagh!, and the Legends of the Waaagh! and Yarrick omnibuses are all pointing the way, and even the most recent anthology, last month's Carnage Unending, has signs aplenty.

Unlike the forthcoming World Ablaze anthology, these stories have all been printed before, but it makes for an eclectic mix that, while perhaps lacking an overt theme, nevertheless has a faint through-line that connects many of the selections, starting with the book's opener.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Ork Hunter, by Dan Abnett

It's not hard to conclude that Games Workshop has brought it's 'A-game' for this latest anthology, given that we're opening with a story by Dan Abnett. One of the Black Library's most storied and celebrated writers- at time of writing he's a ten-time inductee to the Black Library Readers' Hall of Fame, with undoubtedly more on the way- this story's inclusion is also a nifty bit of editing. Note the following:



"Word is Yarrick himself had returned," is exactly the kind of thing you'd expect to see in a new story as we're on the verge of 11th Edition and a return to Armageddon, with Old Bale Eye himself all over lore articles on WARCOM. But that's the thing- this story was first published in 2002. That's either a remarkable coincidence, cosmic serendipity- or inspired editing. Well done.

The story itself is terrific, with Abnett at the peak of his powers. It's a classic trope, told from the perspective of a group of fresh Imperial Guardsmen that have been seconded to another unit that's effectively "gone native." These "ork hunters" are virtually orks themselves, covered in grisly body-part trophies, but after a long deployment in the wilds have become incredibly skilled at doing the one thing that most needs doing: killing greenskins.

As the fresh soldiers fall in, they die easily until only one remains, the one who's learned to walk the path of the ork hunter. The story's a little truncated, but it's a strong opener.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Wulfen, by Chris Wraight

Another story from one of the Black Library's masters, this time it's Chris Wraight. This is another strong inclusion about the survivors of an ill-fated, reckless attack on a daemon world- and the mystery of what saved them. Twice.

Unlike the opener, Wraight's story isn't what I'd call "self-contained," in that if you aren't familiar with some of the lore around the Space Wolves (and, specifically, what the title is referring to) you might struggle to fully appreciate the story.

All the same, it's a very tight, evocative, and descriptive piece if fiction and another

Image credit: Games Workshop

The Glorious Tomb, by Guy Haley

We're right back to Armageddon here with this one from Guy Haley, being an account told in the first person of a Dreadnought of the Black Templars. The story's fine, but what electrifies here most is Haley's prose. He employs a very martial and driven cadence throughout with many passages having a blunt directness, such as this one:


There are thousands of orks here. I blow them to pieces, crush them underfoot, smash them down. So many of them die, die, die, but always there are more.

And this wonderful musing on the nature of his enemy:


Orks are remarkable creatures, a survivor race. I have fought them in swamps, forests, deserts, hives, snow, the sea and the void. They infest them all equally. Their success makes them all the more despicable. They are brutish, violent, inimical to all order and impervious to sense. I respect them and I hate them. I kill all the enemies of man with satisfaction, but I particularly enjoy killing orks. Praise be.

Given that this was originally written as an audio drama, I imagine this must sound terrific in verbal delivery and is going straight into my Audible queue.

Conceptually, Dreadnoughts have a kind of tragedy baked into their very existence, and therefore make for rich veins of poignancy and tragedy. We've seen this before, such as with Mike Vincent's The Vengeful Dead, or the legendary scene from James Swallow's The Flight of the Eisenstein between loyalist Death Guard Huron-Fal and Ullis Temeter ("We'll go together then, shall we?").

Brother Adelard's story here is no exception, and while I won't spoil the story the transition from his external focus to the internal one is terrific work from Haley; this one is a standout.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Packin' Heat, by Mike Brooks

Longtime readers may recall that I'm a bit ambivalent on Ork stories, since often the humor doesn't quite land with me. My first taste of Mike Brooks's Orks was when I reviewed last year's Blood of the Imperium (here), and I found Painboyz to be a hilarious delight. Packin' Heat, originally released as part of the 2022 Advent Calendar short story subscription (with a print appearance in 2024's Black Library Celebration mini-anthology), didn't quite rise to that height for me.

In this story, two grots (including Snaggi Littletoof from the novel Warboss) team up to sneak into an Aeldari ruin in search of lost treasure, only to find it's not as abandoned as they'd hoped. What always turns me off from Ork humor is when I feel the author is trying a little too hard to get the laughs. If Brooks overdid it here, though, it wasn't by much, and the moments of genuine mirth come when he is at his more subtle:


The heat got more intense the lower they went - which just went to show that Mek Zagblutz had been wrong about his claims that heat rose, but what could you expect from an ork?

Still, this one worked, and with the Armageddon-facing element of this anthology it's good to have some of the Ork perspective on display...

Image credit: Games Workshop

Road Rage, by Mike Brooks

...and on that note, why not another? This time Brooks spins a tale of a great Ork race, as Bad Moons Boss Ufthak Blackhawk must put some upstart Speed Freekz in their place by beating them at their own game.

This one boasts a number of characters from Brooks's main body of Ork work, such as the novels Brutal Kunnin' and Da Big Dakka. As a story, it's somewhat middle of the road as in, if you enjoy these storiees (and many do), then you'll enjoy this one as well. It didn't stand out for me, but Brooks writes well and it was another good look at greenskin culture.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Mad Dok, by Nate Crowley

Short stories in the main fall into one of two categories. Some stories are entirely self-contained, meaning that everything within them exists for the purposes of that story alone. The others are what you might call "add-on" stories, stories that have a certain continuity with other works in the Black Library. That's not always readily apparent; for instance, Mike Vincent's short story Blades of Atrocity would have appeared to be self-contained, up until the subsequent publication of the novel The Remnant Blade (review).

I found Mad Dok to be not only a good example of the latter camp, but an absolutely essential story on its own for anyone wanting a good understanding of the dynamic between Mad Dok Grotsnik and Ghazghkull Thraka. While I've been able to piece together much of it from having read Grotsnik: Da Mad Dok (review) and Ghazhgkull Thraka: Warlord of Warlords (review), Mad Dok puts that at the center of the story.

Well written, this one plays the Orks straight rather than chasing the silly. A terrific inclusion.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Blood Rite, by Rachel Harrison

Great news for Blood Angels fans, as tucked halfway through the anthology is a full novella featuring the sons of Sanguinius facing down Word Bearers on an Imperial shrine world.

A tale that's not quite enough for a novel but certainly more than a short story, Blood Rite is the story of a band of Blood Angels seeking to reclaim a sacred relic that's been twisted by the Ruinous Powers, a chalice that was a precious gift from Sanguinius himself. Switching across different points of view as the story progresses, the Blood Angels aren't only up against the Heretic Astartes corrupting the world through a profane ceremony, but the mounting pressures of the Black Rage and Red Thirst within them.

Harrison, perhaps best known for the character of Commissar Severina Raine, was most active in the Black Library between 2012 and 2019, with Blood Rite coming from the end of her tenure. There's a risk with dropping a novella in the middle of a short story anthology, in that it may be a poor fit for the otherwise brisk pace a collection of short stories provides. Not here. This is an excellent inclusion, and Harrisson achieves two feats here.

First, the story maintains a kinetic momentum without ever descending into feeling like bolter porn. There's plenty of action, but it doesn't drag the narrative down. The core mission drives the characters forward, and she hangs just enough introspection and reflection on it to give it depth without compromising the pacing.

Second, it never overstays its welcome. Employing point-of-view swaps and time shifts in the same novella risks trying to do too much, but Harrison wields a very deft pen here as she weaves it all together. And it works! This one is a real treat.

Image credit: Games Workshop

It Bleeds, by David Guymer

The World Eaters are another faction that has a long history on Armageddon, and David Guymer's contribution to the collection is an interesting one. The tale is told from the perspective of a World Eater legionary that actually had defected from a loyalist Successor Chapter, only when he went to have his butcher's nails installed- the cranial implant that amplifies rage and aggression that's de rigueur for the XIIth Legion- something went a little sideways.

It Bleeds is told from the first person, giving you insight into the precarious mental state of a Khornate berzerker as his past eventually catches up to him. It's well-told, striking the right resonant note of tragedy at the heart of so many Heretic Astartes while steering clear of apologia. Originally appearing in Chaos Space Marines Week in 2022, this is the second print anthology that's featured it after 2023's Galaxy of Horrors.

Image credit: Games Workshop

The Price of Morkai, by Marc Collins

Is it possible to have too much of a good thing? Every once in awhile the Black Library seems to saturate a short story's exposure, and this time around it's Mark Collins' prelude short to his terrific Krakenblood (review). Originally published in December 2023's Advent subscription, it found life in print in the Blood of the Imperium anthology in March 2025 before being added to the Krakenblood Special Editon that November. If that wasn't enough Morkai for you, it was then included in this year's Store Anniversary anthology Cannibal Gate and Other Stories, and then now here in Carnage Unending.

Whew!

At risk of sounding curmudgeonly, having the same short story appear in print anthologies two years running is...not ideal. And this is a good story, a reflective tale of a Wolf Priest returning the geneseed of a slain Space Wolf brother.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Consecrated Ground, by Steven B. Fischer

Where has Steven B. Fischer gone? Back when I reviewed Broken Crusade (my first-ever review for Tabletop Battles), I noted that Fischer's second Warhammer novel "remains perhaps the best treatment of faith I've found in a Warhammer book." It was also his last contribution to the Black Library, sadly.

While Consecrated Ground gives us more Castellan Emeric, being a short story we're not going to see a lot of existential ponderings here. Rather, we get a tight, focused short about the Black Templars being called to suppress a planetary uprising by its mutant population. Things aren't quite what they seem, however, and there's a danger in faith being a little bit too blind.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Hell Fist, by Justin Woolley

While not quite as ubiquitous as The Price of Morkai, Justin Woolley's tale of Catachans  taking on Orks in the jungle has been in three anthologies in four years starting with 2023's Galaxy of Horrors. It also appeared in last year's Death and Duty: An Astra Militarum Anthology, so it's another back-to-back inclusion. At least here it's a bit more thematically on-the-nose than Collins's Space Wolves short, providing a good symmetrical counterweight to the book's opening Abnett story, Ork Hunter.

Going by pure page count, the stories that lead up to the end of the book were a bit thinner than the earlier ones. That's only a problem if the story feels undercooked, and this one does end a little abruptly. It also- and I'll admit this is just a peeve of mine- has the main character understanding Orkish without acknowledging it in the text. In Leontus: Lord Solar (review), Rob Young also has his protagonist able to speak the Orks' tongue, but goes into the why of it.

All the same, this is a serviceable action story that gives us a good look at what we might expect in the upcoming Freebooterz Code.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Sarcophagus, by David Annandale

Just as the book opened with a bit of Yarrick (albeit in passing), so it also closes. This time, though, instead of a passing mention we get a story with the real deal, Ol' Bale Eye himself. The title Sarcophagus evokes the central event of this short, which sees Commissar Yarrick trapped inside a tank that's been blown upside down. With no way to get out and the unpleasant prospect of a death by thirst trapped and alone as the sole survivor, Yarrick must need to use every bit of his wits if he's to survive.

While not quite as old as Ork Hunter, this one's from 2014. With Commissar Yarrick back in the limelight, its as timely as ever. Like Hell Fist it's short, to the point, and a fun way to see out the collection.
