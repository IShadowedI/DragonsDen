Tabletop Battles Reviews: Kill Team Terror on Devlan Narrative Mission Pack

May 9th, 2026 via Goonhammer
For the first time, the Terror on Devlan box contains only one Kill Team, which might not seem like a great deal. It also contains the Red Terror, ten Termagants and a Ripper swarm. We get rules for using these models in Joint Ops games and even as a Kill Team, kind of. So does that make up for only getting one team in your FOMO box?

Thanks to Games Workshop for giving us a copy of Terror on Devlan to review. 

The Red Terror is kind of a classic B-movie horror monster. It provides a lot of classic tropes like jump scares, very messy deaths and a stubborn refusal to die, no matter what the protagonists do. 

The Joint Ops: Terror on Devlan Mission Pack

In the book we have a setting from early on in the days of Hive Fleet Kraken, when the Imperium barely knew the Tyranids. The Red Terror has hitched a lift to Devlan, where it’s eating up the defenders of the space port. This is hampering efforts to evacuate the planet ahead of the Tyranid advance. A Spectre Team is sent to remove the problem. 

Red Terror Ambush. Credit: NotThatHenryC

You can recreate these events, using the Volkus Killzone and the Spectres in the box. Alternatively, you can swap in a team (or two half teams) of your choosing to play through the campaign either solo or co-operatively.

There are also rules for using the Red Terror and nine Termagants as a Kill Team, though these are not intended for competitive play. You’ll see why that is as we see the Red Terror’s rules.

The Red Terror Operative (and the Rest)

In terms of Adversaries you have your ten Termagants and Ripper swarms, with identical rules to Typhon, plus the Red Terror. This is an interesting contrast with Typhon or Tomb World, both of which had rules for a lot more types of NPO. It’s good not to need any other models but there’s not a lot of variety here. 

Terror on Devlan NPOs. Credit: NotThatHenryC

It turns out this isn’t a big problem. You’ll mostly be too busy fighting the Red Terror to worry about what the other stuff is. In my experience of playing the campaign the Termagants performed their NPO role well, often dying to no effect but now and then spiking some damage through. They feel right for lesser bioforms outside the Hive Mind, which is the scenario we’re given.

Swiftblade: It's a very odd decision to have such a limited cast of NPO operatives at first blush, but as we go into the mission pack later it's clear that these scenarios are balanced with a pretty specific set of operatives in mind. While it would've been cool to see a wider variety of Tyranids to pick from, it would've been very easy to throw the balance of the missions way off.

The Red Terror is an extremely nasty operative. It has a relatively normal 2APL, 6” move and 4+ save but 30 wounds. It can “shoot” with its Pincer Tail, which has only a 3” range but full Seek, doing 4/5 damage with 4 shots on 3+. Of course it’s much more scary in melee with 6 dice hitting on 3+ for 4/6. In melee it has a Swallow Whole rule, letting it roll a D6 after landing a crit to try and beat the target’s remaining wounds. If it does, the enemy gets eaten right up and the Red Terror heals by the amount it rolled.

The Red Terror. Credit: NotThatHenryC

Two things make the Red Terror particularly unpleasant: it can activate multiple times per turn and it never dies. You activate NPOs in these Joint Ops missions by drawing cards and several of them make the Red Terror activate. It can potentially have multiple turns in a row and make a complete mess of your team. Other times you might end up drawing a load of Termagant activations while the Red Terror sits there being shot.

You might be quite pleased with yourself if you manage to shoot all thirty wounds off the Red Terror, but beware, the thing is not finished yet. The Red Terror isn’t Incapacitated. Instead it switches to Lurk behaviour, then disappears as soon as it can get more than 5” from your operatives or into its drop zone. 

You can keep the Red Terror on the surface by charging it so it has to Fall Back, which may be a good idea to stop it regenerating and eating more of your operatives. If you can cause it thirty wounds while it’s in Lurk you can add a Regeneration token, which will delay its re-emergence and may help with mission objectives.

If you’ve got Typhon then maybe you could mix in some Hormagaunts, or even some of the other stuff if you want more of a challenge. The Red Terror is no joke though, so this may not be needed.

Swiftblade: The Red Terror is a scary operative to be sure, but not as scary as you'd think. While Swallow Whole is a very strong special rule, and pretty much game over for anything that's not a Space Marine, its the only special rule that it has in combat. No Lethal 5+, no Rending, and really shockingly no brutal. Plus, there's no way for the Red Terror to get rerolls here as an NPO. So while yes, statistically you're very likely to get one crit off of six dice, if the Red Terror spikes down on it's rolls even a little bit it can't pull off its most powerful ability or bail itself out of a bad fight. Its still six attacks with 4/6 damage, the Red Terror can still shred, but for beefy elite teams or even dedicated combat midrange teams like Blades of Khaine or Goremongers its not totally unthinkable to walk off a fight with the Red Terror, especially if you charge and initiate the fight so you can parry out the one crit the Red Terror rolls on average. And the Brood Brothers Patriarch just eats this guys lunch.

Mileage may vary on charging the Red Terror, of course. Even with okay math, you're very prone to just getting eaten anyways.

Assorted Tyranid NPOs. Credit: NotThatHenryC

The Joint Ops Campaign

This is a full campaign involving nine missions, linked in a campaign structure. This is the most missions we’ve had so far for Joint Ops and it’s great that they’re put together in a campaign like this. The result is a kind of “choose your own adventure” campaign.

The structure of the campaign is that you play the first three missions in order, regardless of their outcome. This section therefore forms a sort of consequence-free prelude where it doesn’t matter what happens. That’s ideal if you’re using a team for the first time, or perhaps introducing someone to Kill Team.

From the fourth mission, where you get close to the Red Terror’s Lair, things get more serious. The results of each mission from here dictate what you’ll do next, and the ultimate success or failure of the campaign. If you keep winning you’ll have a chance to kill the Red Terror permanently… probably. If you fail an earlier mission, or fail to kill the Red Terror, you can try a mission to just blow the whole area to dust in the hope of finishing it off. Fail that, or do badly earlier, and you fight a Last Stand to try and buy as much time as possible for the evacuation.

Campaign Report

As Spectre Squad are the team in the box I decided to use them as my protagonists here to begin with. They got off to a rough start in the first mission when Termagants spawned all over the previously empty killzone after most of the team had moved on TP1. They took fire from all directions and couldn’t really respond till the following turn, since most of the team was on Conceal. I did manage to get a third of the team off the other side of the board, but only just. Not an auspicious start!

In mission two: On the Trail, you have to blow up three tunnel entrances, which are guarded by Termagants… and something worse. This went better, except that I lost my grenadier and heavy gunner to hot rolls from Termagants. The Red Terror made a brief appearance but my melta gunner managed to see it off. She shot it in her activation and then again when it charged, thanks to the Veteran Sergeant’s orders.

Deathwatch vs Red Terror. Credit: NotThatHenryC

These games took quite a long time. I got my Black Templar Deathwatch team out so I could see how these missions would play with a low-activation count elite team. Mission three: Know your foe, turned out to be very easy for the DW, and certainly quick. The Red Terror is now being a bit more aggressive and it activates a lot. In fact I had a weird few activations where I had a Ready Termagant so I had to draw a new card, but that card told me to either activate the Red Terror or spawn a new Termagant. Then I had two Ready Termagants instead of one, and the end of the TP was even further off! My plasma gunner was eaten but the other four guys were able to escape easily, having scanned the Red Terror. 

I sent the Deathwatch off again to mission 4: the Lair of the Beast. If anything they managed this even more easily, losing no operatives and sprinting through to finish in just three TPs. My gunner had another unpleasant encounter with the Red Terror but lived on one wound, fell back and shot it enough to make it Lurk. Again it helped to not have many activations as my guys could effectively run 10-12” per turn, thanks to the counteracts they all got.

. Credit: NotThatHenryC

I’ve recently put together a Celestian Insidiant team, made out of Sisters of Silence. They’re still in a bit of a WIP state but I thought I’d give them a go. The Sisters first took on mission 5: Flushed out. In this they had to find the Red Terror and take it out four times. The good news was that you could search the board methodically to check drop zones where Termagants were hidden, killing them off before dealing with the Red Terror. It got a bit intense when the Red Terror did appear but it has a rule in this mission to lose an APL if it emerges near one of your operatives. This slowed it, meaning I was able to grind through its wounds and take it out.

This meant I went on to mission 7: Execution. I sent an Inspiring Cremator off to locate the beast and light it up, which she kind of did, inflicting 3 damage and making my Mortisanctus Inspiring. Then the Red Terror went absolutely crazy and ate five of my operatives in a single turn! I was chipping damage off it but quite a lot of that was healed back when it swallowed my operatives. It only stopped when it had eaten all the Sisters on the left side of the board, leaving my Superior, a Warrior, the Denuncia and the Abjuror on the other side, looking very worried.

Sisters of Silence vs Red Terror. Credit: NotThatHenryC

The second turn went a bit better. I’d made my Leader and the Warrior Ceaseless and they were able to shoot some more wounds off the beast. A krak grenade from the Abjuror did 13 damage and finally it went down. In this mission the Red Terror loses some of its permanent wound stat every time it drops to zero and things got much easier after this. It came up and had some less good rolls against my Denuncia, who was then able to disengage. The next card was a Termagant, which didn’t achieve anything, and then the Red Terror dropped again, now going down to just 12 wounds. It came up, ate the Denuncia but took some damage, went to 6 wounds and was then finally finished off by the Superior when it next emerged.

This left three Sisters facing down ten Termagants, which promptly fled. A glorious campaign victory! I definitely didn’t expect to win after the first Turning Point so the Red Terror certainly went out fighting.

Swiftblade: Time's been a bit of a luxury in my household as of late, between a toddler doing toddler things and dealing with the joys of home ownership after a leak in the attic plus a hailstorm a few days later. But, I did manage to get my Murderwing on the table to run Mission 4, as it was the one that jumped out at me as the most thematic if I can only run one.

I did feel some heat pretty early on in the mission, as I tried to get cute with my Curseclaw to shoot a Termagant and failed to do any damage, leaving him exposed on vantage to get shot by all the other Termagants with an itchy trigger claw. It didn't help either that My Chaos Lord's first search for a potential lair had him take four damage, followed by a truly abysmal roll at the second search against a Ripper Swarm. This Chaos Lord, one of the deadliest combatants in all of Kill Team, took six damage from the Ripper Swarm while only dealing five back. Cursed.

Despite these setbacks, once I got to the business of  clearing out Termagants in earnest they went down pretty quickly, and thanks to Long Forgotten Honor my Champion was able to bravely run away from the Red Terror's first ambush attempt. After softening the Red Terror up with a melta gun blast, the Champion was able to close in and finish the job, clearing out the Red Terror's wounds and forcing him into Lurk. After that, I was able to search what remained of the objectives and capture the lairs I needed before the Red Terror had a chance to return to Hunt and charge again.

My impression of the campaign after playing this scenario and reading through the other rules is that these missions are really creative and a fun way to play Kill Team, but this campaign is best enjoyed by leaving the Space Marines at home. To echo what NotThatHenryC said above, Space Marine teams are buff enough to plow through the Termagants and even go toe to toe with the Red Terror, which makes this legendary monster of Warhammer lore feel more like the Red Inconvenience than a terror. Midrange or horde teams feel like the way to go here to really maximize the slasher movie killer vibes that makes these missions shine.

Honestly, its not a bad thing that certain team types are more fun and thematic for this campaign than others. There's probably some good design space there for the future, even. I would just make sure to be aware of the potential imbalance that elite teams bring to this campaign, and set your expectations accordingly.

The Red Terror Kill Team

Unexpectedly (for me at least), there’s an Appendix in the book letting you run The Red Terror and nine Termagants as a Kill Team. The rules say we shouldn’t use this team competitively, or in narrative missions where the Red Terror would not be appropriate. They also note that some maps might need to be adapted for the Red Terror’s 100mm base. Volkus should mostly be ok but Tomb World could be a problem.

The team actually uses the 13 random activation cards that come with the box - though obviously you can substitute any other playing cards. When you draw cards 1-5 you activate a Termagant or Ripper Swarm, 6-8 is two Termagants in succession and 9-13 is the Red Terror. This means the Red Terror can and will activate multiple times each TP. You actually don’t know how many activations you’ll have as you’ll carry on drawing cards so long as you have anyone Ready, potentially activating the expended Red Terror and leaving your Termagant still Ready.

Spectre Squad vs The Red Terror. Credit: NotThatHenryC

Unusually, your operatives are only allowed to do the actions on their cards - plus mission actions so you can open doors and do the Crit Op. Termagants can Fall Back, Shoot, Reposition or Dash but they cannot Charge or Fight, for example. This is weird, but represents that the Hive Mind isn’t here and these little guys are having to follow their instincts. Also note that the Red Terror in Lurk mode can’t Shoot or Fight, so you’ll want to have it escape and regenerate. Watch out for the enemy trapping it in a corner where it can’t escape.

There’s no equipment for the team and they don’t even pick generic stuff. They do get a full set of Strategic and Firefight Ploys, however. They’re allowed to pick Seek and Destroy Tac Ops if playing Approved Ops.

Two of the Strat ploys let you spawn a new Termagant in your Drop Zone or a Ripper Swarm anywhere in your Territory that it can be placed - including in control range of an enemy. Another gives your Termagants Balanced within 6”, which is quite good, while another lets them reroll a save if on the KZ floor and >3” away - which isn’t very good as they only have a 5+.

Firefight Ploys mostly benefit the Red Terror, except one that lets you discard an activation card and draw another. You can have the Red Terror fight twice during an Activation - though it still only has 2 APL. You can increase the range of its Pincer Tail to 6”, but lose Seek. And if it kills an operative in melee you can have every enemy within 3” of it make a stun test, losing an APL on a 3+, which is great for preventing 2APL enemies from falling back.

This is all completely mad, but could be fun to do now and then. It’s certainly a good decision not to allow it in tournaments. The Red Terror gets around a lot of the things a player might use to block a melee operative, simply moving stuff like razor wire and even operatives out of its way as it rampages about.

Final Thoughts

If you like narrative games, and especially if you enjoy monster movies, this is great fun. The Red Terror works really well as an adversary - far better than the Nightbringer did.

The Red Terror. Credit: NotThatHenryC

I wasn’t sure about this box at first. You have only one Kill Team and pay quite a lot for it. To be honest, if you just want to play competitive Kill Team, you can probably proxy a Spectre Squad without needing this box. Unusually, Games Workshop have also now posted all these rules on Warhammer Community, before the box even went on preorder, so you can start playing straight away if you want.

But most of us don’t just play competitively. Fighting the thing is not fun at all for operatives but you’ll have a great challenge from it that will make a refreshing change from playing approved ops on the same boards all the time. Overall Terror on Devlan is a great product for Kill Team and I recommend it.

Swiftblade: Agreed, Terror on Devlan is a great box for Kill Team fans, and the campaign here is the best one they've put out so far. The theming is great, the missions are creative, and the fact that there's a branching path based on how the scenarios go is well designed and makes for excellent storytelling. I'd love to see Games Workshop expand joint ops out in the future with more dedicated campaigns like this, especially through something like White Dwarf.
