How To Paint Everything - Sudden Conflict

May 2nd, 2026 via Goonhammer
This article is part of a series of sponsored articles from Sudden Conflict.

We’ve talked about Sudden Conflict before. It’s a cool 3v3 skirmish game that has just been released on Gamefound. Tabletop Battles has been lucky enough to receive review copies of each of the release boxes and after some time spent in the Paint Mines (No, I'm wearing that leg shackle entirely of my own free will. It's called accessorising, Brad. You should try it sometime.) we’ve come up with a guide on How To Paint Everything for Sudden Conflict.

Choosing Colour Schemes for Factions in Sudden Conflict

Coming into this game I really wanted to sell the idea that these were three separate universes coming together and that meant coming up with three visually distinct styles that matched each of the universes I was painting models for. It was a fun little exercise in trying to find three visually distinct painting styles/schemes. Read on as we explore a few different ways to compose paint schemes for your models.

Ukyo - Metallics and Natural Colours



For the Ukyo setting, I really wanted to lean into natural colours, like low saturation browns and greens along with similar basing to help communicate that this is an agrarian society (and ties in nicely with the idea that the characters are all animals. In order to differentiate between one faction and the other, I had the Emerald Masters use green trim (Warp Lightning green Contrast) on their robes and armour while Heaven's Liberators got that lovely rich blue (this is actually Mystic Blue, a Vallejo Xpress Color and not a GW Contrast but Talassar Blue would also suffice) and orange (I say orange because it was the Gryph-Hound Orange contrast paint, even though it came out more as a rich brown). Both factions got a bit of metallic paints on them but I wanted to use a rich red-ish gold on the Emerald Masters as a nice complement to the green robes while Heaven's Liberators got less metallics (and whatever they got was silver, a less royal/rich metal than gold).

For basing, I wanted to go with the classic 'brown dirt' and 'green grass', it's natural and doesn't clash with the colours used on the models themselves.

Step by Step: Ophella the Revered

Ophella Step-By-Step, Sudden Conflict

Stage 1: Just the usual zenithal undercoat, you'll get used to me starting each process this way. A zenithal undercoat is, for the newly initiated: Using a rattlecan to undercoat the entire model with black, then using 1-2 layers grey/white applied via airbrush or spraycan but angled from above, so that the model has a natural progression from light to dark. This is an excellent starting point for Contrast paints to give them even more depth.

Stage 2: Base coats all round! Creed Camo green Contrast for the skin, Snakebite Leather Contrast for the rear shell. It's worth noting that I feathered the Snakebite leather across the shell, so that the bottom/rear was a rich and dark brown while the top was quite light. Cork-Brown (Vallejo 70.843) was used to base coat the 'Belly-Shell'. Gold can be applied with any metallic gold you like. The Jade used was the exquisite Vallejo Game Color, Jade Green 72.026.

Stage 3: Highlights mostly. The flat colours were generally just highlighted with a blend of their original colour and a dash of white. The gold was highlighted with a 50-50 blend of Gold and Mithril Silver (or equivalent), while the skin was highlighted with Vallejo's Heavy Grey (72.145) and Cream White (70.766), while the shell whorls (What a good word whorls is. Just keeping saying it and see what happens: Whorls. Whorls. Whorls.) are highlighted with a successive blend of Cork-Brown and Cream White.

Stage 4: An additional application of even lighter highlights is applied, a bit of red wash for the gold and the gemstone in the staff is quickly painted, having neglected it entirely until this stage of painting. The gemstone is just any dark green highlighted with increasingly brighter mixes of the original green mixed with a yellow with a strong pigment count/opacity.

Painting Tortoise Shell Patterns

I really loved the texture of Ophalla's shell and was looking forward to painting it up in a way that really shells how shiny and intricate Tortoise shells are.



The trick here was to, as noted above, apply the Snakebite Contrast base coat in a manner that gives it a gradient effect running from top to bottom. Then a few stages of highlights, paying attention to ensuring that the ridges/whorls (there's that word again) of the shell that are more likely to catch light from above are given stronger highlights.

Galactic Throne - Blending Contrasts



Where the other universes got unified low colour saturation palettes, I wanted the characters of Galactic Throne to have a lot of variety between them. This meant that their bases needed to be very neutral, lest they add more 'noise' to the models and make them look busy. Medium-tone grey also serves as a cute 'Moon Rock' basing scheme, which helps sell the Space/Science Fiction side of the setting.

Step by Step: Ula the Imprisoned Empath

I was excited to try using contrast paints to blend two 'cool' colours (blue and purple) together into some sort of weird and unnatural gradient, I tested the technique with Countessa Bly and then recorded the WIP images while painting Ula.

Ula Step-by-Step, Sudden Conflict

Stage 1: Just the usual zenithal undercoat, though worth noting that I gave Ula a little bit of a drybrush with pure white to help the highlights pop when I apply the Contrast paints.

Stage 2: Using Luxion Purple Contrast paint I did half the robes, trying to apply the paint so that the lower elements of the dress got a darker, deeper coat, while 'feathering' the paint near the centre of the model to try and thin it out and provide less coverage.

Feathering (at least my interpretation of it) is the act of using the paintbrush to 'draw' paint from where it has landed on the model and stretch it across new, unpainted areas. This takes pigment of a standard density/coverage, and stretches it across a larger area, hopefully making the paint more translucent/weaker. Where you are using a second paintbrush, loaded with water and not paint to perform the feathering, this is often called 'Two Brush Blending' (or Wet Blending). Ula's headpiece (hair?) and the tentacles behind her are done with Doomfire Magenta.

Her skin is done in grey-tones, as I wanted her skin to be bland and low saturation compared to her colourful dress and hair (...headpiece?). Grey tone skins are done by starting with a blend of 50-50 flat grey (usually a mid-tone like ...) and a skin tone (ie. something with a bit of ruddy pink in it, like Cadian Fleshtone, though I use Army Painter Tanned Flesh here myself.), then success layers/highlights are applied with a greater and greater ratio of skin tone to grey while also adding in (at least, the way I do it) small amounts of cream (Vallejo's 70.766 Cream White)  to add some warmth to the otherwise pallid skin.

The base is black washed to build contrast. Next stage!

Stage 3: The primary intent of this stage is to apply and then feather the blue across the middle of the model, so that the lower coverage blue and purple and blend a bit into each other and create a cool gradient effect. Just repeat what you did with Luxion Purple but working downwards with Talassar Blue. Then you highlight each respective colour, again going for a sort of graduated effect, with the strongest highlights and the top and bottom of the model respectively and getting gradually weaker and finally non-existent where they meet in the middle of the model.

A drybrush of a light grey is used to help build up the base, while the rims are painted black.

The Doomfire Magenta tentacles and hair are highlighted with Game Color's Pale Flesh (72.003) and then washed with a generic red wash to help blend it in with the Magenta. Eyes are painted white in anticipation of the next stage...

Stage 4: This stage is just about finalising your highlights across the model, making sure those grey skin-tones build up to near-white, building up the hair through orange and yellow, as well as a cheeky dab of Fluorescent Green (Vallejo 70.737) for the eyes.

Object Source Lighting (OSL): Heather Sing, the Space Ranger

While I was inconsistent with the rest of the models and didn't do OSL with anyone else, I felt like it was worth exploring what it might look like to have a character bathed in some sort of unnatural, alien light (whether it's a star going supernova, an exotic super-weapon exploding or even the Infinity Matrix itself, it doesn't really matter).

Sudden Conflict - Heather Sing, Space Ranger (Photo courtesy of Musterkrux)

OSL is all about providing targeted highlights that indicate there's a novel light source affecting the model. Is this case, it's a blue light that appear to be radiating from Heather Sing's front-left (2 O'clock as a reference if that helps). There are a few considerations to take to tune up your OSL technique:

1. Graduated blending. The blue of the OSL effect here isn't a solid 'highlight' that sits across the entire model, there's an angle of incoming light (angle of incidence for the physicists) where the reflected light is strongest on the model, but as you depart from that angle, the OSL effect gets weaker. I usually communicate this by blending the light colour (in this case blue) with the primary colour of the material (for heather's pants, mid-tone green) in increasingly weaker ratios.

2.  Shadows. Strong lighting effects generate shadows. Any part of the model that is occluded/blocked from the light source is darker, so often gets a black wash from me. You can't quite see it but I've even used black wash to paint out a shadow for Heather that plays across the base behind her legs.

3. Light diffusion. Different materials reflect light in different ways. You'll see the cloth/leather pants have a more gradual/weaker reflection than Heather Sing's metal armour, which is so shiny and reflective that it even has several 'near white' points of perfect reflection.

4. Selling the Scene with your Scenic Base. You'll see that the base also has some directed OSL to help keep the impression of a light source consistent. Textured bases are really good for helping you show exactly where the light is coming from. A cheeky drybrush (or even a wet-brush) across the texture of the base from a single angle of attack is an easy way to build up the effect.

Valrona - Sin City Styles



Saving the best for last, we have Valrona. When I first saw them I knew I wanted to paint them in Black and White with each model having one element painted in bright, high saturation red to draw attention to it. Readers may recall Frank Miller's 'Sin City', which was the inspiration of this scheme. Having committed to the bit, my next puzzle to solve was how I was going to do the basing. Thankfully, it didn't take long to decide that doing marble tiling, in a chequered pattern, would be a perfect way to keep the black and white motif going. Huzzah.

Painting in Black and White

The theory that I work with when painting in black and white is that I want to make sure I am building at least two, if not three, types of 'contrast':
  1. Contrast between elements. That is to say, visually identifiable elements within the model, such as the hair and face. These two elements are adjacent and if you painted them in a sufficiently similar tone of grey it would be hard to distinguish between them, so it's important that 'adjacent elements' have a sufficiently different 'base tone' to each other.
  2. Contrast within elements: This is just another way of describing highlighting and shading/washing. You don't want your individual elements to be flat colours, so you need to add some visually interesting brushwork to those elements. Here, my emphasis is on avoiding turning two visually distinct but adjacent elements into a hot mess of jumbled paint strokes of different tones of grey as well as trying to define and communicate a coherent 'light source' for the highlights and shades.
  3. Texture contrast: This is somewhat a blend of the first two concepts of contrast (try and say that three times quickly). Your technique for painting detail within an element can be used to communicate a texture (rough leather, shining metal, smooth cloth/skin or even a fur coat) that is different ot the texture of the adjacent element.

Using Trompe-l'œil to paint cracks in marble floors.

If you take a quick look at Krandus' base you'll see that his allocated space on the marble dance floor has seen better days. This technique of communicating a 3D-space using a flat 2D surface (ie. whether it be canvas, a panel of armour or a flat base on a model), this is called Trompe-l'œil (Some people pronounce it: 'Tromp Loy' but I won't embarrass myself trying to write the French pronunciation phonetically).

For these Sudden Conflict models battling on a marble ballroom floor, I wanted to use a little bit of this technique to add visual interest to what was otherwise just black and white checks on the base. So, I painted some cracks and chips into the floor here and there. If you look at the diagram below you'll see my step-by-step process to painting Trompe-l'œil.

Trompe-l'œil demonstration

Moving left to right in the image above, there are four stages to painting a crack/chip using this technique.

Stage 1: Provide a basic outline of your crack or shatter-pattern. Usually, these start at a single point with a thick 'crack' which then splinters (or forks) away into smaller and smaller cracks. These are usually done at acute angles (ie. less than 90 degrees) but that's not a hard and fast rule and I'm sure some clever materials engineer or mathematician and show us why (Editor: Don't. Don't show us why, we won't read your proof and we sure as heck won't be publishing it either.).

Stage 2: Splinter out some more cracks, each 'fork' getting smaller and thinner as it moves away from the impact-point/origin.

Stage 3: Start adding light grey 'highlights' to the outer edges of the cracks, usually on the 'opposite' sides from where your perceived light source is coming from. In the example above, imagine the light source is coming from the South-West of the image (or 7 o'clock if that's a reference that makes sense to you).

Stage 4: Finally, add some white (or near-white) dots where primary/artery cracks meet each other (or separate, depending on which way you look at it).

Sidenote: 'True' (and I use the term lightly) Trompe-l'œil in a miniature painting context often replaces the initial black cracks of Stages 1-2 with a dark brown (or darker shade of the original material) and then you repeat Stage 3 using Black (but on the opposite sides of the cracks). I did not do this for the Black-and-White models of Valrona because I didn't want to introduce brown into the palette (for obvious reasons) and while I probably could have used a dark grey instead, I didn't. Because reasons.

Step by Step: Krandus Moore

Krandus was the last model I painted for the Valrona set and because I was so excited to try out the Sin City Styles (henceforth SCS) on the other models in the set, I rushed through them and was left with him to make sure I got my Work In Progress photos at the end of the day.

Krandus Step-By-Step, Sudden Conflict

Stage 1: Surprise! Another zenithal undercoat.

Stage 2: Block in your primary colours. As discussed, try to make sure your adjacent elements have different base tones to differentiate between them. I also use this step to draft in the square tiles for the base. You can be a smidge messy and imperfect here as you can clean up the lines in later stages of the process.

Stage 3: Mostly this is just highlighting. Each tone of grey is highlighted by 1-2 steps of lighter grey. Worth noting that the boots, head and hair are starting to communicate a directional light source (Krandus' left, the viewers right). You'll see that a fair bit of attention is put into the highlights on his face (see section below), as a focal point for the model this is a good place to concentrate your efforts. You'll also note I leave the eyes without pupils, as I think the risk of mucking them up and making Krandus look goofy isn't worth it. Block in the colours on the marble tiles and proceed to the next stage.

Stage 4: A little bit of black wash helps change the tone of some of the greys as well as build within-element contrast (this is partly why his tunic seems to change tone between stages). We also finalise any highlights, do the Trompe-l'œil on the base, as well as a cheeky bit of Non-Metallic Metal (in grey tones, no less!) for the sword, as well as adding that delicious Sin-City garnish of bright red blood dripping off his sword and splashing on to the tiles below (Note: less is more with the red elements of these models). It's not immediately obvious from the images but I tried to make it look like the blood was running along the cracks, as well. Hell yeah.

Final Thoughts

The beauty of a game like this is that you can mix up your painting techniques and themes not only between boxes/sets but between factions within those sets. I've really enjoyed painting these models and seeing them lined up on the game board is an absolute delight (I'm the sort of dweeb that, once I finish painting something, will set it up on a table and just look at it for hours.). *Chef Kiss*. Well, I guess that's it for now.

Now it's your turn, people. Pick up a brush and get experimenting!

Sudden Conflict is now available on Gamefound, with three core sets available for pledge (Ukyo, Valrona, and the Galactic Throne) as well as two Character Expansion packs (Solaris and the Exiled).
