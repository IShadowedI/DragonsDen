Tabletop Battles Reviews: Ghost Legion by Mike Brooks

May 1st, 2026 via Goonhammer
Image credit: Games Workshop

Ghost Legion is not a book about the Alpha Legion. The Alpha Legion are not real. Solomon Akurra, the bearer of the Pale Spear, is a myth invented by seditious elements of Imperial society and you must not listen to their filthy lies, citizen. If there was a world called Sertra in the Psyphos Sub-sector, it would be an incredibly productive planet filled with loyal and pious citizens of the Imperium. However, this planet does not exist. This means that any rumours you may hear about traitor elements ruling this planet must, by the nature of the fact that this world is not a real place, be false. People who do not have your best interests in mind will tell you to buy the Ghost Legion novel (a book that, I must stress, does not exist) and they will tell you it is a good read. Please report any unsanctioned behaviour you observe in others to your nearest confessor and then report to your local Arbites Precinct for processing.

Full disclosure, I saved up all of my Big Boy allowance to pre-order this book on release, consumed it in short order and then was promptly distracted from writing this review by that terrible experience we call ‘Life’. Zero out of Ten, do not recommend. This book, though? Do recommend.

Credit: Robert "TheChirurgeon" Jones

Plot Overview

It’s not spoilers if it’s the first page, but the book opens with an execution order signed by the High Lords of Terra for Solomon Akurra. You know you’ve made it into the big leagues when the High Lords of Terra want you dead. Is this foreshadowing? Perhaps.

The next five pages (again, doesn’t count for spoilers if it’s something you read in the first five minutes of opening the book) sets a blistering pace for the plot, with an Inquisitor returning from the first novel and a pretty significant reveal about their backstory and intentions moving forwards. Further foreshadowing. This sets the book up with several threats all angling towards our (beloved?) protagonist from the very start.

After the events described in the book Harrowmaster, Akurra is now functionally in charge of an ex-Imperial planet, if not entire sub-system. This is a wildly unfamiliar state of affairs for your typical marauding Chaos Warlord, who is inclined to tear down the walls of civilisation and move on to their next conquest rather than turning around and rebuilding society after they’ve won.

Akurra has a few minor problems to unpack as he goes about preparing for the Imperium’s response. The Imperium simply cannot abide a seemingly viable alternative to their political philosophy and so the Hammer of the Emperor must inevitably fall. And fall it does.

Meanwhile, he’s negotiating with a nearby Adeptus Mechanicus Forge complex. He needs their materiel and they’ll need his protection when the Imperium returns and assumes that anyone who didn’t die resisting the Alpha Legion occupation is a collaborator (and we all know what the Imperium’s default punishment for collaboration is. Hint: it’s going to be some form of execution.). However, in order to remain confident in their loyalty he sets the Diabolicus Secundus (an Abominable Intelligence) to sniff around their databanks and see what secrets it can find (Another hint: Many.).

The Imperial Fleet arrives and the balance of forces sent to retake Sertra far exceeds the numbers they were predicted to muster. Curious. What’s the Imperium’s interest in Sertra? As his Fleetmaster, Krozier Va’kai, works (dark) magic fending off a massive invading fleet, Solomon must prosecute a ground war on several fronts while also managing the politics of the various warbands of the Ghost Legion. Should someone see advantage in it, they would seize the Pale Spear for themselves and take command of the Alpha Legion forces in the region. Akurra knows it, and so does everyone else around him; Akurra leads for only so long as he is fit to do so, this is the (Alpha Legion) way. If that wasn’t all enough to keep our patient, peart and poised protagonist busy, do you perhaps remember the foreshadowing(-s, plural)? Yup, there’s always more complications…

"Objective secured, we hold the sacred text." (Photo credit: Musterkrux)

Character Development

My complaint with Akurra’s characterisation in the first book (Harrowmaster) was that he didn’t really have any personality outside of being the Sensible Marine in the Room. Which is, perhaps, a departure from the average Chaos Space Marine protagonist, but is it enough to carry a story? I don’t recall him having any character flaws and arguably he only made one significant mistake, a mistake that he was able to adapt to and reverse relatively quickly. Does this remain true in Ghost Legion? Honestly? No, I think author Mike Brooks has done a good job in adding some depth to Akurra during the course of this book.

The first issue you have to contend with when trying to write a book about the Alpha Legion is trying to avoid the Plans-within-Plans trap (Also known as the ‘All According to Keikaku’ gambit). You want there to be some big reveal at the end, demonstrating that the protagonist had stacked the deck but you need to walk a delicate balance between setting up the clues/pieces without making it obvious and a sudden or unconvincing Deus Ex Machina. I think the book does a good job of balancing this tension.

The story helps sell Solomon’s strategic acumen by giving him something like three intersecting plots/threats to deal with. Almost every scene Solomon is in, he’s dealing with an immediate problem while also planning his next move with respect to a future problem. By giving him a pair of confidants to argue with, the mortal sorceress Tulva Dyne and his Astartes comrade Kyrin Gadraen, the reader learns what Akurra’s plans are and how he comes to some of his conclusions. It’s a very nice pair of dynamics, as both Tulva and Kyrin have their own perspectives that influence Akurra’s plans (well, somewhat…he is a very stubborn Space Marine some days). These relationships also introduce several conflicts that Solomon needs to try and resolve while also coordinating the defence of an entire planet. Like the other BLGs (Black Library Greats), Brooks ensures that these personal relationships and their associated conflicts get as much spotlight as the bolter-violence. What I really enjoyed about the book was how Mike Brooks played with the common trope of ‘What is the price of victory for the protagonist’ and turned it into: ‘What price will Solomon’s allies have to pay for his victory?’. Solomon Akurra makes mistakes, he gets outplayed, ambushed and his ambition overreaches on occasion. Each time this happens someone else gets left holding the bag. It’s fascinating to watch him ignoring the fact that his allies paying his bills while he focuses on the larger strategic picture. That is until he notices, too late, what consequences of his decisions has wrought.  

As an aside, my favorite scene from the book is the moment when an Alpha Legion sleeper agent reveals themselves to a pair of Legionaries who demonstrate perfect Alpha Legion-tier OpSec, interrogate the agent’s Code Word and then make Alpharius (and Omegon) proud. I’d provide an excerpt here but I fear there’s no way to communicate the humour of the moment without introducing spoilers. You’ll know what I mean when you get there.

Alpha Legion Mk VI Tactical Marines. Credit: Lupe

The Paper to Plastic Pipeline (P2PP)

Will this book inspire you to buy any miniatures? Personally, it’s hard to determine how much this book sparked an enthusiasm to collect Alpha Legion, seeing as I already have an army of those sneaky, no-good, double-crossin’, yeller-bellied traitor scum but you do get some cool scenes with different Alpha Legion sub-factions that you could use as inspiration for squads within your own Alpha Legion Army. These warbands include: The Penitent Sons, who see themselves as Pro-Imperial Traitors (who have to self-flagellate themselves for all of the crimes against the Imperium they just can't stop themselves from doing); the Faceless (they are all Alpharius but only some of the time. They all take turns); the Shrouded Hand, and the biological warfare specialists known as the Sons of Venom (perfect for doing an Alpha Legion unit devoted to Nurgle). That said, enquiring minds need to know: Are we getting a Black Library release of a Solomon Akurra model or not!? If so, will it be a squad/entourage like Titus and Huron? Probably not, those sort of splash releases are reserved for the established/big name characters in the setting but I can at least dream of a Solomon and Tulva duo release.

Final Thoughts

Credit: Robert "TheChirurgeon" Jones

Despite being an Alpha Legion tragic, I can’t say I enjoyed the first Solomon Akurra book as much as I was hoping to at the time. Perhaps part of that was due to the legacy set by Dan Abnett with the amazing Horus Heresy novel, Legion. However, it’s important to recognise that the Alpha Legion 10,000 years ago is very different to the Alpha Legion in the contemporary setting. After so many thousands of years of war, it’s not surprising to see how a Legion that built itself up in layers and layers of secrets and subterfuge would have experienced such a marked culture drift. By giving us a variety of Alpha Legion warbands who each have their own whacky perspectives on how to enact their departed Primarch’s will, I think Mike Brooks does a really good job of reconciling the big question of: Are the Alpha Legion secret loyalists, traitors complicit from Day One of the heresy, unwitting dupes too clever for their own good, or perhaps even the instigators of the Heresy? The answer is: “Yes. Well, maybe. I don’t think anyone in the Alpha Legion even knows anymore.” Having given Akurra the previous book to settle in and start defining himself as a character, I think Mike Brooks has done a great job taking Solomon’s character to the next level and I’m looking forward to the next release and, in typical fashion, the books ends with enough of a hint that there will be another chapter in the Solomon Akurra chronicles. Bring it on!
