Hobby Heresy: Wargames Atlantic Dead Animal Bits

April 29th, 2026 via Goonhammer
In what was a bit of a surprise in late 2023, Wargames Atlantic partnered with Pete the Wargamer, of the Spare Parts Emporium to launch a Kickstarter: Dead Animal Bits. It's been lurking at the edge of my consciousness for a while, but after checking out some of Pete's content (it's good - Pete also partnered with ColourForge to produce the best colour of primer I've ever used!), and realising I suddenly needed a lot of antlers, I thought it was well past time we had a look at this jam-packed, highly useful, kit.

Thanks to Wargames Atlantic for sending this out for review!

I'll be honest, when this box came out I thought beyond the Kickstarter backers and the kitbashed obsessed (like me), it would have a limited appeal. It is all horns, heads, shark teeth and hands on sticks, so all useful bits, but a whole box crammed full of them seemed like overkill. After several months of using it - this is a very late review - I can categorically say the appeal here is exactly what it says on the tin. A whole box of weird bits that belongs in everyone's collection.

It's a bit of a departure from the WA model of versatile plastic kits providing large numbers of bodies - with lots of bits, admittedly - and this is the first of the just bits range, with Sorcerers’ Spoils already following, and I hope that it continues to grow. It's a damn useful set to converters and kitbashers, and I think it's well worth everyone's time to pick up a box to have on hand for all sorts of projects.

What You Get

The Dead Animal Bits box comes with three identical, absolutely packed, sprues of impaled hands, bones, feathers, antlers and jaws, providing an extensive, bits-box stuffing range of high quality plastic components. There's somewhere north of 300 bits in the box, covering a range of dead animal and human components for your modelling. Most come in a range of sizes (surprisingly handy), and orientations. Even within the broad categories there's a lot of variety - three types of antler, 2 types of bird skull, a staggering 20 different styles of impaled hand (a night lords player just sat up and paid attention, somewhere), 9 different pairs of horns, 2 shark jaw styles (and lots of extra teeth) and a couple of rib cages and long bones.

Dead Animal Bits Sprue, Credit: Wargames Atlantic. All manufacturers should put a nicely done sprue shot for me to steal and attach to articles

They're all sculpted to a really high quality, even on the smallest components and sculptor Katherine Hoppe has done an excellent job bringing these to life with realistic but scale-appropriate textures. There's some stand-out sculpting on pieces like the bird skulls and antlers that captures the look and detail of animal textures without becoming too brittle. Bits and component boxes often come with a much lower level of detail than you'd expect from a "main" kit, but this box absolutely bucks that trend, and the sculpting and plastic is on a par with anything else WA put out.

Horns, Teeth and Bone

 

Kitbasher's Karnival

The main reason you're getting this is that it's just damn useful to have a lot of horns, antlers, feathers and hands in 28mm plastic. There are bits in kitbashing that you can make yourself - sculpt twisted horns from armatures, faff about with feather textures, chop hands off arms and impale them on spikes, but having someone already doing that work for you is a real blessing. It's a sprue full of ideas, which is hugely exciting to me and I'm already chopping through the second of the several sprues in the box. While I could write more words here, I'll just do pictures of how I've used them instead - weirdoes and servitors abound - because what I want to do here is spark your ideas. What could you use this kit for?

Titan Crew - Antlers on the Damned Infantry. Credit: Lenoon

The wide variety of bits here opens up a lot of possibilities. Hands on spikes will look great on vehicle plating for a chaos-y look, or on medieval and fantasy terrain. The Shark jaws will adorn a fantastic RPG tavern on the docks while they invite you to base a whole scratch built monster around them. Plastic feathers and long bones are legitimately always useful for all sorts of modelling projects and you'll find reasons to use the rib cages - weirdly, my favourite bit of the box (I will claim several favourite bits before the review is through!

Shark Jaws at the start of a modelling project

Horns as tentacles. Credit: Lenoon

The volume of bits here is significant as well - with one sprue repeated three times you get multiples of everything and an astonishing amount of antlers and horns in various sizes. When I pick up bits and conversion kits I'm often disappointed that there'll be one of a particular thing I want - why is there just one Custodes helmet in the Battlefield Trophies box, GW? - and then I'm stuck with a lot of less useful pieces which will languish in my bits box. Not so here! Depending on what you're after this will range from an essential box - especially if you need horns, antlers, or hands impaled on spikes - to merely a box you definitely should get for your bits box. Purchasing bits can often feel like a rip off, but there's definitely 25 quid (or regional equivalent) of plastic in here, and a box will last all but the most dedicated converters a fair while.

Feathers, horns and impaled hands on a servitor. Credit: Lenoon

There are some immediate uses that will be the go-to options - big shark jaws for Ogor banners, miscellaneous bones for bases, antlers and horns for demons and corrupted guard, so on and so forth - but the world really is your oyster here. This is where providing all the different sizes of component comes in handy - you'll not be stuck with horns that look too small or too big, when there's eight different sizes on each sprue. I've been using this box for everything over the last few months, and when I've got down to the last few components on a sprue, I've been clipping them off and adding them to a basing texture mix to add some incidental shape, colour and grimdark theming to my bases. Now that's flexibility!

Bird Skull on Crooked Dice Miniature. Credit: Lenoon

Because this is Tabletop Battles and if you're reading this you're likely thinking about 40k, then rest assured - this set has a range of options that scale perfectly for 40k (in fact, scale better for 40k and other heroic scale ranges than they do for my historicals!). I've dug out a long abandoned marine and roughly stuck a load of bits to him to show off. For anyone touched by Chaos, Ghyran, or just wanting some horns and antlers, this box will go incredibly far in turning otherwise standard models into kitbashed masterpieces:

Hands on sticks, heads on hooks, horns - all scale perfectly well with this World Eaters Berserker

Everyone Get a Bone!

When this arrived through my door the main question I had was "Who is this for?", but after playing around with it, the answer is clear - everyone! Grimdark modellers, chaos modellers, weird guys will all get a lot of use out of it, but everyone will benefit. I think this is what I would consider a core set, one that should find a place in every single pile of opportunity.

A Core set, for me, isn't a set you'll be rushing out to buy several of, but one that should go into your bits box until it runs out and you get another one to replace it. That's a rare accolade, and one that I'd only share with incredibly useful kits like the Arcoflagellants from Games Workshop. You can pick this up for specific projects, but once it's in the bits box it provides so many different ideas and well-sculpted, well-scaled pieces to explore them with that whatever system you're playing, modelling or painting, it's worth getting a box.
