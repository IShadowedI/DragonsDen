StarCraft Tabletop Miniatures Game Protoss Faction Focus

May 5th, 2026 via Goonhammer

The Protoss (or Firstborn, if you speak Khalani) are a psionic species native to Aiur. They were uplifted by the Xel’naga and gifted with Purity of Form. Though initially revering those they saw as their creators, some eventually turned on the Xel’naga and drove them out.

The ancient Protoss entered a period known as the Aeon of Strife, a massive civil war that only ended once a Protoss named Khas figured out how to use Khaydarin Crystals to create a species-wide psionic link known as the Khala.

Eventually, a bunch of Protoss rejected the Khala and formed another faction known as the Dark Templar, which turned out to be a really good decision when a Xel’naga named Amon used the Khala to mind control the Protoss.

Protoss Starter by Blake Law

How They Play

The Protoss are the most elite faction in StarCraft. All of their units are stronger and more resilient than their counterparts in other factions, and are full of tricks to boot. On the flip side, their elite nature and low unit count means that any mistake or bad roll is far more punishing.

Mechanics

Protoss uniquely have access to the Shield characteristic. They’re also very happy and frequent users of the Place (X) ability.

Shield

Not truly a mechanic, but more of a stat unique to Protoss. Every Protoss unit has a Shield characteristic, which functions as additional HP for the first model in the unit. A squad of Adepts, for example, has 4 models with 3 HP each and 2 Shields.

This means that while 3 of the models will die after 3 damage, the first model will only die after 5. Effectively, this results in Protoss units being more resilient to attrition (at least until the first one dies).

Additionally, while a unit has taken less damage than its Shield characteristic and still has its initial model count, it has the Shielded status. Currently, that does exactly nothing, but it’s worth noting that in the video game there have been several mechanics that interact specifically with Shields.

Place(X)

Functionally a teleport. Place lets you move your leading model (X) inches, ignoring terrain, ignoring line of sight, and generally even ignoring Engagement Range. Stalkers can Place, Adepts have what amounts to a delayed Place, Warp Prism lets you Place, Twilight Council gives you Heal (2) when you Place, and so on.

Cards

Faction Cards

Daelaam - Faction

The Unified Government of the Protoss. After the events of Brood War, the two main Protoss factions united into the Daelaam. The other Protoss factions were later integrated during Legacy of the Void.

In game, Daelaam gives you 1 Elite Slot, 3 Core Slots, and a Support Slot. It has a reaction for damage reduction and a movement phase ability to pull all units within 6” of a faction indicator (about 28mm, so effectively every unit within 180.4mm of a point) back into Reserves, regardless of whether they’re engaged. This can be really powerful if your opponent’s signalling a strong push turn (Say, Terran Armed Forces popping Terran Tenacity), since you have more control over what your opponent can and cannot shoot.

Picking Daelaam over Khalai gives you a Support Slot and the really strong Mass Recall ability, but unfortunately it’s overshadowed by the power of the Khala, since Support Slots are generated by two of the best Faction Cards in Protoss but Hero slots are generated by an otherwise weak card.

Khalai - Faction

The Protoss who follow the Khala: The religion surrounding the psionic bond that once bound all Protoss together.

In game, following the Khala gives you 2 Elite Slots, 3 Core Slots, and 1 Hero Slot. It lets you warp in a Pylon as a free unit, as well as chain activations in the Movement Phase, helping you keep the First Player token. From the round after they’re placed, Pylons let you treat them as a board edge for one unit deploying per round, as well as granting a once-per-round discount of one PE for any special ability used within 4”.

Picking Khalai means you’re going to be focusing more on one area than in Daelaam, but considering how fast Protoss can be and how small the board is, that’s not really an issue.

Protoss Starter by Blake Law

Other Cards

At release, all Protoss cards give 1 PE.

Forge

1 Elite Slot. Tough (1) in any phase, or Critical Hit (1) in the Assault Phase. Tough boosts your durability, which is always great. Critical Hit works like Surge but better, since it stacks with Surge but also is the only way to bypass a Hero’s armour save.

Gate Chronoboosted (Unique)

1 Elite Slot, 1 Core Slot. +2 movement in the movement phase, or Precision (2) in the Combat Phase. At the time of writing, possibly a bit overcosted.

Gateway

1 Core Slot. Precision (2) for a melee weapon. Your cheap option for Zealot Core Slots.

Nexus (Unique)

2 Core Slots, Instant in any phase, or to pull the active, unenaged unit back into Reserves. Unfortunately pulling the active unit back means it can’t immediately redeploy, so your options are limited here. It eats your action so you can at best Blink out with Stalkers and then pull them back into reserves. Instant also lets you bypass many pesky Reactions (Medics, Queens, Cards…)

Observer (Unique)

1 Support Slot. In the Movement Phase lets you remove Hidden from enemy units within 6” of a token you put down, or used to give +1 to a unit’s charge. Notably, the +1 to charge is after the roll, so you can hold it for when you really need to just barely make it in. This is also the only way for Protoss to remove Hidden, so taking an Observer is borderline mandatory.

Overcharged Nexus (Unique)

1 Elite Slot, 1 Core Slot. Like the Nexus, gives you the ability to pull the active, unengaged unit back into Reserves, with all the same caveats as the Nexus. However, you trade off the ability to give a unit Instant for a Photon Cannon shot at a unit that came in from Reserves, immediately handing over 3 hits of 1 damage. It’s pretty limited in scope, but can absolutely make a Raynor’s Raider deployment miserable, since one of their big perks is using Stimpack for free from reserves. Taking 3 hits immediately though means they probably lose 2 models doing that (2 damage from Stim, 2 more damage from the Photon Overcharge on average). This can be massive for the CP economy of a Terran player, but it’s also extremely niche.

Power Field (Unique)

1 Hero Slot, 1 Elite Slot, 1 Core Slot. This card is how you get Artanis into Daelaam. Unfortunately, it only grants one ability, which is Dodge (2). Dodge is the only way other than Evade or reactive healing to block Surge or Critical damage, but Power Field is mostly a pretty weak card.

Twilight Council (Unique)

1 Elite Slot, 1 Support Slot. Comes with the aforementioned Heal (2) after you resolve a Place effect, and +4 Range on a unit’s first Ranged Weapon attack. Unlike Long Range (X), you don’t roll with -1 to hit.

If you run a Stalker pair, they can walk 4” off your board edge with another 4” for coherency, place 6” with another 4” off coherency, and then shoot 16”. That’s 34” of effective range for the front stalker, and 30” if you need both to shoot. If you combine it with Warp Prism for another 3” place, that’s 37”/41”. Considering the board is 54” by 36”, you can hit basically anything that’s moved to an objective T1. An absolute steal of a card.

Warp Gate (Unique)

2 Core Slots. Precision (2) in melee, or deploy your first Deployed unit of the round from any non-player board edge, 10” from any opposing unit. The first Deploy is the main limiting factor here, considering how many mobility options Protoss have. It can also be used to great effect in Round 2 or 3 after you move your already deployed units around, since the limit is first Deployed, not first Activated unit. See what your opponents do, then drop a bunch of Adepts or Stalkers or Zealots to go after their target of choice.

Warp Prism (Unique)

2 Core Slots. As mentioned earlier, Place (3) in the Movement Phase, or a signalled end-of-round Deploy. You get to drop a token anywhere on the board 10” away from any Enemy model, and then after scoring you put a unit’s Lead model base to base with your token and deploy them normally. It’s telegraphed, but lets you either get units up the board really early or force your opponent to react by blocking the token.

Units

All the factions are pretty limited at the moment, so there’s not too much to cover here.

Heroes

Artanis

The man, the myth, the legend. Youngest Templar to ever become Praetor and Executor, the protagonist of Legacy of the Void, the saviour of the entire Protoss Race. Artanis is an absolute tank and blender. He comes in at Speed 7, with 4+ Armour and 5+ Evade, 8 HP and 4 Shields. As with all Heroes so far, he’s 1 Supply, but is treated as 2 for Objectives and Disengages. As a Hero, he’s also neither Light nor Armoured, so the only way to bypass his armour is Criticals. He’s also got a few nasty tricks up his sleeves.

In the Movement Phase he can teleport in using another unit, returning them to Reserves before immediately ending his movement. He can do this to a unit that’s already engaged but before their activation, letting them come back in off a Pylon to shoot or charge. He can teleport into combat, forcing a shooting unit to shoot a sub-optimal target, or swap with a Supply 1 unit which can prevent a Supply 2 unit from freely Disengaging. The possibilities are endless.

In the Assault Phase, he can bodyguard for another unit within 8” if he’s also an eligible target. He can ALSO Evade these attacks, increasing his durability. Bunch of nasty Roaches shooting your squishy Zealots? Artanis can soak that damage right up and avoid any Surge.

On the charge, Artanis comes with Impact (6) 4+, averaging 3 hits into any target when he charges. He can also do a double-dash, charging into one unit before immediately declaring another charge and leaving the first unit without a tango partner. Unfortunately, the second charge doesn’t do Impacts, but this can give him a massive charge range. With a minimum charge each time, Artanis can swing a solid 16” across the board in one swoop, with 26” not being impossible for this man.

In Combat he can Sweep or Strike, hitting on a 2+. His Strike comes with 2 attacks with a D3 Surge against Armoured targets for 3 damage a pop (basically on average he just does 6 damage to an Armoured target), and his Sweep is 6 attacks with D3+1 Surge against Light targets for 1 damage each. He’s not going to single-handedly clean out a Zergling swarm or a Marine brick, but he’ll comfortably shut down most units he gets into contact with.

Supports

Sentry

Your friendly neighbourhood bubble-bot. They come in a pair for 1 supply with a crisp 4/7 move, 5+ Armour and 6+ Evade, 4 HP each and 2 Shields.

Baseline, they have the Debuff cleanse within 4” reaction that all three starting support units have, the ability to pop an 80mm base Force Field down in the Movement Phase, and be able to deploy their Guardian Shields in the Movement Phase to protect all friendly units within 4” from shooting attacks (reducing Attack Pools generated by 1).

Upgrade wise, they can get a second Force Field each round, and/or protect a friendly unit within 4” with Hallucinations (including themselves!), granting an Evade Roll when shot.

Weapons wise, there’s not much to talk about other than their ranged weapon having Instant. Fantastic at popping a Stimpacked Marine camping near Medic friends.

You’re almost always going to want at least one squad of sentries, since their Force Fields can cut off choke points (Just make sure they’re just too far for a Size 3 unit to walk into and break it) and Guardian Shields and Hallucinations really cut down on the strength of enemy shooting.

Protoss Starter by Blake Law

Core

Adept

The ghostly menaces from StarCraft II are here and ready to annoy the hell out of your opponents. They come in 4s for 1 supply with a zippy 5/8 move, 5+ Armour and 5+ Evade, 3 HP each and 2 Shields. They’re your starter skirmishing unit, built to counter enemy skirmishing units.

Baseline, they’re a shooting unit with One Neat Trick (Overminds hate it!). They can, during their activation in the Movement Phase, drop a ghostly shade of themselves somewhere wholly within 12” of themselves. Friendly units targeting enemy units within 4” of the Shade gain Precision (1) both in shooting and in melee, which is pretty sizeable. In addition, at the end of the Round, the Adept unit can choose to teleport to it, treating it as a Leading Model. While this happens after scoring, the presence of the shade can create some set-up quandaries for your opponent, since they never know whether you’re going to teleport to it or not.

They’ve got a pretty brutal 8” shooting attack into Light units, with 2 attacks each, hitting on 3’s with Anti-Evade (1) and Surge D3+1 against Light. This can be upgraded to Anti-Evade (2), or with a 1 PE active ability for 3 attacks each. Unfortunately, since 6s for an Evade roll are always successful, the former only works against units with a 4+ Evade (only Zerglings at the moment). The latter upgrade being an active ability also hurts it, since PE economy is usually going to be tight.

In melee, they start with a single damage 1 attack that hits on 4s with no Surge or special rules. They can upgrade that to have Surge D3 against Light and Piece Light (2), making them fantastic at butchering Marines and Zerglings. While it still hits on 4s, their own Shade can provide Precision for some reliability.

Typically, Adepts are going to be the ones running up the board early to force a response so your Zealots can come and clean up. The Shade can make it tricky to keep them locked down, and if it comes down to it a single surviving model can still use their Shade to buff the rest of your army.

Zealot

My Life for Aiur! The classic infantry staple of Protoss are here to kill Marines and chew bubblegum, and they’ve got no mouths to chew with. They work in threes for 2 Supply (no more or no less) with 4/7 move, 5+ Armour and 5+ Evade, a solid 4 HP each and 3 Shields.

Baseline, they’re a fairly tanky anti-Light melee unit with Devastating Charge for Impact (3) 4+. They can charge for 2d6 drop lowest, and are fairly choppy with 4 Damage 1 attacks each, hitting on 3s with Surge D3 against Light. It’s decent, but nothing fantastic.

Upgrades are where Zealots get spicy. They can improve their Devastating Charge to Impact (4) for more damage. They can also get Leg Enhancements for a 1 PE second Move action of 2”. 2” doesn’t sound like much, but in reality that’s more like 5” once you factor in how moving models in StarCraft works. This extra movement is essential for getting them into combat quickly, which is where you want them to be.

They have a defensive Assault Phase upgrade, allowing them to pop their Reaction and Activation to reduce incoming damage by 2 (with no minimum). While limited in scope, this can be pretty useful if they’re stuck in combat with a shooting unit from last Round, since it gives them something to do with their Assault Phase activation in that case.

Their last upgrade is Precision (2) if they’ve got friends Engaged with their target. Mostly useful if you’ve counter-charged a unit that engaged your shooty models, but your Zealots don’t really want to be spending their time ganging up on enemy units. Rare is the Light unit that will shrug off 3 Zealots in their face.

Zealots serve a dual purpose for Protoss. They’re your cleanup crew for clearing Light units once the Adepts have whittled down the numbers. They’re also really good at contesting objectives, since even a single Zealot has a Supply value of 1. Your opponent has to make sure the unit is properly dead, lest your single Zealot make a heroic sprint for a backline objective and force them to split their forces to deal with him.

Elite

Stalker

Blink and you’ll miss them. Your anti-Armour staple, each Stalker in a unit costs 1 Supply, and they come with a 4” Coherency, 4/8 move, 4+ Armour and 6+ Evade, a staggering 6 HP each and 3 Shields.

All Stalkers can Place (6) for 1 PE, though they’ve got the additional restriction of having to avoid Engagement Range while doing so. They’ve got 12” on their guns at 4 attacks per model, hitting on 3s, Surge D3 against Armoured for 2 damage a pop. In sharp contrast, the less said about their melee the better.

In terms of upgrades, they can purchase Path of Shadows, a 1 PE ability to gain Hidden at any point until they do anything else. They can also gain Fury of the Nerazim to gain Instant when shooting at a unit that already Activated, making them deadlier into units that charge them or after biding their time while Hidden.

Before you think about it, the cap on ability use per round means you can’t move-hide-shoot-hide. You can however move and hide, either gaining initiative in shooting or forcing detection. Alternatively, they can move, wait, and then hide after shooting, preventing a counterattack. You can even leave them hidden for a turn, then shoot and hide again.

As stated earlier, Stalkers can shove a ridiculous distance up the board in a single turn with proper card use, making them one of the few units that reliably do damage turn 1. They’re your premier unit for sniping out pesky T1 Omega Worms and Pylons, and very reliably deal with opposing armoured units.

Praetor Guard

Artanis’ bodyguard. You can only take one and they’re only available for the Khalai sub-faction, with a statline identical to Zealots.

Like the other faction special units, they come with certain upgrades built in and no options. They have Leg Enhancements built in, and their main selling point is their upgraded defensive abilities, since they have Tough (2) on the first Armour Roll each Round in addition to having Evade rolls in melee.

Attacks wise, they’re the same as Zealots, but with a slight twist: If they’re swinging at a Size 3 unit, they’re Damage 2. Unfortunately, all Size 3 units at the time of writing are Armoured and therefore they won’t get any Surge, so it’s less something that you aim to use and more something that’s nice to have when it comes up.

Protoss Starter by Blake Law

Other

Pylon

“You must construct additional Pylons!” is what I would say if this game didn’t cap you out at just one (and then, only if you’re playing Khalai). Your Pylon doesn’t move, has a 5+ Armour with 8 HP and 2 Shields.

As a Structure, Pylons can’t contest objectives, can’t activate, and can’t perform actions. You also can’t use abilities on it unless the ability specifies it works on Structures, so no popping a Forge to give it Tough (1).

On the turn it’s placed, Pylons do nothing while they warp in. After that, one unit per turn can treat it as an Entry Edge (but immediately end its activation, so no Zealot/Stalker move/place shenanigans). Additionally, it improves your PE economy by discounting one ability use within 4” per round.

Combos

STALKER T1 BLINK DELIGHT

Best served in response to Burrow Ambush or a Pylon/Omega Worm drop in the midboard. Do note that distances are for the maximum possible, not necessarily what’s ideal.

Ingredients: 2 Stalkers, Twilight Council, Warp Prism, 1-2 PE

Step 1: Bring in 2 Stalkers from your board edge, landing the further model 8” from the board edge

Step 2: Spend 1 PE to Blink for Place (6), pushing the further model forward to 18” from the deployment edge

Step 3: Use Warp Prism for Place (3), pushing the further model to 25” from the deployment edge.

Step 4: Use Twilight Council for 16” range, allowing you to hit units with one Stalker at 41” from your board edge or 37” with both.

Step 5: Season to taste, preferably serve with Path of Shadows for a subtle hint of Hidden.

MASS RECALL-REDEPLOY

One of the most crucial elements of Daelaam is proper use of the Mass Recall ability (or at least, threatening it). Fundamentally, this means your opponent will often have difficulty heavily committing to an attack somewhere on the board since you can just pick your units up and deploy them again if they haven’t yet activated. Alternatively, if they have already activated, you can bring in different friends. Fundamentally, this amounts to the following:

Step 1: Aggressively posture with some units, the earlier the better. If your opponent heavily commits to dealing with these units to threaten these units, proceed to step 2. Otherwise, sit pretty in the knowledge that you’re comfortably winning a flank.

Step 2: Mass Recall to pull them back

Step 3: Now deploy them again, laugh at misery as their units are now horribly out of position to deal with your army that is now collapsing the other flank.

Do note that since you can only do this once, it’s sometimes better to be able to threaten with the capacity for this than it is to actually use it.
