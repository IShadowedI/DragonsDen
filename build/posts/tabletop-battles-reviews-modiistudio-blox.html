Tabletop Battles Reviews: ModiiStudio Blox

May 11th, 2026 via Goonhammer
ModiiStudio set up - product photo

We're taking a look at the least fun part of our hobby today: How to keep our workspaces organised and tidy.

I’ve never been very good at organising. It’s a no-good habit I’ve got that spawned from a quirk of my brain mixed with years of ignoring said quirk. I put things down and they vanish, and it sends me spiralling, creating more mess trying to find something I most likely just put out of line of sight in a place I don’t usually.



My previous setup failed to account for any of my bad habits. Paints would end up mixed in with each other, or never make it back to their racks, paint brushes never made it back to their holders, and worst of all I had it split onto a secondary desk. It seemed like a good idea at the time, under the big window where I could use the natural daylight to my advantage… but the problem is that I much prefer painting at my computer desk because I like to chat with my mates or have videos on while I do my hobby. I’m not someone who does things alone very easily, I really benefit from body doubling, so this causes issues pretty frequently.

It made my bad habit of misplacing things even worse too. Stuff would end up getting brought over to my desk and never going back where they belonged. No matter how diligent I tried to be, my main desk would still end up cluttered with paints, model bits, and brushes that’d eventually get swept to somewhere I couldn’t see, or completely forgotten about.

My space isn’t the biggest, it’s an office, a hobby workshop, and an attic to store a houses worth of junk in all in one and it means space is premium. I need everything on my desk to be something I’m using, or else it'll contribute to the dysfunction.

UK Games Expo 2025

Enter: ModiiStudio. When I first glimpsed ModiiStudio’s Bloxs at the UKGE last year, I was immediately impressed by it. I was drawn in by the impressive set up they had put together and was pulled in for amazing chat about their entire process of printing and finishing it. When they offered to let us have a few modules for a review piece, I leapt at the chance.

What Are Bloxs, Anyway?

ModiiStudio's Blox is a 3D printed slot-together, modular hobby organisation solution.  While 3D printed, it’s not printable. It’s only available as a physical product on their website, which you can find here.

The whole, core premise is unrivalled customisation and you can really see it. Nearly every module, give or take some specifics like the drawers, or the photobooth, comes in a breadth of sizes and widths that are aimed at letting you create stacks that fit your needs. It can be a little overwhelming to plan without pictures, but it's simple once you do.

Every module is aimed at being compatible with one another, and all build up to form either a full-width or half-width stack.



For the purpose of the review, I got a healthy mix of everything to mix together to exactly how I need it. I own a lot of paint, but rarely do I use it all, so having the option to stuff utility underneath my most used pots is a massively handy feature.

The Modules

ModiiStudio has racks for every brand you'll need (except for Darkstar, my beloved), as you might expect. I think these are the least exciting modules they have to offer but they're understandably the most useful for us hobbyists. Each rack sits the paint it’s made for nearly perfectly, and except for the Citadel rack, you can slot them forwards or back if you prefer to get a look at the colour from the butt, or the tip if you've dapped it in a bit of paint or something similar.



 

More exciting are the storage and utility modules. I found the drawers great for keeping spare bits, and having the option to have them with inserts for droppers is nice even if I don't quite have as many Vallejo as I used to.



I filled the normal drawers sanding sticks and magnets, finally helping me stop losing them, and\is great for keeping my oil paints in my sight-line so I actually remember to use them. There's some little MVP bits I've gotten to try too, like the segmented tray that fits under a full-width stack that I filled with bitz (and the occasional hair-tie.) as well as the adorable paint-pot holder.

Dino buddy always got my back.

In my time using the system, I found that not every module is equal in its design. What I thought I’d barely use at all, the photobooth, has literally transformed my workflow by making me way more excited to paint because it makes taking good photos of my miniatures incredibly easy. The provided pieces of acrylics are great, easy to use, and they store away smartly when you don’t want them in the way.



It’s legitimately made me paint more. I don’t love the gooseneck USB lights, they’re a bit finicky and fit a bit too loosely for my liking but man, they’re perfect for getting the right amount of light I need. Like, look! I have photos that actually make it look like I can paint. I love it to bits.

'95 Goliath and Orlock gangers. Credit to Josh 'Moogus' Boyes.

On the other end of the spectrum is the corner module. It’s a bitterly weighty piece that upsets the balance of everything around it unless they’re heavy with paint, limiting what you can attach to it and ruining the whole customisation angle. I find it doesn’t provide much storage either, as I can’t find anything useful to store on it outside some cheap brushes and some plastic glue that I usually keep closer to me anyway. I thought about storing my more expensive brushes but I felt like I’d crush the bristles reaching over for them.

It’s a low-light of the entire system, and I hope there’s a redesign of it sometime in the future.



I’ve found a use for every module I’ve been set to test, and outside the corner module, I'm satisfied with each of them. I’m excited to expand my set to include a bit more for brush and tool storage, as I'm always in need of places to throw my sanding sticks after I’m done with them or they inevitably end up on the floor.

It's nice to have a system that makes it easy to encourage myself to put everything back in its proper place when I’m done. It’s the point of any storage system really, but Modii does it better than any I’ve had so far, and that counts for something.



Price-wise, I find it very comparable to other premium hobby systems made out of MDF, a material I’ve come to loathe, with full-sized modules costing around £30 each and the modules with lighting costing closer to £80.

Ordering in half and third sizes and widths is cheaper individually, but will cost you more overall to make up a single stack. It makes sense when you consider it’ll be taking up three printers instead of one and take hours longer to be finished.

Build Quality

Speaking of being a printed product, the Blox system suffers from being brittle in the thinner areas. Most of it is printed thick, with little or no hollowing at all, making it sturdy being shifted around on a desk. It's just the parts that aren't that are worrying.

I've had a few non-serious breakages of clips and the drawer handles, with the most serious being a shelf that had a clip snap under what I consider a reasonable amount of pressure for friction fitting it into place and making it completely non-functional.



 

Most of it I found I could fix with a bit of superglue, which I’ve no doubt if you’re interested in this stuff you’ll have laying around but for the bits you can’t it’s pretty frustrating.

In the thicker parts, the only problem I saw was a crack along the seam of the print on the Vallejo rack. Despite feeling like it should be compromising the integrity of the entire thing, it doesn’t feel like it’s affected anything at all. It hasn't bowed or anything, or gotten worse even as I shove more paints in so I haven't felt the need to fret about it.

For the sake of transparency, I can’t fairly weigh this against the product. Due to some circumstances, we had to ship the set an additional time than was necessary for this review, and a couple of breakages occurred during the second shipping. I still think it's worth bringing up, so that if you buy into it you know to go in and be a bit more delicate than you think you might need to be, but a few things like the foot or clip were more on us shipping it more than needed than it was from natural usage.

For a bit more background on the studio and the processes behind the curtain, my fellow writer Rich conducted an interview with Dav, the main person behind ModiiStudio!



Looking over the sample pieces you sent, it's clear that there is some assembly and gluing done by you before sending parts out (e.g. the joining tracks on the sides of the Blox, some of the paint inserts). How do you decide what is worth doing in-house vs leaving for the end user to assemble?

My aim has been to  make ModiiStudio as easy to set up and get ready to use - so people can get on with painting as quickly as possible. So I want to supply the parts complete. I would also be uneasy leaving assembly to the new owner - would they use the correct adhesive, stick their fingers to it etc!

Do you worry at all about scalability of your manufacturing if you receive a large number of orders? Do you envision a future where every surface in your house is covered in printers?

We’re very mindful about scalability. A large insert can take a good few hours to print. We haven’t got a full on print farm, but it’s certainly a smallholding of printers! To manage demand, we keep some of the most popular Blox in stock, while less common variants are produced on demand. That’s why we quote a 2–3 week delivery window.

If order volumes were ever to exceed our in-house capacity, we already have relationships with third-party manufacturers (the same partners we use for Stowii), so scaling up would be possible. In the long term, injection moulding would be the ideal solution for high-volume production, but the setup costs and minimum order quantities make that a future dream rather than an immediate one.

It seems like there's a dedicated section of Kickstarter for 3d printed hobby terrain/tool STLs at this point, and I suppose it's easy to understand why given that you can receive huge numbers of backers without putting yourself on the hook for manufacturing and shipping an equivalent number of physical pledge deliveries. Have you given any thought to releasing the .STL files for ModiiStudio for purchase, for fans of the setup who have their own printers?

It’s true that many people choose the crowdfunding route with STL files, since it avoids the challenge of producing and shipping physical products. Having run crowdfunded projects before, I know how stressful it can be to suddenly face hundreds of physical orders to fulfil, and I didn’t want to be in that position again.

From the outset, I always envisioned ModiiStudio as a physical product rather than just a set of printable files. Most miniature painters own resin printers, but ModiiStudio needs filament printers. On top of that, I feel painters should spend their time painting, not troubleshooting prints to build storage.

It’s taken a lot of trial and error to refine our own setup to produce ModiiStudio to the standard we wanted. So while STL files can be great in other contexts, I was clear from the start that ModiiStudio should be offered as a finished, ready-to-use product.

Getting ModiiStudio to print consistently and to the standard we wanted has taken a huge amount of trial, testing, and refinement. It’s not quite as straightforward as dropping a file into a slicer and pressing “print.” While STL files can be fantastic in some contexts, for ModiiStudio I felt strongly that it should be offered as a finished, reliable, ready-to-use product rather than asking hobbyists to take on the printing process themselves.



 

Thanks Dav for the behind the scenes insight on the processes behind the product, and thank you and ModiiStudio for providing the product for the review.

I’m happy to throw my recommendation behind ModiiStudio if you’re considering upgrading your hobby set up. It’s worth every penny so far and I’m excited to expand as far as my desk space (and wallet) will allow me.

ModiiStudio’s website can be found here if you want to browse the modules for yourself. Let ‘em know Tabletop Battles sent ya’!
