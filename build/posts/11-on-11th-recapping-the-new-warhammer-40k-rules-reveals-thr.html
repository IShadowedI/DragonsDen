11 on 11th: Recapping the New Warhammer 40K Rules Reveals Through 5/1

May 2nd, 2026 via Goonhammer
We're about a month out from the release of Warhammer 40,000 11th Edition, and in the past few weeks we've seen the debut of a few new rules tidbits, and the models of the entire intro box. Last time, we recapped the majority of the changes coming to terrain, cover, & more, while today's collected info hones in on what to expect when jumping in to 40k this coming June. The goal with this series is to condense all of that into exactly what you need to know, and how it will impact armies during the transition between Editions. Especially with some of the changes coming to things like Fights First, the relative power-to-cost ratios of many units & Enhancements will shift drastically, so it's key to note what might survive the scuffle. With that prelude out of the way, let's dive in.

1. Engagement Range Is Changing

Credit: Games Workshop

We're kicking off with an incredibly impactful change, of the move to a 2" engagement range from 1". This is coupled with a sneakier shift in the rules, that units can move through engagement ranges during a move (but, similar to 10th, not end within them). A few problems get solved by this alteration, and the big one they've even called out in the reveal article is that hiding 1.1" behind ruin walls no longer ensures safety from most charges from anything with a base larger than 25mm. In 10th, a core aspect of the competitive game was positioning units either against, or 'off' walls, the intent of which was to either allow them to be fought through terrain or not; this was a bit annoying to teach, with much frustration caused by this rules quirk in a 10th learner's league, and also allowed for extremely hard-to-shift infantry units in the midboard. As seems typical of 11th however, melee is being thrown a bone. With the change to 2", staging 2.1" behind a wall now only prevents models on bases >50mm from getting through, which is a rare few units that couldn't already be stopped by walls (Kataphrons, for example, are Infantry on 60mm bases). 50mm bases themselves measure ~1.96" in diameter, so you can just barely squeeze into a unit trying to pull 10th's tricks in the new edition.

Being able to move through engagement ranges does make sense with this change, as otherwise screening would be too strong, with Deep Striking chaff like Vespids potentially jailing an entire army in its deployment zone. That being said, without the previous buffer, jail lists lose a lot of steam (which is likely good for the health of the game). Coupled with asymmetric missions, it's possible the gameplan of preventing opposing scoring might go the way of the dodo, as some armies won't even need to move to the midboard and stand on points, much less be blocked by 10 40mm bases.

2. Charge Variance Is Reduced

Charging has been, since the beginnings of modern 40k, one of the game's most mathematically divisive aspects. The chance of a unit doing nothing if a charge is failed always exists, and has broadly been an issue with melee armies feeling inconsistent in their execution. Warhammer is a dice game, yes, but the difference in missing an average number of ranged attacks versus failing a charge and making no melee attacks is profound. Age of Sigmar has, since its rules were codified, allowed charges to be decided after a dice roll (reducing risk when considering a risky, long charge), and now 40k is adopting this facet of gameplay in 11th. The upside with being a charge-centric army with this change comes down to having both higher peaks, along with the existing valleys. While there's still the possibility of failing and netting 0 points in a melee trade, when you spike and charge the unit you'd never have tried to touch previously, those hot rolls require savvy opponents to play far cagier. Charges now measure base-to-base to check their legality, rather than the previous check for engagement range, and units need only end within 1" of each legal 'Charge Target' to be engaged in combat. This makes for fewer arguments about charge distances, and allows for our next change to work properly, regarding the change to reserves.

3. It's Easier to Arrive from Reserves

Now called 'Ingress Moves', both Deep Strike & Strategic Reserves can arrive within a distance of (just outside) 8" of enemy models, rather than 9". This seems like a small change, but especially in the case of Deep Strikes, you need to be significantly cagier about screening out against utility pieces like Assassins. Meanwhile, for bricks of Terminators, widening the range of arrival makes it less annoying to drop in, and I can already sense Chaos Space Marine players eyeing up Warpstrike Champions now that you can chuck those bodies deeper into the fray. Crucially, the effective roll required to charge out of reserves hasn't changed, as you measure from base-to-base, so it's still dependent on rolling 9s. Lists whose conceit is charging after dropping down will remain inconsistent, as hitting that 9 is a mere 28% chance, and even with free rerolls that is still less than a coinflip at 48%; if you're just trying to shoot though, there's a ton of potential for finding great lanes for your Obliterators or 5 man Terminator squads in 11th, even with Leaders in tow. We haven't heard how this is affecting reduced ranges like Deathshroud Terminators or Inceptors, though for units that use these tools to increase charge consistency, they'll be effectively nerfed if their rules remain unchanged. Given the terror Deathshrouds were in 10th, I'd not be surprised if this was a happy coincidence.

4. Pile In & Consolidate Are No Longer Individual

Credit: Games Workshop

There's a push in 11th Edition to move actions that used to be resolved on a per-unit basis to the broader sequence of the game, and Pile In/Consolidate are no different. Instead of resolving Pile In before fighting with a unit selected to activate, instead it's merely done at start of combat. Likewise, Consolidate happens at end of combat, once fighting's been finished, but you jump back into the sequence if you Consolidate into a unit that hasn't yet fought. A unit can, as far as we can tell, only Pile In/Consolidate once per turn unless otherwise stated, so once that fighting starts or is finished you don't get to move again (though the unit that's been Consolidated-into should be able to). This does change the tactics surrounding piling in to inconvenient pieces like Vehicles a bit, but should broadly be a time-saving measure more than anything else. The fact you can re-enter the Fight sequence, as illustrated by how Consolidation works, is perhaps the more interesting facet and it may be possible that this is a tool the designers can use in simplifying the order of operations concerning units like the World Eaters Helbrute.

If you would be eligible to fight, but are no longer engaged (perhaps because your target was already eliminated), you now get an 'Overrun Fight', that includes an extra Pile In before you start swinging. This gives tremendous movement to units that have access to a 6" Pile In, and if arranged correctly, could provide a full foot of movement in the Fight Phase alongside a unit to kill their initial charge target. I don't think this makes, say, Hearthband for Leagues of Votann immediately good, but Saga of the Hunter has immense appeal with this change in mind. If you can flex a 6" Pile In with a stratagem or rule, remembering to use it as a slingshot in this way is going to score you plenty of points.

5. Fights First Is Dead

Some will cheer, some will jeer, but for all intents and purposes the changes to activation have neutered the power of Fights First in 11th Edition. Instead of beginning with the player whose turn it isn't, in the Fight First sequence, you always start with the player whose turn it is. No longer does the threat of Heroically Intervening with a Fights First unit completely wash melee armies, but by that same virtue...it approaches being a non-rule now. So long as the turn player activates appropriately, a single Fights First unit being charged will never get a chance to activate before being swung at. Instead, in order for Fights First to matter, you now need to have 2+ units in combat with units that have charged, who have the rule; if the designers spread it to a number of different squads, such as among the Aeldari, that could be used as a means of disincentivizing opponents from charging more than one unit at a time, but even so they'd still get to hit the juiciest target before they hit back. All told, it is reasonable to expect current units that are overpriced (or priced appropriately) due to their Fights First to go down in cost, and need re-evaluation in 11th. Personally, I think this is an over-correction coupled with the numerous changes we've seen that are favorable to melee armies, but time will tell.

Following the Fight First sequence, however, the player next in order of activation gets to pick a unit to fight, among non-Fights First units. In most cases, this means the player whose turn it isn't picks the first unit to fight after all the charging units swing, but hilariously units with Fights First are further punished, because charging into one of them and forcing your opponent to resolve their combat in the Fight First sequence means you could, as turn player, get the first activation in a prolonged combat where neither unit has the charge bonus (now no longer codified, and just Fights First). All of these changes were essentially made to punish exactly the 'Intervene with a FF unit' counterpunch that was central to many 10th Edition lists, and otherwise not alter the sequence radically.

6. Keywords Are More Faction-Agnostic

It's important to start this section by saying this is a good thing, and does a lot to shift the thematic centerpoint of 40k's rules away from standard Imperial-issue armaments. We've seen various Keywords/Stratagems changed during the previewed rules, with such examples as 'Pistol' being shifted to 'Close Quarters', 'Grenades' to 'Explosives', and 'Tank Shock' to 'Crushing Impact'. These re-namings seem unimpactful, but may help the designers better spread out the rules to units which previously lacked them. Not every faction has pistols in the literal sense, but quickly drawn sidearms appear in every force, and the battlesuit defense systems of the Tau could certainly be reframed as being Close Quarters, even if they weren't literally Pistols. Agnosticism of armaments like this is ultimately a step towards better handling factions that aren't just Guard & Marines, and likely mean we'll see a greater variety of Keywords come into play on the tabletop.

Along those lines, we saw the debut of a brand-new Keyword, 'Cleave', which is a melee version of Blast. This seems tailor-made for units that want to crunch hordes, but aren't big enough to deserve an entire division between a Strike & Sweep profile. We've seen it on the new Warboss, and that's precisely the kind of model where I'd expect it. There's a bit of overlap here with Sustained Hits, in terms of application, but much the same as with Ranged weapons it gives more granularity to the expected number of attacks, when firing into the correct targets. Blast/Cleave is a reward for proper targeting, while Sustained/Lethal gives you the chance to spike against a broader array of foes.

7. Overwatch & Heroic Intervention Are Harder to Bait

We don't have the full rules regarding Overwatch & Heroic Intervention, but from the snippets we've seen there appears to be substantial changes to how these operate, akin to their counterparts in Age of Sigmar. Each happens at the end of the enemy Movement or Charge Phase, respectively, and that already should raise some eyebrows. No longer can you 'bait' Overwatch, but in turn it appears not to work in response to Charges; as seems to bear repeating in nearly every section, this is a huge boon for melee armies, as you won't get mowed down on the charge anymore, but your opponents do get better target selection...right? Not exactly—your target has to be visible at the end of the Movement Phase, meaning you can't Overwatch a unit that's trying to run into/behind a piece of terrain for cover, if it's fully obscured by the end of that move. Only units that are tanky enough to survive full exposure, or that are somehow left with their tabards hanging down in the open, will suffer at the hands of an Overwatch. Introduced back alongside this is the idea of 'Snap Shots', which represent shots that only ever hit on 6s, if it functions similar to its counterpart in 6th/7th Edition. I wager this is not only to clean up shoot back/out-of-phase shooting more broadly, but also provide the designers with an extra knob to twist, in terms of defensive Stratagems. Firing at a unit 18" or more away? How about a Strat to spent 1CP, and turn those shots into Snap Shots? There's been no confirmation of this specific scenario, but it being codified points to more usage across 11th than just Overwatch.

As for Heroic Intervention, what we do know is that its range to trigger has been buffed to 12", and that it's been broken up into two forms: Leap to Defend & Into the Fray. Leap to Defend works like you'd expect, letting you countercharge an opponent's unit that charged, at the end of their Charge Phase, with anything that isn't a Vehicle unit (except Vehicles that are also Walkers and/or Characters). This asks you to "resolve a charge", which is a different phrasing from the outlined "declared a charge" which provides Fights First, so the assumption is that you don't get what we'd call the 'charge bonus' in 10th Edition terms, similar to how it works now. The other mode, Into the Fray, hasn't been fully revealed...but based on its name one can imagine it concerning intervention into prolonged combats. Heroic Intervention is harder to bait, but more flexible, although combined with the changes to Fights First I imagine it has less impact than we've previously seen.

8. Smoke Billows Out

Another Stratagem change, this time for Smoke, now called 'Smoke Screen'. It's chosen at the start of the opposing Shooting Phase (meaning opponents can in theory ignore the Smoked unit to waste a CP)...but it isn't just for the unit with this keyword. Now, any unit with models selected as targets for shooting that are partially obscured by the unit that used Smoke Screen also gain the Stratagem's benefit, namely Benefit of Cover. This is a pretty massive change to a very widespread Keyword, and it does a ton for armies looking to buff the defensive profile of their Secondary Play pieces like Battle Sisters. While it no longer grants Stealth in 11th Edition (the rules for which we don't know), as a reminder cover now provides -1BS to the enemy unit firing at you, which approximates 10th Edition's Stealth. You can use a number of Dedicated Transports to not only jettison troops up the board, but also keep them safe from shooting once they disembark, and because the Transport itself is the target of Smoke Screen, units benefiting from the strat can still be targeted for their own Stratagems in the same phase, given 11th is moving to '1 Stratagem per unit, per phase. I'm very bullish on Smoke Screen, and it does a lot to help make Transports worthwhile once their cargo has been delivered up the board.

9. Chapter Approved Is Back

Credit: Games Workshop

10th Edition had a few different 'seasons' for its formal Matched Play Mission Packs, between Leviathan, Pariah Nexus, and Chapter Approved. Confusingly, the first set of rules along these lines is also called Chapter Approved, but its actual makeup is likely radically different. As we covered in the last recap, 11th has centered its missions around asymmetrical Primaries, with match-ups between different Force Dispositions (based on your Detachment) resulting in wildly different mechanisms to score your Primary VP. There are a whopping 25 Primary Missions in the deck, one for each potential combination of Dispositions, and that's the kind of competitive shakeup everyone's excited for at the outset of the Edition. Curiously, it doesn't appear that this deck is much thicker (if at all) than the previous versions, so its actual contents in terms of cards & markers shouldn't be overwhelmingly dense.

10. A Push(-Fit) for Flexibility

Credit: Games Workshop

We're finishing this coverage off with a quick look at the new units arriving in the Armageddon box set, starting with the Orks. Despite being push-fit, there's been a focus placed on individuality in this box, with both sides having units with customizable heads, for those looking to avoid the follies of monopose miniatures. There's a whopping 20 Boyz in this box, and as if to apologize for their previous iteration, every model is considered to have both Shootas & Choppas. Notably, the Boss Nob can have two different Kombi options, so we may be returning to different profiles between Kombi-weapons in 11th. The new Gretchin are a ton of fun, but are lacking their Runtherd, which doesn't bode well for the model's likelihood to transfer in from 10th. It's always possible we see one appear as a Support Leader in a separate, post-launch kit, but it's hard to say. Still, Games Workshop has made a point to call out the fact each individual Gretchin has 2 build options, which means no two starter boxes will look exactly alike; for the ramshackle Orks, that's not only a huge hobby win, but a victory for flavor as well. There's talk of things like the Wartrakk being seen among Warbikers, implying it likewise receives the Speed Freeks keyword, and the centerpiece of the entire box—the Big Mek Dakkarig—supposedly serves as a shootier Deff Dread that's "surprisingly accurate" when braced. Sounds like Heavy to me. On the whole, this half of the box looks to be a blast to assemble, paint, and even play, and I expect to see a ton of players start Ork forces once they get their proverbial power klawz on 'em

11. New Takes on Old Classics

The Adeptus Astartes on Armageddon are but the newest wave in an eternal conflict, and that is represented by a few variations on armor mark spread across their forces. I will say, this feels like a fairly slim selection for their half of the box, with only 10 Intercessors, 5 Vanguard Veterans, 3 Eradicators, and some Leaders. Lest we forget, there is also a brand-new Land Speeder that echoes the old kit, but on the whole this is likely to come up short in terms of points compared to Leviathan. Still, there's a purposeful mirroring of each side in this set which wasn't true of the previous launch box, with the Ancient & Bannernob, Librarian & Weirdboy, even the Chaplain & Bigboss. The way this set is laid out, both sides have no obvious iconography, allowing you to choose their personally dedicated Waaagh or Chapter without issue; instead of their bases or shoulders being adorned with, say, Tyranid gribblies (e.g. Lieutenant With Combi-weapon), you can tell a story even beyond Armageddon itself. I think this box is a fantastic jumping-off point for anyone getting into either army, or indeed Warhammer 40k itself, and I'm absolutely thrilled to get more information on what's coming in 11th Edition.
